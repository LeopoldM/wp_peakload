\subsection{Technicalities on the sufficient statistics}


Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir} provides sufficient regularity conditions under which \eqref{Eujmd} is single-crossing in \(k_i\), so its sign can be characterized sharply. The key condition is an equilibrium sorting property: capacity expansion must reallocate the endogenous responsiveness \(R_q(\theta)\) across types in a monotone way.

\begin{definition}
$R_q$ is \emph{log-submodular (resp. log-supermodular) in $(\theta,k_i)$} if $\partial^2 \log R_q/\partial\theta\,\partial k_i\leq (\text{resp.} \geq 0)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Single-crossing]\label{lem:SC-dir}
Assume that, for each \(k_i\), the map \(\theta\mapsto R_\theta(\theta)\) is monotone, and that \(R_q(\theta)\) has log-monotone differences in \((\theta,k_i)\). Then the map
\[
k_i \longmapsto \mathbb E_s\!\left[
\E_i^w[uJ\mid s]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]
\]
is single-crossing in $k_i$. Moreover, if a sign change occurs, its direction is pinned down by the combination of monotonicities: it crosses from $+$ to $-$ when $R_\theta$ is increasing and $R_q$ is log-submodular, or when $R_\theta$ is decreasing and $R_q$ is log-supermodular; it crosses from $-$ to $+$ in the two remaining cases.
\end{lemma}

The economic interpretation of log-monotone differences in \((\theta,k_i)\) is intuitive. Suppose that, for each \(k_i\), the map \(\theta\mapsto R_\theta(\theta)\) is increasing, so higher types receive higher effective virtual surplus, and that \(R_q(\theta)\) is log-submodular in \((\theta,k_i)\). Then capacity expansion disproportionately raises \(R_q\) for lower types, in the sense that for \(k_i'>k_i\), the ratio \(R_q(\theta,k_i')/R_q(\theta)\) is decreasing in \(\theta\). Combined with \(R_\theta(\theta)\) increasing, this makes the covariance term in \eqref{signEuJ} more negative as \(k_i\) grows. Thus, as capacity expands, the weighted average \eqref{Eujmd} shifts monotonically from the regime in which the average effect dominates toward the regime in which the sorting effect dominates. Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir} formalizes this single-crossing intuition.

The sign of both ratios depends on the model primitives as follows. For $R_q'(\theta)$, only the curvature of $u$ matters:
%##########################################
\[\sign\!\left(R_q'(\theta)\right)=-\sign\!\left((u_q(\cdot))^2-u(\cdot)u_{qq}(\cdot)\right),\]
%##########################################
so $R_q'(\theta)<0$ for linear and quadratic demand, while $R_q'(\theta)>0$ for CES/isoelastic demand. For $R_\theta'(\theta)$, the answer depends on whether the virtual surplus $J_i(\theta)$ grows faster or slower than the IC term $\Lambda_i(\theta)$ as $\theta$ increases,
%##########################################
\[
\label{signRt}\tag{$\sign R_\theta$}
\sign\!\left(R_\theta'(\theta)\right) = \sign\!\left(J_i(\theta)\right)\,\sign\!\Bigg(\underbrace{\frac{J_i'(\theta)}{J_i(\theta)}}_{\text{revenue term}} - \underbrace{\frac{\Lambda_i'(\theta)}{\Lambda_i(\theta)}}_{\text{IC term}}\Bigg).
\]
%##########################################
The first term flags whether a type contributes to revenue ($J_i(\theta)>0$) or not ($J_i(\theta)<0$). The second compares the proportional growth rates of the two: when $\Lambda_i'(\theta)<0$, the IC term is shrinking, and the revenue term automatically dominates, giving $R_\theta'(\theta)>0$.\footnote{It is not possible to have a clear-cut sign of $R'_\theta(\theta)$. Indeed, the sign of $\Lambda_i'(\theta)$ depends on $g_i(\theta)$ and $\lambda_i(\theta)$: $\Lambda_i'(\theta)=-\Lambda_i(\theta)\frac{g_i'(\theta)}{g_i(\theta)}-\lambda_i(\theta)$. For instance, under a uniform distribution $\Lambda_i'(\theta)<0$, while its sign may be ambiguous for other distributions. Hence, under a uniform distribution, the sign of $R_\theta(\theta)$ is ambiguous as soon as $J_i(\theta)<0$. A utilitarian market designer (i.e., $\lambda_i(\theta)=G_i(\theta)$) does not imply a unique sign for $R_\theta(\theta)$, since it places implicit weight on higher types: in that case $\G_i(\theta)=\theta$, and the IC term in \eqref{signRt} is positive (equal to $1/\theta$). If instead the correlation between types and social weights is zero, then the IC term is null and the sign of $R_\theta'(\theta)$ is determined solely by $J_i'(\theta)$, which is positive under regularity of $g_i(\theta)$.} 


\begin{remark}[Primitive foundation]\label{rem:prim}
On the IR-unconstrained region, where $\beta_i=0$, the log-monotone-differences condition becomes a condition on primitives alone: since $\partial q_i/\partial k_i = w_i/\overline{w}_i$ is proportional across types, the sign of $\partial^2\log R_q/\partial\theta\,\partial k_i$ reduces to the sign of $R_q''(q)$, so $R_q$ is log-submodular whenever $R_q''(\cdot)\le 0$ and log-supermodular whenever $R_q''(\cdot)\ge 0$. On the IR-constrained region, the response of the shadow cost $\beta_i$ enters the derivative of $\partial q_i/\partial k_i$ and the condition is no longer implied by the curvature of $U$ alone. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, however, $R_q = 1/\alpha$ is constant, so all cross-derivatives of $\log R_q$ vanish identically; the log-monotone-differences condition is therefore satisfied with equality regardless of the regime considered. Finally, note that unlike the log-monotone-differences condition on $R_q$, the monotonicity of $R_\theta(\theta)$ does not depend on $k_i$: from \eqref{signRt}, its sign is determined entirely by the type distribution and the welfare weights.
\end{remark}



\subsection{Redistributive preferences and the sorting effect}\label{app:sorting}

This section examines how a change in redistributive preferences affects the sufficient statistic \eqref{Eujmd} that governs the welfare incidence of capacity expansion. Differentiating $\mathbb{E}^w[uJ\mid s] = \varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_q(\theta)R_\theta(\theta)]$ with respect to the preference perturbation yields three terms:
\[
\Delta\mathbb{E}^w[uJ\mid s] = \underbrace{\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_q R_\theta]}_{\text{scarcity rent}} + \underbrace{\varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[\Delta R_q \cdot R_\theta]}_{\text{behavioral}} + \underbrace{\varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_q \cdot \Delta R_\theta]}_{\text{sorting}}.
\]
The scarcity rent term is governed by $\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)/\varepsilon_i(s) = \mathbb{E}^w_i[R_\mathcal{G}\mid s]$, established in Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}. The sorting term is governed by the identity $\Delta R_\theta(\theta) = -R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)R_\theta(\theta)$, which is the formal bridge discussed in the main text. The behavioral term cannot be signed in general, as established in Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation}. Proposition~\ref{prop:Elasticity} below provides conditions under which the sorting term can nonetheless be signed from primitives. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the behavioral term vanishes entirely since $R_q$ is constant, and both the scarcity rent and sorting terms collapse to a single covariance condition between $R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)$ and $R_\theta(\theta)$, as stated in Corollary~\ref{cor:CARA}.

\begin{proposition}[Elasticity condition]\label{prop:Elasticity}
Assume that $R_\theta(\theta)$ and $R_q(\theta)$ are monotone, that $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$ has a constant sign, and that, for all $\theta$ such that $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)R_\theta(\theta)\neq 0$,\footnote{At isolated zeros of $R_\theta(\theta)$, the cross-term $-R_\theta(\theta)R_{\mathcal{G}}'(\theta)$ vanishes and the sign of $\Delta R_\theta'(\theta)$ is determined by $-R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)$ directly. At isolated zeros of $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$, the constant-sign hypothesis forces $R_{\mathcal{G}}'(\theta)=0$ by differentiability, so the cross-term vanishes there as well. Monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ is preserved in both cases.}
\[
\left|\frac{R_{\mathcal{G}}'(\theta)}{R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)}\right| \le \left|\frac{R_\theta'(\theta)}{R_\theta(\theta)}\right|.
\]
Then $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ is monotone and
\[
\mathrm{sign}\!\left(\mathrm{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(\Delta R_\theta(\theta),R_q(\theta)\right)\right) = \mathrm{sign}\!\left(-R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)\right)\cdot \mathrm{sign}\!\left(R_q'(\theta)\right).
\]
If the elasticity condition fails, monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ can no longer be inferred from primitives alone.
\end{proposition}

The elasticity condition compares two elasticities: (i) $\left|R_{\mathcal{G}}'(\theta)/R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)\right|$, which measures how sharply the preference change varies across types, and (ii) $\left|R_\theta'(\theta)/R_\theta(\theta)\right|$, which measures how sharply the existing sorting pattern varies across types. The condition requires that the preference change be smoother in proportional terms than the existing sorting pattern. To interpret its economic meaning, consider the case where lower types are allocated more capacity ($R_q'(\theta)\le 0$), higher types get higher rents ($R_\theta'(\theta)\ge 0$), the change of preferences is toward higher types ($R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)\ge 0$), and we seek to reduce the adverse sorting effect. Then a preference change — even in the correct direction — can exacerbate adverse sorting by creating local distortions. The sufficient elasticity condition binds in two regions: (a) types contributing negatively to the trade-off ($R_\theta(\theta)<0$) but receiving increasing proportional weights ($R_{\mathcal{G}}'(\theta)\ge 0$), and (b) types contributing positively to the trade-off ($R_\theta(\theta)>0$) but receiving decreasing proportional weights ($R_{\mathcal{G}}'(\theta)\le 0$). In both regions, preference changes that are too concentrated reverse the sign of $\Delta R_\theta'(\theta)$; the elasticity bound ensures changes remain sufficiently gradual.

Figure~\ref{fig:variation_uniform_preference_tradeoff} illustrates this: even when the direction of a preference shift favors the same consumers, a particular concentration of social weight adjustments can exacerbate adverse sorting, worsening rather than improving the trade-off effect.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:CARA}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the behavioral channel vanishes, and the entire implementation region $[\tilde{k}_i^-,\tilde{k}_i^+]$ and its peak $k^\ast$ are governed by a single covariance condition: (i) if $\operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}(R_{\mathcal{G}},R_\theta)>0$, the region expands and $k^\ast$ shifts left; (ii) if $\operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}(R_{\mathcal{G}},R_\theta)<0$, the region contracts and $k^\ast$ shifts right.
\end{corollary}

Both results share the same spirit: they characterize when a preference shift exacerbates or relaxes the fundamental negative sorting in the revenue structure. The covariance $\operatorname{Cov}(R_{\mathcal{G}}, R_\theta)$ directly captures this: when the preference shift allocates proportionally more weight to types with low $R_\theta(\theta)$ — types contributing little to virtual surplus — the budget constraint tightens and the implementation region contracts.


\subsection{Marginal response of category allocations to aggregate capacity}\label{app:dkidk}

The next lemma characterizes the local response of the optimal category allocation to a marginal change in aggregate capacity. The result is local: it holds on a neighborhood of an interior optimum in which the set of active and contributing categories is unchanged. Its main purpose is to show that cross-category heterogeneity enters only through the sufficient statistic
\[
\mathbb E_s\!\left[\mathbb E_i^w[uJ\mid s]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right],
\]
which is the same object that governs the within-category comparative statics.

\begin{lemma}[Marginal response of category allocations to aggregate capacity]\label{lem:dkidk}
Fix an interior optimum \(\{k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast\}_{i\in N}\) and suppose that, on a neighborhood of the optimum, the set of active and contributing categories is unchanged. For each interior category \(i\), define
\[
\Omega_i
:=
\mathbb E_s\!\left[\frac{1}{\bar w_i(s)}\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right].
\]
Then the marginal response of category \(i\) is
\[
\frac{dk_i^\ast}{dk}
=
\frac{
\Omega_i^{-1}
\left(
\mathbb E_s\!\left[\mathbb E_i^w[uJ\mid s]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]
-I'(k^\ast)
\right)
}{
\sum_{j\in N } \mu_j\,\Omega_j^{-1}
\left(
\mathbb E_s\!\left[\mathbb E_j^w[uJ\mid s]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_j\}}\right]
-I'(k^\ast)
\right)
}
\mathcal{B}
-\Omega_i^{-1}\tilde\lambda^\ast I''(k^\ast).
\]
where
\[\left(
1+\tilde\lambda^\ast I''(k^\ast)\sum_{j\in N } \mu_j \Omega_j^{-1}
\right)>0\]
and $\tilde\lambda^\ast I''(k^\ast)>0$. In particular, since \(\Omega_i>0\), for a given marginal change in aggregate capacity, the sign of the category capacity change is fully determined by the sufficient statistic \eqref{Eujmd} 

\end{lemma}

The lemma shows that the response of \(k_i^\ast\) to aggregate expansion depends on category \(i\) only through the sufficient statistic \(\mathbb E_s\!\left[\mathbb E_i^w[uJ\mid s]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]\). This makes it possible to connect the outer comparative statics to the within-category ordering results.

\subsection{Proof of Proposition FB-alloc}



\begin{proof}
Let $\beta$, $\varepsilon(s)$, $\phi_i(\theta,s)$, and $\rho_i(\theta)$ be the Lagrange multipliers on the budget, the capacity, the transfer positivity, and the participation constraints, respectively. The Lagrangian is
\[\label{KKT}\tag{L$^*$}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L} &= \sum_i \mu_i\, \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[\lambda_i(\theta)\,\theta\, U(q_i,s) - t_i(\theta,s)\right] + \beta\!\left(\sum_i \mu_i \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}[t_i(\theta,s)] - I(k)\right) \\
&\quad + \mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\varepsilon(s)\!\left(k - \sum_i \mu_i \mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[q_i(\theta,s)]\right)\right] + \sum_i  \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\left[ \rho_i(\theta)(\theta U(q_i,s) - t_i(\theta,s))\right] dG_i(\theta) \\
&\quad + \sum_i \mu_i\, \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[\phi_i(\theta,s)\,t_i(\theta,s)\right],
\end{aligned}
\]
with $\beta, \varepsilon(s), \rho_i(\theta), \phi_i(\theta,s) \geq 0$. The FOCs for each $(i,\theta,s)$ are
\begin{align*}
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial q_i} = 0 \quad &\Rightarrow \quad \left(\mu_i\lambda_i(\theta) + \rho_i(\theta)\right)\theta\, u(q_i,s) = \mu_i\varepsilon(s), \\
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial t_i} = 0 \quad &\Rightarrow \quad \mu_i\beta - \mu_i\lambda_i(\theta) - \rho_i(\theta) + \phi_i(\theta,s) = 0.
\end{align*}
Complementary slackness conditions are
\[
\varepsilon(s)\!\left(k - \sum_i \mu_i \mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[q_i(\theta,s)]\right) = 0, \quad \beta\!\left(\sum_i \mu_i \mathbb{E}_{s,\theta}[t_i] - I(k)\right) = 0,
\]
\[
\rho_i(\theta)\,\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\theta U(q_i,s) - t_i(\theta,s)\right] = 0, \quad \phi_i(\theta,s)\,t_i(\theta,s) = 0.
\]

\textit{Step 1: $s$-independence of transfers (WLOG).} The objective, the budget constraint, and the IR constraint all depend on $t_i(\theta,s)$ only through its expectation $\mathbb{E}_s[t_i(\theta,s)]$. Therefore any feasible state-contingent transfer schedule $t_i(\theta,s)$ can be replaced by $\overline{t}_i(\theta) := \mathbb{E}_s[t_i(\theta,s)]$ without altering the objective value or any constraint. Transfer positivity is preserved since $t_i(\theta,s) \geq 0$ for all $s$ implies $\overline{t}_i(\theta) \geq 0$. Hence without loss of generality $t_i^*(\theta,s) = t_i^*(\theta)$, and $\rho_i(\theta)$ and $\phi_i(\theta)$ are also $s$-independent.

\textit{Step 2: structure of optimal transfers.} Suppose two types $(\theta,i)$ and $(\theta',i')$ satisfy $\lambda_i(\theta) < \lambda_{i'}(\theta')$. Assume for contradiction that $t_{i'}(\theta') > 0$ and $t_i(\theta) = 0$. If both participation constraints are slack, then $\rho_i(\theta) = \rho_{i'}(\theta') = 0$ and $\phi_{i'}(\theta') = 0$, so the FOC on $t$ gives $\lambda_{i'}(\theta') = \beta$. But $t_i(\theta) = 0$ with slack participation requires $\phi_i(\theta) > 0$, giving $\lambda_i(\theta) = \beta + \phi_i(\theta)/\mu_i > \beta = \lambda_{i'}(\theta')$, contradicting $\lambda_i(\theta) < \lambda_{i'}(\theta')$. The case where the participation constraint of $(\theta', i')$ binds leads to the same contradiction. Therefore at the optimum, any type with $\lambda_i(\theta) < \lambda^*$ has $t_i^*(\theta) > 0$ and any type with $\lambda_i(\theta) > \lambda^*$ has $t_i^*(\theta) = 0$, where $\lambda^* = \beta$ is the marginal welfare weight pinned by the budget constraint.

\textit{Step 3: optimal quantities.} When capacity is slack, $\varepsilon(s) = 0$ and the FOC on $q$ gives $\theta\, u(q_i^*,s) = 0$, which is \eqref{eq:FB-S}. When capacity binds, the FOC on $q$ gives $\theta\, u(q_i^*,s) = \varepsilon(s)/(\lambda_i(\theta) + \rho_i(\theta)/\mu_i)$. For types with $\lambda_i(\theta) > \lambda^*$: $\rho_i(\theta) = 0$ and $\theta\, u(q_i^*,s) = \varepsilon(s)/\lambda_i(\theta)$. For types with $\lambda_i(\theta) \leq \lambda^*$: from the FOC on $t$, $\lambda_i(\theta) + \rho_i(\theta)/\mu_i = \beta$, so $\theta\, u(q_i^*,s) = \varepsilon(s)/\beta$. This establishes \eqref{eq:FB-T}.

\textit{Step 4: monotonicity of $q_i^*$ in $s$ and uniqueness of $s^*$.} When capacity is slack, totally differentiating $\theta\, u(q_i^*,s) = 0$ gives
\[
\frac{\partial q_i^*(\theta,s)}{\partial s} = -\frac{u_s(q_i^*,s)}{u_q(q_i^*,s)} > 0,
\]
since $u_q < 0$ and $u_s > 0$ by assumption. Hence aggregate demand $\sum_i \mu_i \mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[q_i^*(\theta,s)]$ is strictly increasing in $s$, which guarantees existence of a unique threshold $s^*$ separating $S^*$ and $T^*$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition CI-welfare}

\begin{proof}

\noindent{Part i)}
We maintain throughout that $B(k)+M(k)\neq 0$, where $B(k)+M(k)$ is the derivative of the binding residual equation with respect to $\beta$ after substituting the optimal allocation; this is the local regularity condition needed to apply the implicit function theorem, while $B(k)+M(k)>0$ delivers the monotone comparative statics used below.

\noindent\textbf{Corner case: $\beta(k)=0$.} If $\beta(k)=0$ there are no contributing types. On $T^*$, the first-order condition is $\theta\,u(q_i^*(\theta,s),s) = \varepsilon(s)/\lambda_i(\theta)$. Differentiating and substituting into the capacity constraint gives $\frac{\partial\varepsilon(s)}{\partial k} = -1/\overline w^{nc}(s) < 0$ and $\frac{\partial q_i^*(\theta,s)}{\partial k} = w_i^{nc}(\theta,s)/\overline w^{nc}(s) > 0$ on $T^*$. Since non-contributing types pay no transfer:
\[
\frac{\partial CS_i(\theta;k)}{\partial k} = \mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\theta\,u(q_i^*(\theta,s),s)\,\frac{\partial q_i^*(\theta,s)}{\partial k}\,\mathbf{1}_{s\in T^*}\right] \geq 0.
\]

\noindent\textbf{Interior case: $\beta(k)>0$ with coexistence.} Assume contributing and non-contributing types coexist on a set of peak states of positive probability. On $T^*$, the first-order conditions are
\[
\theta\,u(q_i^*(\theta,s),s) = \frac{\varepsilon(s)}{\beta(k)}, \quad \lambda_i(\theta)\leq\beta(k), \qquad \theta\,u(q_i^*(\theta,s),s) = \frac{\varepsilon(s)}{\lambda_i(\theta)}, \quad \lambda_i(\theta)>\beta(k).
\]
The weights $w_i^c$, $w_i^{nc}$, $\overline w^c(s)$, $\overline w^{nc}(s)$, $\overline w(s)$ and the inframarginal component
\[
m^c(s) := \frac{1}{\overline w(s)}\sum_i\mu_i\,\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}\!\left[w_i^c(\theta,s)\,\theta\,u(q_i^*(\theta,s),s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\beta(k)}\right]
\]
are as defined above. Differentiating the first-order conditions with respect to $k$:
\[
\frac{\partial q_i^*(\theta,s)}{\partial k} = w_i^c(\theta,s)\!\left(-\frac{\partial\varepsilon(s)}{\partial k} + \theta\,u(q_i^*,s)\,\frac{d\beta}{dk}\right), \quad \lambda_i(\theta)\leq\beta(k), \tag{$\partial_k q^c$}\label{dqc}
\]
\[
\frac{\partial q_i^*(\theta,s)}{\partial k} = -\,w_i^{nc}(\theta,s)\,\frac{\partial\varepsilon(s)}{\partial k}, \quad \lambda_i(\theta)>\beta(k). \tag{$\partial_k q^{nc}$}\label{dqnc}
\]
Differentiating the capacity constraint and using continuity of the allocation at each cutoff $\theta_i^*(k)$ --- so that boundary terms cancel --- yields
\[
\frac{\partial\varepsilon(s)}{\partial k} = m^c(s)\,\frac{d\beta}{dk} - \frac{1}{\overline w(s)}. \tag{$\partial_k\varepsilon$}\label{depsk}
\]

\noindent\textbf{Step 1: sign of $\frac{d\beta}{dk}$.} Since the IR constraint of marginal types binds, $R^M(k)\equiv 0$. Differentiating this identity with respect to $k$, substituting \eqref{dqc} and \eqref{depsk}, and collecting all terms in $\frac{d\beta}{dk}$ on one side yields
\[
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\sum_i\mu_i\,\mathbb{E}^w_i[u^c|s]\,\mathbf{1}_{s\in T^*}\right] - I'(k) + \frac{d\beta}{dk}\,B(k) = 0,
\]
where, for convenience of exposition, we write $\mathbb{E}^w_i[u^c|s]$ for the full per-category object defined above --- combining the inframarginal weighted marginal utility and the boundary term from the movement of $\theta_i^*(k)$ --- and
\[
B(k) := \mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\frac{\varepsilon(s)^2}{\beta(k)^2}\frac{\overline w^c(s)\,\overline w^{nc}(s)}{\overline w(s)}\,\mathbf{1}_{s\in T^*}\right] > 0.
\]
Solving for $\frac{d\beta}{dk}$:
\[
\frac{d\beta}{dk} = \frac{I'(k) - \mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\sum_i\mu_i\,\mathbb{E}^w_i[u^c|s]\,\mathbf{1}_{s\in T^*}\right]}{B(k)}.
\]
Since $B(k)>0$, condition \eqref{Ui*} implies $\frac{d\beta}{dk}\leq 0$.

\noindent\textbf{Step 2: surplus of non-contributing types.} Since $\frac{d\beta}{dk}\leq 0$ and $m^c(s)>0$, equation \eqref{depsk} gives $\frac{\partial\varepsilon(s)}{\partial k}<0$ for all $s\in T^*$. From \eqref{dqnc}: $\frac{\partial q_i^*(\theta,s)}{\partial k} = -w_i^{nc}(\theta,s)\frac{\partial\varepsilon(s)}{\partial k} > 0$ for every non-contributing type. Since $t_i^*(\theta,s)=0$:
\[
\frac{\partial CS_i(\theta;k)}{\partial k} = \mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\theta\,u(q_i^*(\theta,s),s)\,\frac{\partial q_i^*(\theta,s)}{\partial k}\,\mathbf{1}_{s\in T^*}\right] \geq 0.
\]

\noindent\textbf{Corner case: all types strictly contributing.} If all types satisfy $\lambda_i(\theta)<\beta(k)$, there are no non-contributing types and the statement is vacuous.

\noindent{Part ii)}

From the Lagrangian \eqref{P-CI}, the FOC with respect to $k$ is
\[
-\beta\, I'(k) + \E_s[\varepsilon(s)\,\mathbf{1}_{T^*}(s)] = 0,
\]
where we used $\varepsilon(s)=0$ for all $s\in S^*$, so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:FOC-k}
\beta\, I'(k) = \E_s[\varepsilon(s)\,\mathbf{1}_{T^*}(s)].
\end{equation}

\textit{Step 1: contributing types.} From \eqref{eq:FB-T}, for every type with $\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*$ and every $s\in T^*$:
\[
\theta\, u(q_i^*(\theta,s),s) = \frac{\varepsilon(s)}{\beta}.
\]
Multiplying by $\mu_i$, integrating over contributing types, and taking the expectation over $s\in T^*$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:step1}
\E_s\!\left[\sum_i \mu_i\,\E_{\theta_i}\!\left[\theta\, u(q_i^*,s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*}\right]\mathbf{1}_{T^*}(s)\right] = \frac{\E_s[\varepsilon(s)\mathbf{1}_{T^*}(s)]}{\beta}\,\mu^c = I'(k)\,\mu^c,
\end{equation}
where the last equality uses \eqref{eq:FOC-k}. Dividing by $\mu^c$ gives \eqref{eq:FB-k1}.


\begin{lemma}\label{lem:CI_interior_k}
If $I(\cdot)$ is convex, then $V^{CI}$ is concave in $k$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
When $I(\cdot)$ is convex, the complete-information problem is a concave maximization problem with a convex feasible set: the objective is concave in the choice variables, the capacity constraint is affine in $k$, and the budget constraint is convex because $I(k)$ is convex. Hence the value function $V^{CI}$ is concave in $k$.
\end{proof}


\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Lemma Transfer}

\begin{proof}

For each category $i$, define
\[
B_i(\theta,s)
:=
\theta U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)
- \underline\theta_i U\!\left(q_i(\underline\theta_i,s),s\right)
- \int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\theta}
U\!\left(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s\right)\,d\tilde\theta,
\]
and let
\[
\overline{B}_i(\theta)
:=
\mathbb{E}_s[B_i(\theta,s)].
\]

Start with the following individual transfer for each consumer of type $\theta$ in category $i$:
\begin{align*}
t_i(\theta,s)
&=
\tau_i
+ \overline{B}_i(\theta)
- \sum_{j\neq i}  \frac{\mu_j}{1-\mu_j}\,
   \mathbb{E}_{\theta_j}[\overline{B}_j(\theta_j)] + \Big(I(k) -I_i+\sum_{j\neq i}  \frac{\mu_j}{1-\mu_j}I_j\Big),
\end{align*}
where
\[
\tau_i
=
T_{i0}
+\sum_{j\neq i}  \frac{\mu_j}{1-\mu_j}\,
   \mathbb{E}_{\theta_j}[\overline{B}_j(\theta_j)]
- \Big(I(k) -I_i+\sum_{j\neq i}  \frac{\mu_j}{1-\mu_j}I_j\Big).
\]

Taking expectations over $\theta$, and noting that only $\overline{B}_i(\theta)$ depends on $\theta$, yields
\begin{align*}
\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[t_i(\theta,s)]
&=
\tau_i
+ \mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[\overline{B}_i(\theta)]
- \sum_{j\neq i}  \frac{\mu_j}{1-\mu_j}\,
   \mathbb{E}_{\theta_j}[\overline{B}_j(\theta_j)] 
+ \Big(I(k) -I_i+\sum_{j\neq i}  \frac{\mu_j}{1-\mu_j}I_j\Big).
\end{align*}

Multiplying by the size $\mu_i$ of category $i$, summing over all categories, and using $\sum_{i=1}^n\mu_i = 1$, we obtain
\begin{align*}
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[t_i(\theta,s)]
&=
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \tau_i
+ \sum_{i=1}^n\mu_i\,\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[\overline{B}_i(\theta)]-\sum_{i=1}^n\mu_i\sum_{j\neq i} \frac{\mu_j}{1-\mu_j}\,
   \mathbb{E}_{\theta_j}[\overline{B}_j(\theta_j)]
\\
&\quad
+ I(k)
-\sum_{i=1}^n\mu_iI_i
+\sum_{i=1}^n\mu_i\sum_{j\neq i}  \frac{\mu_j}{1-\mu_j}I_j.
\end{align*}

The second and third terms cancel, and the fifth and sixth terms also cancel, so that
\[
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[t_i(\theta,s)]
=
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \tau_i + I(k).
\]
Therefore, an allocation satisfies the budget constraint if and only if $\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \tau_i \geq 0$.

Next, substitute the expression for $\tau_i$:
\begin{align*}
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \tau_i
&=
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \left(
T_{i0}
+  \sum_{j\neq i}  \frac{\mu_j}{1-\mu_j}\,
     \mathbb{E}_{\theta_j}[\overline{B}_j(\theta_j)]
\right)
- I(k) \\
&=
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \left(
T_{i0}
+  \mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[\overline{B}_i(\theta)]
\right)
- I(k).
\end{align*}

By Fubini's theorem and the usual hazard-rate argument, the expectation of $\overline{B}_i(\theta)$ can be written in terms of the virtual utility
\[
U(q_i(\theta,s),s) J_i(\theta)
:= U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)(\theta -
\gamma_i(\theta)\,),
\]
as
\[
\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[\overline{B}_i(\theta)]
=
\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\left[
U(q_i(\theta,s),s) J_i(\theta)
- \underline{\theta_i} U\!\left(q_i(\underline{\theta_i},s),s\right)
\right].
\]
Hence
\[
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \tau_i
=
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\left[
T_{i0} + U(q_i(\theta,s),s) J_i(\theta) - \underline{\theta_i} U\!\left(q_i(\underline{\theta_i},s),s\right)
\right]
- I(k).
\]

Finally, replacing $\tau_i$ in $t_i(\theta,s)$ yields
\[
t_i(\theta,s)
=
T_{i0}
+ \overline{B}_i(\theta)
=
T_{i0}
+ \mathbb{E}_s\left[
\theta U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)
- \underline{\theta_i}U\!\left(q_i(\underline{\theta_i},s),s\right)
- \int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\theta}
U\!\left(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s\right)\, d\tilde\theta
\right].
\]

Thus
\begin{align*}
\mathbb{E}_s\big[\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\big]
&=
\mathbb{E}_s\big[\underline{\theta_i}U(q_i(\underline{\theta_i},s),s)\big]
- T_{i0}
\\
&\quad
+ \int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\theta}
\mathbb{E}_s\big[U(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s)\big]\,d\tilde\theta.
\end{align*}
Defining
\[
\E\underline{CS}_i
:=
\mathbb{E}_s\big[\underline{\theta_i}U(q_i(\underline{\theta_i},s),s)\big]
- T_{i0},
\]
we get
\[
\mathbb{E}_s[\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)]
=
\E\underline{CS}_i
+ \int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\theta}
\mathbb{E}_s\big[U(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s)\big]\,d\tilde\theta,
\]
which is the usual envelope representation and therefore satisfies the IC condition. Moreover, substituting $\underline{\theta_i}U(q_i(\underline{\theta_i},s),s) = \E\underline{CS}_i + T_{i0}$ into the expression for $\sum_i \mu_i \tau_i$ gives the claimed transformed budget constraint
\[
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \tau_i
=
\sum_{i=1}^n \mu_i \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\big[ U(q_i(\theta,s),s) J_i(\theta) - \E\underline{CS}_i\big]
- I(k).
\]

Multiplying the utility of a consumer with type $\theta$ from category $i$ and taking the expectation over the whole category yields

\[
\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}[\lambda_i(\theta) \;( \;\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\;)\;]
=
\tilde{\lambda}_i \; \E\underline{CS}_i
+ \mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[\lambda_i(\theta)\int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\theta}
\mathbb{E}_s\big[U(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s)\big]\,d\tilde\theta],
\]

We apply Fubini's theorem to the second term:

\begin{align*}
\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}\!\left[\lambda_i(\theta)\int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\theta}
\mathbb{E}_s[U(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s)]\,d\tilde\theta\right]
&= \int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\overline{\theta}_i} \lambda_i(\theta) g_i(\theta) 
   \left(\int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\theta} \mathbb{E}_s[U(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s)]\,d\tilde\theta\right) d\theta \\
&= \int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\overline{\theta}_i} \mathbb{E}_s[U(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s)] 
   \left(\int_{\tilde\theta}^{\overline{\theta}_i} \lambda_i(\theta) g_i(\theta)\,d\theta\right) d\tilde\theta \\
&= \int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\overline{\theta}_i} \mathbb{E}_s[U(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s)] 
    \gamma_i(\theta) \E[ \ \lambda_i(\tilde \theta) \ | \ \tilde \theta \ \ge \ \theta \ ] d\tilde\theta \\
&= \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[\Lambda_i(\theta)\, U(q_i(\theta,s),s)\right].
\end{align*}

This yields the desired expression:

\[ \tilde{\lambda}_i \;\E\underline{CS}_i 
      + \E_{(s,\theta_i)} \big[\; \Lambda_i(\theta)\;  U(q_i(\theta,s),s)\; \big] \]

\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Lemma FOC Without Ironing}
\begin{proof}
Consider the within-category problem
\[
\max_{q_i(\theta,s)} 
\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[\Gamma_i(\theta)\,U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\right]
\]
subject to
\[
\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)J_i(\theta)\right]-I_i \ge 0,
\qquad
\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}\!\left[q_i(\theta,s)\right]\le k_i
\quad \forall s.
\]

Let \(\beta_i \ge 0\) and \(\varepsilon_i(s)\ge 0\) denote the multipliers on \eqref{Rmdi-def} and \eqref{Kmdi-def}, respectively. The associated Lagrangian is
\[
\mathcal L
=
\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[
\left(\Gamma_i(\theta)+\beta_i J_i(\theta)\right)
U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)
\right]
-
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s)
\left(\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[q_i(\theta,s)]-k_i\right)
\right]
-
\beta_i I_i.
\]
Hence the pointwise first-order condition is
\[
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\mathcal G_i(\theta)-\varepsilon_i(s)=0,
\qquad
\text{where }
\mathcal G_i(\theta):=\Gamma_i(\theta)+\beta_i J_i(\theta).
\]

We first characterize the states in which the capacity constraint is slack. If \(\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[q_i(\theta,s)]<k_i\), complementary slackness implies \(\varepsilon_i(s)=0\). Since Assumption~\ref{ass:interior} gives \(\mathcal G_i(\theta)>0\) for all \(\theta\), the first-order condition reduces to
\[
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)=0.
\]
By strict concavity of \(U(\cdot,s)\), this equation has a unique solution, denoted \(q_i^0(s)\). In particular, the off-peak allocation is independent of \(\theta\), so it automatically satisfies monotonicity.

Moreover, implicit differentiation of \(u(q_i^0(s),s)=0\) yields
\[
\frac{\partial q_i^0(s)}{\partial s}
=
-\frac{u_s\!\left(q_i^0(s),s\right)}{u_q\!\left(q_i^0(s),s\right)}
\ge 0,
\]
since \(u_s\ge 0\) and \(u_q<0\). Therefore the unconstrained aggregate demand $Q_i^0(s):=\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[q_i^0(s)]$ is nondecreasing in \(s\). It follows that the set of states in which the capacity constraint is slack is an interval of the form \(S_i=[\underline s,s_i)\), while the set of binding states is \(T_i=[s_i,\overline s]\), where \(s_i\) is defined by $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}\!\left[q_i(\theta,s_i)\right]=k_i$, with the obvious boundary cases if one of the two sets is empty.

Now consider \(s\in T_i\). For such states, the allocation solves the state-by-state problem with binding aggregate quantity constraint, and the first-order condition is exactly $u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\mathcal G_i(\theta) = \varepsilon_i(s)$. Because \(u_q<0\) and \(\mathcal G_i(\theta)>0\), the objective is strictly concave in \(q_i(\theta,s)\) pointwise, so this first-order condition characterizes a unique allocation for each \((\theta,s)\).

Finally, Assumption~\ref{ass:interior} also imposes that \(\mathcal G_i(\theta)\) is nondecreasing. Differentiating the first-order condition with respect to \(\theta\) gives
\[
\frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s)}{\partial \theta}
=
-
\frac{u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)}{u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)}
\frac{\mathcal G_i'(\theta)}{\mathcal G_i(\theta)}
\ge 0,
\]
so the monotonicity constraint is satisfied. This proves part (i).

For part (ii), if \eqref{Rmdi-def} is slack at \((k_i,I_i)\), complementary slackness implies \(\beta_i=0\). Hence $\mathcal G_i(\theta)=\Gamma_i(\theta)+\beta_i J_i(\theta)\equiv \Gamma_i(\theta)$. This proves part (ii).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:welfare}}
\begin{proof}
Define $\mathcal W_i(\theta) := \partial \mathbb E CS_i(\theta)/\partial k_i$. From the envelope representation \eqref{ICmd-def},
\[\mathcal W_i(\theta) = \mathcal W_i(\underline\theta_i) + \int_{\underline\theta_i}^{\theta}\mathcal W_i'(\tilde\theta)\,d\tilde\theta, \qquad \mathcal W_i'(\theta) = \mathbb E_s\!\left[u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s)}{\partial k_i}\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right].\]
For each binding state $s\in T_i$, differentiating the pointwise FOC \eqref{KKT-qMD} with respect to $k_i$ and imposing the binding capacity constraint \eqref{Kmdi-def} gives
\[\frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s)}{\partial k_i} = \frac{w_i(\theta,s)}{\overline w_i(s)} - w_i(\theta,s)\,\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)\,\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i) + w_i(\theta,s)\,u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)J_i(\theta)\,\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i),\]
where $w_i$, $\overline w_i$, and $\mathsf H_i$ are as defined in the preliminary step of Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir}.


\noindent\textbf{IR-unconstrained allocation.} Since $\beta_i = 0$, we have $\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i) = 0$ and $\mathcal G_i(\theta)\equiv\Gamma_i(\theta)$. The expression for $\partial q_i/\partial k_i$ reduces to $w_i(\theta,s)/\overline w_i(s)$, so
\[\mathcal W_i'(\theta) = \mathbb E_s\!\left[\frac{u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,w_i(\theta,s)}{\overline w_i(s)}\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right] = \mathbb E_s\!\left[\frac{R_q(\theta,s)}{\Gamma_i(\theta)\,\overline w_i(s)}\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right] > 0,\]
since $R_q > 0$, $\Gamma_i(\theta) > 0$, and $\overline w_i(s) > 0$ by Assumption~\ref{ass:interior}. Hence $\mathcal W_i$ is strictly increasing on $[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i]$. By the preliminary step of Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir}, $\mathcal W_i(\underline\theta_i) = \partial \mathbb E\underline{CS}_i/\partial k_i$ has the sign of \eqref{Eujmd}. If \eqref{Eujmd} is positive, $\mathcal W_i(\underline\theta_i)>0$ and strict monotonicity implies every type gains. If \eqref{Eujmd} is negative, $\mathcal W_i(\underline\theta_i)<0$ and, by strict monotonicity, $\mathcal W_i$ crosses zero at most once; if and only if $\mathcal W_i(\overline\theta_i)>0$, a unique cutoff $\tilde\theta_i$ exists and types below lose while types above gain.


\noindent\textbf{IR-constrained allocation.}

\noindent\textit{Step 1: initial condition.} Since $\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i = 0$ holds identically along the IR-constrained region by definition, its total derivative with respect to $k_i$ is zero. By the envelope representation \eqref{ICmd-def}, this total derivative equals $\mathcal W_i(\underline\theta_i)$, so $\mathcal W_i(\underline\theta_i) = 0$.

\noindent\textit{Step 2: shape of $\mathcal W_i$.} Under Assumption~\ref{ass:mult}, $u(q,s)=\kappa(q)\zeta(s)$ so the ratio $R_q(\theta) := -\zeta(q_i(\theta,s))/\zeta_q(q_i(\theta,s))$ is $s$-independent on the binding region by Lemma~\ref{lem:statewise-zero-mult}. Using the first-order condition \eqref{KKT-qMD}, we have $u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,w_i(\theta,s) = R_q(\theta)/\mathcal G_i(\theta)$ and $u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,J_i(\theta) = \varepsilon_i(s)\,R_\theta(\theta)$. Substituting into $\mathcal W_i'(\theta)$ yields
\[\mathcal W_i'(\theta) = \frac{R_q(\theta)}{\mathcal G_i(\theta)}\left(\mathcal A_i - \beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)\mathcal B_i + \beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)\mathcal C_i R_\theta(\theta)\right),\]
where $\mathcal A_i := \mathbb E_s[\overline w_i(s)^{-1}\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}]>0$, $\mathcal B_i := \mathbb E_s[\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}] = \mathcal S_i(k_i)$, and $\mathcal C_i := \mathbb E_s[\varepsilon_i(s)\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}]>0$. Since $\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i) = -\mathcal S_i(k_i)/\mathcal V_i(k_i)$ from the preliminary step of Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir}, the term $-\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)\mathcal B_i = \mathcal B_i^2/\mathcal V_i(k_i)\ge 0$, so the constant part of the bracket is strictly positive. Since $R_q(\theta)/\mathcal G_i(\theta)>0$, the monotonicity of $\mathcal W_i'(\theta)$ is governed entirely by the term $\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)\mathcal C_i R_\theta(\theta)$: if $R_\theta(\theta)$ is monotone, $\mathcal W_i'(\theta)$ is monotone in $\theta$, so $\mathcal W_i$ is either concave or convex. The sign of $\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)$ is opposite to the sign of \eqref{Eujmd}, so if $R_\theta(\theta)$ is increasing, $\mathcal W_i$ is concave when \eqref{Eujmd} is positive and convex when \eqref{Eujmd} is negative; if $R_\theta(\theta)$ is decreasing, these conclusions are reversed.

\noindent\textit{Step 3: existence and direction of the second cutoff.} Since $\mathcal W_i(\underline\theta_i)=0$, a concave $\mathcal W_i$ crosses zero at most once in the interior. If $\mathcal W_i'(\underline\theta_i)>0$, the function initially rises from zero, eventually returns toward zero, and crosses it at a unique interior cutoff $\tilde\theta_i\in(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)$; types below $\tilde\theta_i$ gain and types above lose in case~(i), and the reverse in case~(ii). If $\mathcal W_i'(\underline\theta_i)\le 0$, no interior cutoff exists and either all types lose or all types gain. The convex case is symmetric, with concavity and convexity interchanged, and all welfare inequalities reversed. If $R_\theta(\theta)$ is decreasing, concavity and convexity are swapped and all inequalities reverse throughout.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition Region}
\begin{proof}
Let
\[
Y_i(k_i):=\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k_i).
\]
By the preliminary step of Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir},
\[
Y_i'(k_i)
=
\frac{\partial \mathbb E\underline{CS}_i}{\partial k_i}
=
\mathcal S_i(k_i).
\]
Under the conditions of Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir}, the map $k_i\mapsto \mathcal S_i(k_i)$ is single-crossing. Hence $Y_i'$ is monotone and crosses zero at most once in a given direction.

When \eqref{Eujmd} crosses from $+$ to $-$, $Y_i'$ is decreasing and crosses zero at most once from above, so $Y_i$ is single-peaked on $[0,k_i^+]$. When \eqref{Eujmd} crosses from $-$ to $+$, $Y_i'$ is increasing and $Y_i$ is single-dipped. At $k_i=0$ the capacity constraint binds at zero, so $q_i(\theta,s)=0$ for all $(\theta,s)$, giving
\[
Y_i(0)=-I_i<0
\qquad
\text{for any }I_i>0.
\]

\noindent\textit{Case: \eqref{Eujmd} crosses from $+$ to $-$.}
Suppose first that $Y_i(k_i^+)\ge 0$. Since $Y_i(0)<0$ and $Y_i(k_i^+)\ge 0$, continuity gives at least one zero in $(0,k_i^+]$. Single-peakedness implies that this zero is unique, defining $\tilde k_i$, and
\[
Y_i(k_i)\ge 0
\Longleftrightarrow
k_i\in[\tilde k_i,k_i^+].
\]

This proves part~(i).

Suppose instead that $Y_i(k_i^+)<0$. Then $Y_i$ is negative at both endpoints. If the peak value is negative, then $Y_i<0$ throughout. If the peak value is nonnegative, continuity and single-peakedness yield exactly two zeros
\[
\tilde k_i^-<\tilde k_i^+
\]
in $(0,k_i^+)$ such that
\[
Y_i(k_i)\ge 0
\Longleftrightarrow
k_i\in[\tilde k_i^-,\tilde k_i^+].
\]
This proves part~(ii).

\noindent\textit{Case: \eqref{Eujmd} crosses from $-$ to $+$.}
Then $Y_i$ is single-dipped with $Y_i(0)<0$. Either the trough value is negative and $Y_i<0$ throughout, or $Y_i$ rises above zero after the trough, crossing zero exactly once from below at a unique $\tilde k_i\in(0,k_i^+]$, and
\[
Y_i(k_i)\ge 0
\Longleftrightarrow
k_i\in[\tilde k_i,k_i^+].
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Preferences}
\begin{proof}
Write $\lambda_i^1(\theta):=\lambda_i(\theta)$, $\lambda_i^2(\theta):=\lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)$, and define
\[
\tilde\lambda_i^k := \int_{\underline\theta_i}^{\overline\theta_i}\lambda_i^k(\theta)\,dG_i(\theta), \qquad \Lambda_i^k(\theta) := \frac{1}{g_i(\theta)}\int_\theta^{\overline\theta_i}\lambda_i^k(t)\,dG_i(t), \qquad k\in\{1,2\}.
\]
Let $m(\theta):=\lambda_i^2(\theta)/\lambda_i^1(\theta)$, which is increasing in the upward-shift case and decreasing in the downward-shift case.


\noindent\textit{Core step: monotonicity of $\theta\mapsto\Lambda_i^2(\theta)/\Lambda_i^1(\theta)$.}
For each $\theta\in[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)$, define the probability measure $Q_\theta$ on $[\theta,\overline\theta_i]$ by
\[
dQ_\theta(t) := \frac{\lambda_i^1(t)\,dG_i(t)}{\displaystyle\int_\theta^{\overline\theta_i}\lambda_i^1(x)\,dG_i(x)}\,\mathbf{1}\{t\ge\theta\}.
\]
Since $\lambda_i^2(t)=m(t)\lambda_i^1(t)$, we have $\Lambda_i^2(\theta)/\Lambda_i^1(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{Q_\theta}[m(\tilde\theta)]$. For $\theta'<\theta$, $Q_\theta$ is $Q_{\theta'}$ conditioned on $\{\tilde\theta\ge\theta\}$. When $m$ is increasing, this conditioning shifts mass toward larger values of $m$, so by first-order stochastic dominance $\mathbb{E}_{Q_\theta}[m(\tilde\theta)] \ge \mathbb{E}_{Q_{\theta'}}[m(\tilde\theta)]$. Hence $\theta\mapsto\Lambda_i^2(\theta)/\Lambda_i^1(\theta)$ is increasing on $[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)$. When $m$ is decreasing the inequality reverses.


\noindent\textit{Proof of (i).}
Since $\theta\mapsto\Lambda_i^2(\theta)/\Lambda_i^1(\theta)$ is monotone, its logarithm is monotone too. Therefore, for almost every $\theta\in(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)$,
\[
0 \le \frac{d}{d\theta}\log\!\left(\frac{\Lambda_i^2(\theta)}{\Lambda_i^1(\theta)}\right) = \frac{\Lambda_i^{2\,\prime}(\theta)}{\Lambda_i^2(\theta)} - \frac{\Lambda_i^{1\,\prime}(\theta)}{\Lambda_i^1(\theta)},
\]
in the upward case, which yields $\Lambda_i^{\Delta\,\prime}(\theta)/\Lambda_i^\Delta(\theta) \ge \Lambda_i'(\theta)/\Lambda_i(\theta)$ for almost every $\theta$. The reverse inequality holds in the downward case.


\noindent\textit{Proof of (ii).}
Write
\[
\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) = J_i(\theta)\bigl(\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i\bigr)+\Lambda_i(\theta), \qquad \mathcal{G}_i^\Delta(\theta) = J_i(\theta)\bigl(\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta+\beta_i^{\times,\Delta}\bigr)+\Lambda_i^\Delta(\theta).
\]


\noindent\textbf{Upward case.} Assume $m$ is increasing and $1\le c_{\mathcal{G}}\le c_\Lambda$. The boundary evaluation gives
\[
\frac{\Lambda_i^\Delta(\underline\theta_i)}{\Lambda_i(\underline\theta_i)} = \frac{\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta}{\tilde\lambda_i} = c_\Lambda.
\]
Since the ratio is increasing by the core step and starts at $c_\Lambda \ge c_{\mathcal{G}}$,
\[
\Lambda_i^\Delta(\theta) \ge c_\Lambda\,\Lambda_i(\theta) \ge c_{\mathcal{G}}\,\Lambda_i(\theta) \qquad \text{for all }\theta.
\]
Using the definition of $c_{\mathcal{G}}$,
\[
\mathcal{G}_i^\Delta(\theta) = c_{\mathcal{G}}\,J_i(\theta)\bigl(\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i\bigr)+\Lambda_i^\Delta(\theta) \ge c_{\mathcal{G}}\,J_i(\theta)\bigl(\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i\bigr)+c_{\mathcal{G}}\,\Lambda_i(\theta) = c_{\mathcal{G}}\,\mathcal{G}_i(\theta).
\]
Since $c_{\mathcal{G}}\ge 1$ and $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)>0$, it follows that $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) \ge (c_{\mathcal{G}}-1)\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) \ge 0$ for all $\theta\in[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i]$.


\noindent\textbf{Downward case.} Assume $m$ is decreasing and $c_\Lambda\le c_{\mathcal{G}}\le 1$. The boundary evaluation gives $\Lambda_i^\Delta(\underline\theta_i)/\Lambda_i(\underline\theta_i) = c_\Lambda \le c_{\mathcal{G}}$. Since the ratio is decreasing by the core step and starts at $c_\Lambda$,
\[
\Lambda_i^\Delta(\theta) \le c_\Lambda\,\Lambda_i(\theta) \le c_{\mathcal{G}}\,\Lambda_i(\theta) \qquad \text{for all }\theta.
\]
Therefore $\mathcal{G}_i^\Delta(\theta) \le c_{\mathcal{G}}\,\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$, and since $c_{\mathcal{G}}\le 1$ and $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)>0$, it follows that $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) \le (c_{\mathcal{G}}-1)\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) \le 0$ for all $\theta\in[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i]$.


The special cases in the ``in particular'' clause follow immediately: if $\beta_i=\beta_i^{\times,\Delta}=0$ then $c_{\mathcal{G}}=c_\Lambda=\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta/\tilde\lambda_i$, so the ordering $c_{\mathcal{G}}\le c_\Lambda$ holds with equality and the condition reduces to $\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta\ge\tilde\lambda_i$; if $\beta_i^{\times,\Delta}+\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta=\beta_i+\tilde\lambda_i$ then $c_{\mathcal{G}}=1$ and the condition reduces to $c_\Lambda=\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta/\tilde\lambda_i\ge 1$.
\qedhere
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}}
\begin{proof}
When $\beta_i=0$, one has $\mathcal G_i(\theta)=\Gamma_i(\theta)$ and therefore $R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)=R_\Gamma(\theta)$; the result in the main text is stated in terms of $R_\Gamma$ for that case. Fix $s\in T_i$ and a local perturbation $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta)$ holding $k_i$ fixed.

Using the weights $w_i(\theta,s)$ and $\mathbb E_i^w[\cdot\mid s]$ from the preliminary step of Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir}, note that
\[
R_q(\theta,s)=\varepsilon_i(s)\,w_i(\theta,s),
\]
which follows from
\[
R_q(\theta,s)=
-\frac{u(q_i,s)}{u_q(q_i,s)}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\varepsilon_i(s)=u(q_i,s)\mathcal G_i(\theta).
\]

Linearizing the pointwise first-order condition
\[
u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\mathcal G_i(\theta)=\varepsilon_i(s)
\]
gives
\[
u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\mathcal G_i(\theta)\Delta q_i(\theta,s)
+
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\Delta\mathcal G_i(\theta)
-
\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)
=
0.
\]
Solving for $\Delta q_i(\theta,s)$ and substituting the definitions of $R_q$, $R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)=\Delta\mathcal G_i(\theta)/\mathcal G_i(\theta)$, and $\varepsilon_i(s)$ yields
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s)
=
R_q(\theta,s)
\left(
R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)
-
\frac{\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)}{\varepsilon_i(s)}
\right).
\]
Since the capacity constraint binds state-by-state and $k_i$ is held fixed,
\[
\mathbb E_{\theta_i}\!\left[\Delta q_i(\theta,s)\right]=0
\qquad
\text{for each }s\in T_i.
\]
Substituting the previous expression and using $R_q(\theta,s)=\varepsilon_i(s)w_i(\theta,s)$, dividing by $\varepsilon_i(s)\overline w_i(s)>0$, and rearranging gives
\[
\frac{\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)}{\varepsilon_i(s)}
=
\mathbb E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right].
\]
Substituting back,
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s)
=
R_q(\theta,s)
\left(
R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)-\mathbb E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]
\right),
\]
which is the desired identity.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation}}

\begin{proof}
Under the IR-unconstrained allocation, $\beta_i=0$, so Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc} gives, for each $s\in T_i$,
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s) = R_q(\theta,s)\left(R_\Gamma(\theta)-\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_\Gamma\mid s\right]\right).
\]

\noindent\textit{First part: IR-unconstrained allocation.}
Since $\mathbb{E}_i^w[R_\Gamma\mid s]$ is a weighted average of $R_\Gamma$ with positive weights, if $R_\Gamma$ is not almost everywhere constant then $R_\Gamma(\theta)-\mathbb{E}_i^w[R_\Gamma\mid s]$ must take both positive and negative values on $\Theta_i$. Because $R_q(\theta,s)>0$, the same is true of $\Delta q_i(\theta,s)$. Next, recall that $\Gamma_i(\underline\theta_i)=\tilde\lambda_i\,\underline\theta_i$ and $\Gamma_i(\overline\theta_i)=\tilde\lambda_i\,\overline\theta_i$, so for any perturbation,
\[
R_\Gamma(\underline\theta_i)=R_\Gamma(\overline\theta_i) = \frac{\Delta\tilde\lambda_i}{\tilde\lambda_i}.
\]
Therefore the endpoint values of $\Delta q_i(\theta,s)$ have the same sign (or are both zero). Since $\Delta q_i(\theta,s)$ takes both signs while its endpoint values coincide in sign, it cannot be monotone on $\Theta_i$.

\noindent\textit{Second part: IR-constrained allocation.}
Under $\beta_i^\Delta+\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta=\beta_i+\tilde\lambda_i$, we have $\Delta\tilde\lambda_i+\Delta\beta_i=0$. At $\overline\theta_i$, since $\Lambda_i(\overline\theta_i)=0$ and therefore $\Delta\Lambda_i(\overline\theta_i)=0$,
\[
\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\overline\theta_i) = J_i(\overline\theta_i)(\Delta\tilde\lambda_i+\Delta\beta_i) = 0,
\]
so $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\overline\theta_i)=0$ and therefore $\Delta q_i(\overline\theta_i,s)=0$. Since the capacity constraint requires $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[\Delta q_i(\theta,s)]=0$ for each $s\in T_i$, and since $\Delta q_i(\overline\theta_i,s)=0$ while $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$ is not almost everywhere constant, the function $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$ must take both positive and negative values on $\Theta_i$. A function that vanishes at one endpoint and takes both signs cannot be monotone on $\Theta_i$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:rprefreallocation}}

\begin{proof}
Assume $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta)$ is mean-preserving, so $\Delta\tilde\lambda_i=0$. Then $R_\mathcal{G}(\underline\theta_i)=R_\mathcal{G}(\overline\theta_i)=0$ in both regimes: under the IR-unconstrained allocation this follows from $R_\Gamma(\underline\theta_i)=R_\Gamma(\overline\theta_i)=\Delta\tilde\lambda_i/\tilde\lambda_i=0$; under the IR-constrained allocation, $\Delta\beta_i=-\Delta\tilde\lambda_i=0$ so the same endpoint evaluations apply. By Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation}, $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$ is not monotone and therefore takes both signs, so the set of cutoffs $\{\theta:\Delta q_i(\theta,s)=0\}$ contains at least two elements. Let $\theta_-$ and $\theta_+$ denote its minimum and maximum elements.

\noindent\textit{Part (i).} Suppose $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\ge 0$ for all $\theta$, so $R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)\ge 0$. Since $R_\mathcal{G}$ is nonnegative, not identically zero, and vanishes at both endpoints, the weighted average satisfies $\mathbb{E}^w_i[R_\mathcal{G}\mid s]>0$. Therefore $R_\mathcal{G}(\underline\theta_i)-\mathbb{E}^w_i[R_\mathcal{G}\mid s]\le 0$ and $R_\mathcal{G}(\overline\theta_i)-\mathbb{E}^w_i[R_\mathcal{G}\mid s]\le 0$, so by Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}, $\Delta q_i(\theta,s)\le 0$ for $\theta\in[\underline\theta_i,\theta_-)\cup(\theta_+,\overline\theta_i]$.

\noindent\textit{Part (ii).} Apply the same argument to $-\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$, reversing all inequalities.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:CARA}}\begin{proof}
The welfare change follows from the IC representation \eqref{ICmd-def}:
\[\Delta\mathbb{E}CS_i(\theta) = \Delta\mathbb{E}\underline{CS}_i + \int_{\underline\theta_i}^{\theta}\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[u\!\left(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s\right)\Delta q_i(\tilde\theta,s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]d\tilde\theta.\]
Substituting Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc} and using the first-order condition $u(q_i,s)=\varepsilon_i(s)/\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$ together with $R_q(\theta)=1/\alpha$ under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the derivative with respect to $\theta$ is
\[\frac{\partial\Delta\mathbb{E}CS_i(\theta)}{\partial\theta} = \frac{1}{\alpha}\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\frac{\varepsilon_i(s)}{\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)}\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal{G}}]\right)\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right] = \frac{\overline\varepsilon_i}{\alpha\,\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)}\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal{G}}]\right),\]
where $\overline\varepsilon_i:=\mathbb{E}_s[\varepsilon_i(s)\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}]>0$. Since $\overline\varepsilon_i/(\alpha\,\mathcal{G}_i(\theta))>0$, the sign of the derivative is determined solely by $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal{G}}]$. Under unimodality of $R_{\mathcal{G}}$, this expression crosses zero at most twice, so $\Delta\mathbb{E}CS_i(\theta)$ has at most two turning points and at most three zeros in $[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i]$.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Boundary conditions.}

\noindent\textit{IR-constrained allocation.} The IR constraint $\mathbb{E}\underline{CS}_i=0$ binds at both the baseline and the perturbed allocation, so $\Delta\mathbb{E}\underline{CS}_i=0$ and therefore $\Delta\mathbb{E}CS_i(\underline\theta_i)=0$.

\noindent\textit{IR-unconstrained allocation.} Linearizing \eqref{ECSi} with respect to the preference perturbation and substituting Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}:
\[\Delta\mathbb{E}\underline{CS}_i = \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[u\!\left(q_i,s\right)\Delta q_i(\theta,s)\,J_i(\theta)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right] = \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\,R_\theta(\theta)\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal{G}}]\right)\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right].\]
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, $\mathbb{E}_i^w[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s]=\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal{G}}]$ is $s$-independent, so the expression separates and yields
\[\Delta\mathbb{E}\underline{CS}_i = \overline\varepsilon_i\,\mathrm{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(R_\theta(\theta),R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)\right).\]

\medskip\noindent\textit{Cutoff structure.} In both regimes, $\Delta\mathbb{E}CS_i(\theta)$ starts from its boundary value, has at most two turning points, and its derivative pattern is governed by the sign of $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$ through the unimodality of $R_{\mathcal{G}}$. Under $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\ge 0$, $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$ peaks above its mean in the interior so the derivative pattern is $-,+,-$, and the number of zeros of $\Delta\mathbb{E}CS_i(\theta)$ beyond the boundary is determined by the starting value: zero in the IR-constrained case and $\overline\varepsilon_i\,\mathrm{Cov}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})$ in the IR-unconstrained case, giving the patterns stated in the proposition. Under $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\le 0$, all signs are reversed throughout.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:outer} (Across-category allocation)}

\begin{proof}
The feasible set
\[
\mathcal F := \left\{ \{(k_i,I_i)\}_{i\in N} : \sum_{i\in N} k_i = k,\ \sum_{i\in N} I_i = I,\ k_i\ge 0,\ I_i\ge 0\ \forall i \right\}
\]
is compact and convex. Continuity of each $V_i$ follows from Berge's maximum theorem: the objective $\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}[\Gamma_i(\theta)U(q,s)]$ and the constraints of the within-category problem are continuous in $(k_i,I_i)$, and the feasible correspondence is continuous and compact-valued under the maintained assumptions. By Lemma~\ref{lem:Vi-concave} below, each $V_i$ is concave. Hence the outer objective $W:=\sum_i V_i$ is continuous and concave on $\mathcal F$. Existence and convexity of the solution set follow from the extreme value theorem and concavity of $W$. Uniqueness under the stated condition follows from Lemma~\ref{lem:Vi-concave}(i), which gives strict concavity of each $V_i$ on the IR-constrained, capacity-binding region, hence strict concavity of $W$ at the optimum.

\begin{lemma}[Concavity of the within-category value function]\label{lem:Vi-concave}
For each category $i$, the value function $V_i(k_i,I_i)$ is concave on its feasible domain. More precisely:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item on the IR-constrained, capacity-binding region, where $\beta_i(k_i,I_i)>0$ and $\Pr(s\in T_i(k_i))>0$, the Hessian of $V_i$ is negative definite and $V_i$ is strictly concave;
    \item when $\beta_i(k_i,I_i)=0$, the Hessian is negative semidefinite;
    \item when $T_i(k_i)=\varnothing$, the Hessian is negative semidefinite.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:Vi-concave}]
Fix a category $i$. On binding states $s\in T_i(k_i)$, the pointwise first-order condition is
\[
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\mathcal G_i(\theta)=\varepsilon_i(s), \qquad \mathcal G_i(\theta)=\Gamma_i(\theta)+\beta_i J_i(\theta).
\]


\noindent\textbf{Step 1: \(k_i\)-derivative and the boundary term.}

By the envelope theorem,
\[
\partial_{k_i}V_i(k_i,I_i) = \mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s;k_i,I_i)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i(k_i)\}}\right].
\]
Differentiating with respect to $k_i$ gives a boundary term proportional to $\varepsilon_i(s_i)$. At the threshold state, the off-peak condition $u(q_i(\theta,s_i),s_i)=0$ holds for every $\theta$, and combined with the first-order condition this gives $\varepsilon_i(s_i)=0$. Hence the boundary term vanishes and
\[
V_{i,k_ik_i}(k_i,I_i) = \mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\partial_{k_i}\varepsilon_i(s;k_i,I_i)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i(k_i)\}}\right].
\]


\noindent\textbf{Step 2: \(\partial_{I_i}\varepsilon_i(s)\) and the cross derivative.}

Fix $k_i$ and $s\in T_i$. Differentiating the first-order condition with respect to $I_i$ and imposing the binding capacity constraint $\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[\partial_{I_i}q_i(\theta,s)]=0$ yields
\[
\partial_{I_i}\varepsilon_i(s) = \partial_{I_i}\beta_i\,\mathsf H_i(k_i,s), \qquad \mathsf H_i(k_i,s):=\mathbb E_i^w[uJ\mid s],
\]
where $w_i(\theta,s):=-1/(u_q(q_i,s)\mathcal G_i(\theta))>0$ and the weighted expectation is as defined in Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir}. Differentiating $\Phi_i(k_i,\beta_i(k_i,I_i))=0$ with respect to $I_i$, using $\partial_\beta\Phi_i=\mathcal V_i(k_i)>0$, gives $\partial_{I_i}\beta_i=1/\mathcal V_i(k_i)$ and hence $\partial_{I_i}\varepsilon_i(s)=\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)/\mathcal V_i(k_i)$. The boundary term vanishes again since $\varepsilon_i(s_i)=0$, so defining $\mathcal S_i(k_i):=\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i(k_i)\}}\right]$,
\[
V_{i,I_i}(k_i,I_i)= -\tilde\lambda_i-\beta_i(k_i,I_i), \qquad V_{i,I_iI_i}(k_i,I_i)= -\frac{1}{\mathcal V_i(k_i)}<0, \qquad V_{i,k_iI_i}(k_i,I_i) = \frac{\mathcal S_i(k_i)}{\mathcal V_i(k_i)}.
\]


\noindent\textbf{Step 3: \(V_{i,k_ik_i}\) on the IR-constrained region.}

Holding $\beta$ fixed gives $\partial_{k_i}\varepsilon_i(s)|_\beta = -1/\overline w_i(s)$. Along the optimal path, the chain rule and the identity $\partial_\beta\varepsilon_i(s)=\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)$ (established in the proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir}) give
\[
\partial_{k_i}\varepsilon_i(s) = -\frac{1}{\overline w_i(s)} - \mathsf H_i(k_i,s)\frac{\mathcal S_i(k_i)}{\mathcal V_i(k_i)}.
\]
Integrating over binding states and defining $\mathcal A_i(k_i):=\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\overline w_i(s)^{-1}\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i(k_i)\}}\right]>0$,
\[
V_{i,k_ik_i}(k_i,I_i) = -\mathcal A_i(k_i)-\frac{\mathcal S_i(k_i)^2}{\mathcal V_i(k_i)}<0.
\]
The Hessian on the IR-constrained, capacity-binding region is
\[
\nabla^2 V_i(k_i,I_i) = \begin{pmatrix} -\mathcal A_i-\mathcal S_i^2/\mathcal V_i & \mathcal S_i/\mathcal V_i \\[0.4em] \mathcal S_i/\mathcal V_i & -1/\mathcal V_i \end{pmatrix},
\]
with determinant $\det\nabla^2 V_i = \mathcal A_i/\mathcal V_i > 0$. Both diagonal entries are negative and the determinant is positive, so $\nabla^2 V_i$ is negative definite, proving part~(i).


\noindent\textbf{Step 4: the remaining regimes.}

If $\beta_i=0$, then $V_i$ is locally affine in $I_i$, so $V_{i,I_iI_i}=0$ and $V_{i,k_iI_i}=0$. If capacity binds on a positive-mass set of states, the fixed-$\beta$ formula gives $V_{i,k_ik_i}=-\mathcal A_i\le 0$, so the Hessian is negative semidefinite, proving part~(ii). If $T_i(k_i)=\varnothing$, all second derivatives vanish and the Hessian is the zero matrix, proving part~(iii).


\noindent\textbf{Step 5: global concavity across regime boundaries.}

It remains to verify that $\nabla V_i$ is continuous across the two regime boundaries, which is required for the piecewise concavity argument to yield global concavity.

\textit{IR-constrained to IR-unconstrained.} From Step~2, $V_{i,I_i}=-\tilde\lambda_i-\beta_i$. As $(k_i,I_i)$ approaches the boundary from the constrained side, $\beta_i\to 0^+$ by definition, so $V_{i,I_i}\to -\tilde\lambda_i$, matching the unconstrained value. As $\beta_i\to 0^+$, the allocation and hence $\varepsilon_i(s)$ converge to their unconstrained counterparts, so $\partial_{k_i}V_i$ is continuous across this boundary.

\textit{Capacity-binding to capacity-slack.} As $s_i(k_i)\to\overline s$, $\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i(k_i)\}}\right]\to 0$ continuously, matching the slack-side value of zero.

With $\nabla V_i$ continuous at both boundaries, fix any two feasible points $x^0,x^1$ and let $g_i(t):=V_i((1-t)x^0+tx^1)$. Then $g_i$ is piecewise $C^2$ with $g_i'$ continuous at every switching point. On each regime piece $g_i''(t)\le 0$, so $g_i'$ is nonincreasing on each piece. Continuity at switching points prevents upward jumps in $g_i'$, so $g_i'$ is globally nonincreasing and $g_i$ is concave. Hence $V_i$ is globally concave.
\end{proof}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Local category ranking}


\begin{proof}
Since $V_i(k_i,I_i)$ is strictly concave in $k_i$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:Vi-concave}, the marginal scarcity rent
\[
\Pi_i(k_i):=\partial_{k_i}V_i(k_i,I_i)
=
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s;k_i)\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i(k_i)\}}
\right]
\]
is strictly decreasing in $k_i$. At the outer optimum, the stationarity condition equalizes $\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)=\mu_k$ across all active categories. Therefore, to first order in $\Delta\lambda$,
\[
k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\Delta \Pi_i(k_i^\ast)>0,
\]
where $\Delta \Pi_i(k_i^\ast):=\Pi_j(k_i^\ast)-\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)$ is the change in marginal scarcity rent induced by the preference perturbation, evaluated at the baseline capacity $k_i^\ast$. By the envelope theorem,
\[
\Delta \Pi_i(k_i^\ast)
=
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[
\Delta \varepsilon_i(s;k_i^\ast)\,
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i(k_i^\ast)\}}
\right].
\]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}, which holds for any allocation regime,
\[
\frac{\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)}{\varepsilon_i(s)}
=
\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]
\qquad
\text{for each } s\in T_i(k_i^\ast),
\]
where $R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)=\Delta \mathcal G_i(\theta)/\mathcal G_i(\theta)$ and $\mathcal G_i(\theta)=\Gamma_i(\theta)+J_i(\theta)\beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$. Substituting gives
\[
\Delta \Pi_i(k_i^\ast)
=
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s;k_i^\ast)\,
\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s;k_i^\ast\right]
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i(k_i^\ast)\}}
\right],
\]
which proves the first claim. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, $u_q=-\alpha u$, so $w_i(\theta,s)=1/(\alpha\varepsilon_i(s))$ is independent of $\theta$. Therefore $\mathbb{E}_i^w[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s]=\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G}(\theta,k_i^\ast)]$ for every state $s$, and the condition reduces to
\[
\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)\cdot\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G}(\theta,k_i^\ast)]>0.
\]
Since $\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)>0$ at any interior optimum, the sign is determined solely by $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G}(\theta,k_i^\ast)]$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:ordered-path}}

\begin{lemma}[Revenue positivity]\label{lem:rev-pos}
Under the maintained assumption that $J_i'(\theta)>0$, if $I_i=0$ then $\beta_i=0$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:rev-pos}]
Suppose for contradiction that $I_i=0$ and $\beta_i>0$. Then complementary slackness on the IR/budget constraint requires $\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}[U(q_i,s)J_i(\theta)]=0$. However, since $J_i'(\theta)>0$ and $q_i(\theta,s)$ is increasing in $\theta$ due to the IC constraint, hence both $J_i(\theta)$ and $U(q_i(\theta,s),s)$ are increasing in $\theta$. Therefore $\mathrm{Cov}_{\theta_i}(J_i,U)\ge 0$, and combined with $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[J_i]=\underline\theta_i>0$ and $U>0$ at any interior allocation, we obtain $\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}[U(q_i,s)J_i(\theta)]>0$ strictly, a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}
We proceed in three steps.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 1: Regime membership is monotone.}
Suppose for contradiction that there exists an adjacent pair \(i-1,i\) such that
\[
\beta_{i-1}^\ast=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\beta_i^\ast>0.
\]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:rev-pos}, \(\beta_i^\ast>0\) implies \(I_i^\ast>0\). Hence the outer KKT condition for category \(i\) gives
\[
\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i^\ast=-\mu_I.
\]
For category \(i-1\), the stationarity condition with \(\beta_{i-1}^\ast=0\) gives
\[
\tilde\lambda_{i-1}=-\mu_I+\xi_{i-1},
\qquad
\xi_{i-1}\ge 0.
\]
Therefore
\[
\tilde\lambda_{i-1}\ge -\mu_I=\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i^\ast>\tilde\lambda_i,
\]
which contradicts \(\tilde\lambda_i\ge \tilde\lambda_{i-1}\). Hence \(N^+\) is an initial segment and \(N^0\) a terminal segment of \(N\).

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 2: The sequence \((\beta_i^\ast)_{i\in N^+}\) is weakly decreasing.}
Fix \(i\in N^+\). Since \(\beta_i^\ast>0\), Lemma~\ref{lem:rev-pos} implies \(I_i^\ast>0\), so the outer KKT condition yields
\[
\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i^\ast=-\mu_I.
\]
Thus, for every \(i\in N^+\),
\[
\beta_i^\ast=-\mu_I-\tilde\lambda_i.
\]
Because \(\tilde\lambda_i\) is weakly increasing in \(i\) under Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path}, it follows that \(\beta_i^\ast\) is weakly decreasing on \(N^+\).

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 3: Capacity is weakly increasing across adjacent categories.}
Fix an adjacent pair \(i-1,i\), and let
\[
t\longmapsto \lambda(t,\theta)
\]
be the ordered path from Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path}, with endpoints
\[
\lambda(0,\theta)=\lambda_{i-1}(\theta),
\qquad
\lambda(1,\theta)=\lambda_i(\theta).
\]
Let
\[
t\longmapsto \bigl(k^\ast(t),I^\ast(t),\beta^\ast(t)\bigr)
\]
denote the associated equilibrium path.

By the argument of Step 1 applied pointwise along the path, the regime \(\beta^\ast(t)\) can move from \(>0\) to \(0\), but never from \(0\) to \(>0\). Hence, for almost every \(t\), one of the following three local cases applies between \(t\) and \(t+h\) for all sufficiently small \(h>0\):
\[
(\beta^\ast(t),\beta^\ast(t+h))=(0,0),\qquad
(\beta^\ast(t),\beta^\ast(t+h))=(+, +),\qquad
(\beta^\ast(t),\beta^\ast(t+h))=(+,0).
\]

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path}, the local perturbation from \(t\) to \(t+h\) satisfies:
\[
\frac{\lambda(t+h,\theta)}{\lambda(t,\theta)}
\quad\text{is weakly increasing in }\theta,
\]
and
\[
\tilde\lambda(t+h)\ge \tilde\lambda(t).
\]
We now verify that this implies
\[
\mathcal G(\theta;t+h)-\mathcal G(\theta;t)\ge 0
\qquad\text{for all }\theta\in\Theta.
\]

\smallskip
\noindent\underline{Case 1: \(\beta^\ast(t)=\beta^\ast(t+h)=0\).}
Then
\[
\mathcal G(\theta;t)=\Gamma(\theta;t),
\qquad
\mathcal G(\theta;t+h)=\Gamma(\theta;t+h),
\]
so
\[
c_{\mathcal G}=c_\Lambda=\frac{\tilde\lambda(t+h)}{\tilde\lambda(t)}\ge 1.
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder} therefore yields
\[
\mathcal G(\theta;t+h)-\mathcal G(\theta;t)\ge 0
\qquad
\text{for all }\theta.
\]

\smallskip
\noindent\underline{Case 2: \(\beta^\ast(t)>0\) and \(\beta^\ast(t+h)>0\).}
Since both categories are in \(N^+\), the outer KKT conditions give
\[
\tilde\lambda(t)+\beta^\ast(t)
=
\tilde\lambda(t+h)+\beta^\ast(t+h)
=
-\mu_I.
\]
Hence
\[
c_{\mathcal G}=1,
\qquad
c_\Lambda=\frac{\tilde\lambda(t+h)}{\tilde\lambda(t)}\ge 1.
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder} again implies
\[
\mathcal G(\theta;t+h)-\mathcal G(\theta;t)\ge 0
\qquad
\text{for all }\theta.
\]

\smallskip
\noindent\underline{Case 3: \(\beta^\ast(t)>0\) and \(\beta^\ast(t+h)=0\).}
From the KKT conditions,
\[
\tilde\lambda(t)+\beta^\ast(t)=-\mu_I,
\qquad
\tilde\lambda(t+h)=-\mu_I+\xi(t+h),
\qquad
\xi(t+h)\ge 0.
\]
Therefore
\[
\tilde\lambda(t+h)\ge \tilde\lambda(t)+\beta^\ast(t),
\]
so
\[
c_{\mathcal G}
=
\frac{\tilde\lambda(t+h)}{\tilde\lambda(t)+\beta^\ast(t)}
\ge 1.
\]
Moreover,
\[
c_\Lambda
=
\frac{\tilde\lambda(t+h)}{\tilde\lambda(t)}
\ge
\frac{\tilde\lambda(t+h)}{\tilde\lambda(t)+\beta^\ast(t)}
=
c_{\mathcal G}.
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder} then gives
\[
\mathcal G(\theta;t+h)-\mathcal G(\theta;t)\ge 0
\qquad
\text{for all }\theta.
\]

\smallskip
In all three cases,
\[
\mathcal G(\theta;t+h)-\mathcal G(\theta;t)\ge 0
\qquad
\text{for all }\theta,
\]
hence
\[
R_{\mathcal G}(\theta;t):=
\frac{\partial_t\mathcal G(\theta;t)}{\mathcal G(\theta;t)}
\ge 0
\qquad
\text{for all }\theta
\]
for almost every \(t\). Since the weights defining \(\mathbb E^w[\cdot\mid s]\) are positive,
\[
\mathbb E^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s;t\right]\ge 0
\qquad
\text{for every peak state } s.
\]
Applying Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order} at the current optimum
\[
\bigl(k^\ast(t),I^\ast(t)\bigr)
\]
yields
\[
\frac{d k^\ast(t)}{dt}\ge 0
\qquad
\text{for almost every } t\in[0,1].
\]
Integrating from \(t=0\) to \(t=1\) gives
\[
k_i^\ast=k^\ast(1)\ge k^\ast(0)=k_{i-1}^\ast.
\]

Since this holds for every adjacent pair \(i-1,i\), we conclude that
\[
k_1^\ast\le \cdots \le k_n^\ast.
\]
This proves (iii), and hence the corollary.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:revenueordering}}
\begin{proof}
\textit{General case.} Since \(\beta_i^\ast>0\) and \(\beta_j^\ast>0\), both categories are interior contributors. By Proposition~\ref{prop:outer}, the revenue stationarity condition holds with equality and without slackness:
\[
\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast) = \tilde\lambda_j+\beta_j(k_j^\ast,I_j^\ast).
\]
Let $\Delta\tilde\lambda:=\tilde\lambda_j-\tilde\lambda_i$, $\Delta k:=k_j^\ast-k_i^\ast$, $\Delta I:=I_j^\ast-I_i^\ast$. Since category \(j\) is obtained from \(i\) through a marginal perturbation, a first-order expansion around \((k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\) gives
\[
0 = \Delta\tilde\lambda + \frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,\Delta k + \frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial I_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,\Delta I.
\]
Rearranging,
\[
\Delta I = -\frac{\Delta\tilde\lambda + \frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,\Delta k}{\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial I_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)}.
\]
From Lemma~\ref{lem:Vi-concave}, $\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial I_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)=1/\mathcal{V}_i(k_i^\ast)>0$. Hence $\Delta I<0$ if and only if $\Delta\tilde\lambda+\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,\Delta k<0$. Since $\Delta k>0$ by hypothesis, dividing by $\Delta k$ yields
\[
I_j^\ast<I_i^\ast \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast) < -\frac{\Delta\tilde\lambda}{\Delta k}.
\]
From the preliminary step of Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir}, the sign of $\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)=-\mathcal{S}_i(k_i^\ast)/\mathcal{V}_i(k_i^\ast)$ is opposite to the sign of \eqref{Eujmd}. This proves the general condition.

\textit{CARA reduction.} Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, $w_i(\theta,s)=1/(\alpha\varepsilon_i(s))$ is $\theta$-independent, so $\overline{w}_i(s)=1/(\alpha\varepsilon_i(s))$ and $\mathsf{H}_i(k_i,s)=\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[u(q_i,s)J_i(\theta)]=\varepsilon_i(s)\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\theta]$. Integrating over binding states gives $\mathcal{S}_i(k_i^\ast)=\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\theta]\cdot\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)$. Since $\mathrm{Var}_i^w(uJ\mid s)=\varepsilon_i(s)^2\mathrm{Var}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta)$ under CARA, one obtains $\mathcal{V}_i(k_i^\ast)=\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)\mathrm{Var}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta)/\alpha$. The $\Pi_i$ factors cancel and
\[
\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast) = -\frac{\mathcal{S}_i}{\mathcal{V}_i} = -\frac{\alpha\,\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\theta]}{\mathrm{Var}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta)}.
\]
For $\Delta k$, since $\Delta\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)=\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}]\cdot\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)$ and $\Pi_i'(k_i^\ast)=-\mathcal{A}_i-\mathcal{S}_i^2/\mathcal{V}_i$, the CARA expressions $\mathcal{A}_i=\alpha\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)$ and $\mathcal{S}_i^2/\mathcal{V}_i=\alpha(\mathbb{E}[R_\theta])^2\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)/\mathrm{Var}(R_\theta)$ yield
\[
\Pi_i'(k_i^\ast) = -\frac{\alpha\Pi_i(k_i^\ast)\,\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\theta^2]}{\mathrm{Var}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta)},
\]
using $\mathrm{Var}(R_\theta)+(\mathbb{E}[R_\theta])^2=\mathbb{E}[R_\theta^2]$. Hence $\Delta k=-\Delta\Pi_i/\Pi_i'=\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}]\,\mathrm{Var}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta)/(\alpha\,\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\theta^2])$. Substituting into the general condition,
\[
-\frac{\alpha\,\mathbb{E}[R_\theta]}{\mathrm{Var}(R_\theta)} < -\Delta\tilde\lambda\cdot\frac{\alpha\,\mathbb{E}[R_\theta^2]}{\mathbb{E}[R_\mathcal{G}]\,\mathrm{Var}(R_\theta)}.
\]
Multiplying both sides by $-\mathrm{Var}(R_\theta)/\alpha<0$ reverses the inequality:
\[
\mathbb{E}[R_\theta] > \Delta\tilde\lambda\cdot\frac{\mathbb{E}[R_\theta^2]}{\mathbb{E}[R_\mathcal{G}]}.
\]
Since $\mathbb{E}[R_\mathcal{G}]>0$ (as $\Delta k>0$) and $\mathbb{E}[R_\theta^2]>0$, rearranging gives the stated condition.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:investment}}
\begin{proof}
Define the outer indirect value
\[
\overline W(K,I)
:=
\max_{\{k_i,I_i\}_{i\in N}}
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i\,V_i(k_i,I_i)
\]
subject to
\[
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i k_i = K,
\qquad
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i I_i = I,
\qquad
k_i\geq 0,
\qquad
I_i\geq 0
\quad \forall i\in N.
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:outer}, $\overline W$ is well defined and continuous, and by Lemma~\ref{lem:Vi-concave} it is concave in $(K,I)$. Moreover, since
\[
\partial_{I_i}V_i(k_i,I_i)=-\tilde\lambda_i-\beta_i(k_i,I_i)<0,
\]
the function $\overline W(K,I)$ is strictly decreasing in $I$.

Now define
\[
W(k):=\overline W\!\left(k,I(k)\right).
\]
Let $k_0,k_1\geq 0$ and $t\in(0,1)$. By concavity of $\overline W$ and convexity of $I$,
\[
\begin{aligned}
W\!\left(tk_0+(1-t)k_1\right)
&=
\overline W\!\left(tk_0+(1-t)k_1,\ I\!\left(tk_0+(1-t)k_1\right)\right) \\
&\geq
\overline W\!\left(tk_0+(1-t)k_1,\ tI(k_0)+(1-t)I(k_1)\right) \\
&\geq
t\,W(k_0)+(1-t)\,W(k_1).
\end{aligned}
\]
Since $I$ is strictly convex and $\overline W$ is strictly decreasing in its second argument, the first inequality is strict whenever $k_0\neq k_1$. Hence $W$ is strictly concave, so any maximizer is unique.

By the maintained Inada condition on $u$, the marginal value of capacity tends to $+\infty$ as $k\downarrow 0$, while strict convexity of $I$ implies $W(k)\to -\infty$ for large $k$. Therefore the unique maximizer satisfies $k^\ast>0$.

At any point of differentiability, the envelope theorem for the outer problem gives
\[
W'(k)=\zeta_k+\zeta_I I'(k),
\]
where $\zeta_k$ and $\zeta_I$ are the multipliers on the two aggregate constraints. Since $k^\ast$ is an interior maximizer,
\[
0=W'(k^\ast)=\zeta_k+\zeta_I I'(k^\ast).
\]
Let
\[
\tilde\lambda^\ast:=-\zeta_I>0.
\]
Then
\[
\tilde\lambda^\ast I'(k^\ast)=\zeta_k.
\]

By Proposition~\ref{prop:outer},
\[
\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)=\zeta_k
\qquad\text{for every }i,
\]
where, by the within-category envelope formula and the first-order condition~\eqref{KKT-qMD},
\[
\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)
=
\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[
u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\mathcal G_i(\theta)\,\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}
\right].
\]

Let
\[
\mu^+ := \sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i.
\]
Since $\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)=\zeta_k$ for every contributing category,
\[
\mu^+\,\zeta_k
=
\sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\,\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast).
\]
Combining this with $\tilde\lambda^\ast I'(k^\ast)=\zeta_k$ yields
\[
I'(k^\ast)
=
\frac{1}{\mu^+}
\sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\,
\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[
u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,
\frac{\mathcal G_i(\theta)}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}\,
\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}
\right].
\]

For the comparative statics below, it is convenient to note the equivalent whole-$N$ representation
\[
I'(k^\ast)
=
\frac{1}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i\,\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast),
\]
which follows from the same identity $\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)=\zeta_k$.

Finally, the outer KKT condition is
\[
\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)=\tilde\lambda^\ast+\xi_i,
\qquad
\xi_i I_i^\ast=0,
\qquad
\xi_i\geq 0.
\]
If $i\in N^+$, then $\beta_i^\ast>0$, so
\[
\tilde\lambda_i<\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i^\ast=\tilde\lambda^\ast+\xi_i.
\]
Since contributing categories are interior in the revenue margin, $\xi_i=0$, and therefore
\[
\tilde\lambda_i<\tilde\lambda^\ast.
\]
If instead $i\in N^0$, then $\beta_i^\ast=0$, so
\[
\tilde\lambda_i=\tilde\lambda^\ast+\xi_i\geq \tilde\lambda^\ast.
\]
Thus
\[
\max_{i\in N^+}\tilde\lambda_i
<
\tilde\lambda^\ast
\leq
\min_{i\in N^0}\tilde\lambda_i.
\qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:investment}}
\begin{proof}
Let \(\Delta_\lambda\) denote the first-order effect of the perturbation of
\(\lambda_i(\theta)\) evaluated at the baseline equilibrium allocation.

From Proposition~\ref{prop:investment}, we use the equivalent whole-\(N\)
representation
\[
I'(k^\ast)
=
\frac{1}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i\,\Pi_i.
\]
Differentiating this expression with respect to the perturbation yields
\[
W''(k^\ast)\,\Delta k^\ast
=
I'(k^\ast)\,\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast
-
\frac{1}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i\,\Delta_\lambda \Pi_i.
\]

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the same simplification as in
Corollary~\ref{prop:cat-order-beta0} applies, so for every category \(i\),
\[
\Delta_\lambda \Pi_i
=
\Pi_i\,\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G,i}].
\]
Substituting into the previous display gives
\[
W''(k^\ast)\,\Delta k^\ast
=
I'(k^\ast)\,\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast
-
\frac{1}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i\,\Pi_i\,\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G,i}].
\]

It remains to characterize \(\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast\). For every contributing
category \(i\in N^+\), the revenue constraint binds. Since the perturbation is
mean-preserving, \(\Delta\tilde\lambda_i=0\), and Proposition~\ref{prop:investment}
implies
\[
\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i^\ast=\tilde\lambda^\ast
\qquad\text{for all }i\in N^+,
\]
hence
\[
\Delta\beta_i^\ast=\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast.
\]
Taking the first-order effect of the perturbation in the binding condition gives
\[
\mathcal V_i(k_i^\ast)\,\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast
+
\Delta_\lambda
\mathbb E_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[
U(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,J_i(\theta)
\right]
=
0.
\]

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, we have
\[
\mathcal V_i(k_i^\ast)
=
\frac{\overline\varepsilon_i}{\alpha}\,
\Var_{\theta_i}\!\left(R_{\theta,i}(\theta)\right),
\qquad
\overline\varepsilon_i
:=
\mathbb E_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s)\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}
\right],
\]
and
\[
\Delta_\lambda
\mathbb E_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[
U(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,J_i(\theta)
\right]
=
\frac{\overline\varepsilon_i}{\alpha}\,
\Cov_{\theta_i}\!\left(
R_{\theta,i}(\theta),R_{\mathcal G,i}(\theta)
\right).
\]
Therefore, summing over all contributing categories \(i\in N^+\) with weights
\(\mu_i\), we obtain
\[
\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast
=
-
\frac{
\sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\,\overline\varepsilon_i\,
\Cov_{\theta_i}\!\left(
R_{\theta,i}(\theta),R_{\mathcal G,i}(\theta)
\right)
}{
\sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\,\overline\varepsilon_i\,
\Var_{\theta_i}\!\left(
R_{\theta,i}(\theta)
\right)
}.
\]

Finally, under a smooth ordered path that tilts redistributive preferences toward
higher types, we have
\[
\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G,i}]>0
\qquad\text{for all }i\in N.
\]
Hence the direct term
\[
-\frac{1}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i\,\Pi_i\,\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G,i}]
\]
is negative. Since \(W''(k^\ast)<0\), this direct channel pushes \(\Delta k^\ast\)
upward. The overall sign of \(\Delta k^\ast\) nevertheless also depends on the
induced change in \(\tilde\lambda^\ast\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Single crossing}
\begin{proof}
For each binding state $s\in T_i(k_i)$ and each $k_i$, define
\[
\mathsf H_i(k_i,s):=\mathbb E_i^w[uJ\mid s],
\qquad
\mathcal S_i(k_i):=\int_{s_i(k_i)}^{\overline s}\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)\,f(s)\,ds.
\]

For every binding state $s\in T_i(k_i)$, define the probability measure
\[
dP_{k_i,s}(\theta):=
\frac{R_q(\theta,k_i,s)\,g_i(\theta)}
{\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_q(\theta,k_i,s)]}\,d\theta.
\]
Since
\[
R_\theta(\theta,k_i)=\frac{J_i(\theta)}{\mathcal G_i(\theta,k_i)},
\]
we can write
\[
\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)
=
\frac{\mathbb E_{\theta_i}\!\left[R_q(\theta,k_i,s)\,R_\theta(\theta,k_i)\right]}
{\mathbb E_{\theta_i}\!\left[R_q(\theta,k_i,s)\right]}
=
\int_{\Theta_i} R_\theta(\theta,k_i)\,dP_{k_i,s}(\theta).
\]

Differentiating $\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)$ with respect to $k_i$ gives
\[
\frac{\partial \mathsf H_i(k_i,s)}{\partial k_i}
=
M_i(k_i,s)+D_i(k_i,s),
\]
where
\[
M_i(k_i,s)
:=
\left.
\frac{\partial}{\partial t}
\int_{\Theta_i}R_\theta(\theta,k_i)\,dP_{t,s}(\theta)
\right|_{t=k_i}
\]
is the pure measure-shift term, and
\[
D_i(k_i,s)
:=
\int_{\Theta_i}
\frac{\partial R_\theta(\theta,k_i)}{\partial k_i}\,dP_{k_i,s}(\theta)
\]
is the direct effect through the $k_i$-dependence of $R_\theta$.

By the log-monotone-differences assumption on $R_q$, the family $\{P_{k_i,s}\}_{k_i}$ is ordered by monotone likelihood ratio for each binding state $s$. Hence, since $\theta\mapsto R_\theta(\theta,k_i)$ is monotone for each $k_i$,
\[
M_i(k_i,s)\ge 0
\]
in the two cases
\[
R_\theta(\cdot,k_i)\ \text{increasing and }R_q\ \text{log-supermodular},
\qquad
R_\theta(\cdot,k_i)\ \text{decreasing and }R_q\ \text{log-submodular},
\]
while
\[
M_i(k_i,s)\le 0
\]
in the two opposite cases.

Next, since
\[
R_\theta(\theta,k_i)=\frac{J_i(\theta)}{\mathcal G_i(\theta,k_i)},
\qquad
\mathcal G_i(\theta,k_i)=\Gamma_i(\theta)+J_i(\theta)\beta_i(k_i),
\]
we have
\[
\frac{\partial R_\theta(\theta,k_i)}{\partial k_i}
=
-\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)\,
\frac{J_i(\theta)^2}{\mathcal G_i(\theta,k_i)^2}.
\]
Therefore
\[
D_i(k_i,s)
=
-\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)\,\mathcal Q_i(k_i,s),
\]
where
\[
\mathcal Q_i(k_i,s)
:=
\int_{\Theta_i}
\frac{J_i(\theta)^2}{\mathcal G_i(\theta,k_i)^2}\,dP_{k_i,s}(\theta)
>0.
\]
Thus
\[
\frac{\partial \mathsf H_i(k_i,s)}{\partial k_i}
=
M_i(k_i,s)-\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)\,\mathcal Q_i(k_i,s).
\]

We now relate $\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)$ to $\mathcal S_i(k_i)$. On the IR-constrained region, define
\[
\Phi_i(k_i,\beta)
:=
\mathbb E_s\!\left[
\mathbb E_{\theta_i}\!\left[
U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s;k_i,\beta),s\right)J_i(\theta)
\right]
\right]
-I_i.
\]
By construction,
\[
\Phi_i\!\left(k_i,\beta_i(k_i)\right)=0.
\]

Holding $\beta$ fixed, the standard comparative-statics identity gives, for every binding state $s$,
\[
\frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s;k_i,\beta)}{\partial k_i}
=
\frac{w_i(\theta,s)}{\overline w_i(s)}.
\]
Hence
\[
\partial_{k_i}\Phi_i(k_i,\beta)
=
\mathbb E_s\!\left[
\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i(k_i)\}}
\,
\mathbb E_i^w[uJ\mid s]
\right]
=
\mathcal S_i(k_i),
\]
where the moving-threshold term vanishes because
\[
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s_i(k_i)),s_i(k_i)\right)=0
\qquad
\text{for every }\theta.
\]

Holding $k_i$ fixed, differentiating the pointwise FOC
\[
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\mathcal G_i(\theta,k_i)
=
\varepsilon_i(s)
\]
with respect to $\beta$, using $\partial_\beta\mathcal G_i(\theta,k_i)=J_i(\theta)$ and imposing
\[
\mathbb E_{\theta_i}\!\left[\partial_\beta q_i(\theta,s)\right]=0,
\]
yields
\[
\frac{\partial \varepsilon_i(s)}{\partial \beta}
=
\mathbb E_i^w[uJ\mid s]
=
\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)
\]
and
\[
\frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s)}{\partial \beta}
=
w_i(\theta,s)
\left(
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)J_i(\theta)
-
\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)
\right).
\]
Therefore
\[
\partial_\beta \Phi_i(k_i,\beta)
=
\mathbb E_s\!\left[
\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i(k_i)\}}
\,
\overline w_i(s)\,
\mathrm{Var}_i^w(uJ\mid s)
\right]
=:
\mathcal V_i(k_i),
\]
with $\mathcal V_i(k_i)>0$ away from knife-edge cases. Differentiating
\[
\Phi_i\!\left(k_i,\beta_i(k_i)\right)=0
\]
with respect to $k_i$ gives
\[
0
=
\mathcal S_i(k_i)+\mathcal V_i(k_i)\,\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i),
\]
hence
\[
\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)
=
-\frac{\mathcal S_i(k_i)}{\mathcal V_i(k_i)}.
\]

Finally, differentiate
\[
\mathcal S_i(k_i)
=
\int_{s_i(k_i)}^{\overline s}\mathsf H_i(k_i,s)\,f(s)\,ds.
\]
Using Leibniz' rule,
\[
\mathcal S_i'(k_i)
=
-
\mathsf H_i\!\left(k_i,s_i(k_i)\right)
f\!\left(s_i(k_i)\right)
s_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)
+
\int_{s_i(k_i)}^{\overline s}
\frac{\partial \mathsf H_i(k_i,s)}{\partial k_i}\,f(s)\,ds.
\]
At the threshold state $s_i(k_i)$ we have
\[
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s_i(k_i)),s_i(k_i)\right)=0
\qquad
\text{for every }\theta,
\]
so $\mathsf H_i\!\left(k_i,s_i(k_i)\right)=0$. Hence the boundary term vanishes and
\[
\mathcal S_i'(k_i)
=
\overline M_i(k_i)-\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i)\,\overline{\mathcal Q}_i(k_i),
\]
where
\[
\overline M_i(k_i)
:=
\int_{s_i(k_i)}^{\overline s}M_i(k_i,s)\,f(s)\,ds,
\qquad
\overline{\mathcal Q}_i(k_i)
:=
\int_{s_i(k_i)}^{\overline s}\mathcal Q_i(k_i,s)\,f(s)\,ds
>0.
\]

Now let $k_i^0$ satisfy $\mathcal S_i(k_i^0)=0$. Then
\[
\beta_i^{\times\prime}(k_i^0)=0,
\]
so
\[
\mathcal S_i'(k_i^0)=\overline M_i(k_i^0).
\]
Since each $M_i(k_i^0,s)$ has the same sign across binding states, $\overline M_i(k_i^0)$ has that same sign. Therefore every sign-changing zero of $\mathcal S_i$ is crossed in the same direction:
\[
\mathcal S_i'(k_i^0)>0
\]
when
\[
R_\theta(\cdot,k_i)\ \text{increasing and }R_q\ \text{log-supermodular},
\qquad
R_\theta(\cdot,k_i)\ \text{decreasing and }R_q\ \text{log-submodular},
\]
so any sign change is from $-$ to $+$; and
\[
\mathcal S_i'(k_i^0)<0
\]
in the two opposite cases, so any sign change is from $+$ to $-$.

Thus the map
\[
k_i\longmapsto \mathcal S_i(k_i)
=
\mathbb E_s\!\left[
\mathbb E_i^w[uJ\mid s]\,
\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i(k_i)\}}
\right]
\]
is single-crossing in $k_i$, with crossing direction pinned down by the combination of monotonicities stated in the lemma.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:Elasticity}}

\begin{proof}
The proof proceeds in three steps.

\noindent\textit{Step 1: the elasticity condition implies that $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ is monotone.}
Using the identity established above,
\[
\Delta R_\theta(\theta)=-R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)R_\theta(\theta),
\]
we differentiate to obtain
\[
\Delta R_\theta'(\theta)
=
-R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)-R_\theta(\theta)R_\mathcal{G}'(\theta).
\]
If the two terms on the right-hand side have the same sign, then $\Delta R_\theta'(\theta)$ has that sign directly. If they have opposite signs, then
\[
\operatorname{sign}\!\left(\Delta R_\theta'(\theta)\right)
=
\operatorname{sign}\!\left(-R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)\right)
\]
whenever
\[
|R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)|
\ge
|R_\theta(\theta)R_\mathcal{G}'(\theta)|.
\]
Since $R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)$ and $R_\theta(\theta)$ are nonzero on the relevant domain, this condition is equivalent to
\[
\left|\frac{R_\mathcal{G}'(\theta)}{R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)}\right|
\le
\left|\frac{R_\theta'(\theta)}{R_\theta(\theta)}\right|.
\]
Hence, under the elasticity condition,
\[
\operatorname{sign}\!\left(\Delta R_\theta'(\theta)\right)
=
\operatorname{sign}\!\left(-R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)\right)
\qquad\text{for all }\theta.
\]
Because $R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)$ has constant sign and $R_\theta(\theta)$ is monotone, the right-hand side has constant sign. Therefore $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ is globally monotone.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 2: monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ determines the sign of the covariance.}
By Step~1, $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ is monotone. Since $R_q(\theta)$ is also monotone by assumption, the standard monotone-covariance property implies
\[
\operatorname{sign}\!\left(
\operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(\Delta R_\theta(\theta),R_q(\theta)\right)
\right)
=
\operatorname{sign}\!\left(\Delta R_\theta'(\theta)\right)
\cdot
\operatorname{sign}\!\left(R_q'(\theta)\right).
\]
Using the conclusion of Step~1 yields
\[
\operatorname{sign}\!\left(
\operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(\Delta R_\theta(\theta),R_q(\theta)\right)
\right)
=
\operatorname{sign}\!\left(-R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)\right)
\cdot
\operatorname{sign}\!\left(R_q'(\theta)\right).
\]
This gives the claimed sign result.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 3: if the elasticity condition fails, no primitive sign result remains.}
If the elasticity condition fails at some $\theta_0$, namely if
\[
\left|\frac{R_\mathcal{G}'(\theta_0)}{R_\mathcal{G}(\theta_0)}\right|
>
\left|\frac{R_\theta'(\theta_0)}{R_\theta(\theta_0)}\right|,
\]
then the term $-R_\theta(\theta_0)R_\mathcal{G}'(\theta_0)$ dominates
$-R_\mathcal{G}(\theta_0)R_\theta'(\theta_0)$ whenever the two terms have opposite signs. In that case, the sign of $\Delta R_\theta'(\theta_0)$ is no longer pinned down by the primitive sign of $-R_\mathcal{G}(\theta_0)R_\theta'(\theta_0)$. More generally, once the elasticity condition fails, monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ can no longer be inferred from primitives alone. Since the covariance sign in Step~2 relies on that monotonicity, no distribution-free sign result for
\[
\operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(\Delta R_\theta(\theta),R_q(\theta)\right)
\]
is available in general.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:CARA}}
\begin{proof}
We work on the IR-unconstrained region characterized in Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation}. On that region, $\beta_i=0$, so $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)=\Gamma_i(\theta)$ and therefore
\[
R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)=R_\Gamma(\theta).
\]
The proof proceeds in three steps.

\noindent\textit{Step 1: under CARA, the weighted averages collapse to ordinary averages.}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, we have
\[
u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)=-\alpha\,u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)
\qquad\text{for some }\alpha>0,
\]
hence
\[
R_q(\theta):=-\frac{u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)}{u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)}=\frac{1}{\alpha},
\]
which is constant. In any on-peak state $s\in T_i(k)$, the first-order condition with $\beta_i=0$ is
\[
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\Gamma_i(\theta)=\varepsilon_i(s).
\]
Using the preliminary step in the proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc},
\[
w_i(\theta,s)
=
-\frac{1}{u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\Gamma_i(\theta)}
=
\frac{1}{\alpha\,\varepsilon_i(s)},
\]
which is constant in $\theta$. Therefore, for every on-peak state $s$,
\[
\mathbb E_i^w[X\mid s]=\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[X].
\]
Applying Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc} then yields
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s)
=
\frac{1}{\alpha}\left(
R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)-\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)]
\right),
\]
and, by the same derivation,
\[
\frac{\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)}{\varepsilon_i(s)}
=
\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)]
\qquad\text{for every }s\in T_i(k).
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 2: the sign of the covariance determines whether the implementation region expands or contracts.}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation}, the IR-unconstrained implementation region is characterized by
\[
\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k)\ge 0.
\]
Using the general perturbation formula derived in the proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation}, and the collapse of weighted averages from Step~1, we obtain on the IR-unconstrained region
\[
\Delta\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k)
=
\overline\varepsilon_i(k)\,
\operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(R_\theta(\theta),R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)\right),
\]
where
\[
\overline\varepsilon_i(k):=
\mathbb E_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s;k)\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i(k)\}}
\right]>0.
\]
Hence the sign of $\Delta\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k)$ is constant over the whole IR-unconstrained region and equals the sign of
\[
\operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(R_\theta(\theta),R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)\right).
\]
If this covariance is positive, then $\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k)$ shifts upward pointwise, so the set of $k$ such that $\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k)\ge 0$ expands:
\[
\tilde k_i^- \text{ decreases and } \tilde k_i^+ \text{ increases.}
\]
If the covariance is negative, the opposite occurs and the implementation region contracts.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 3: the same covariance determines the direction of the shift in the peak $k^\ast$.}
Let
\[
F(k):=\partial_k \mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k).
\]
By the derivative formula established before, on the IR-unconstrained region,
\[
F(k)
=
\mathbb E_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s;k)\,
\mathbb E_i^w[R_\theta\mid s;k]\,
\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i(k)\}}
\right].
\]
Under CARA, Step~1 implies $\mathbb E_i^w[R_\theta\mid s;k]=\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_\theta(\theta)]$, so
\[
F(k)=\overline\varepsilon_i(k)\,\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_\theta(\theta)].
\]
Its perturbation is therefore
\[
\Delta F(k)
=
\Delta\overline\varepsilon_i(k)\,\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_\theta(\theta)]
+
\overline\varepsilon_i(k)\,\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[\Delta R_\theta(\theta)].
\]
Using Step~1,
\[
\Delta\overline\varepsilon_i(k)
=
\overline\varepsilon_i(k)\,\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)].
\]
Moreover, since
\[
\Delta R_\theta(\theta)=-R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)R_\theta(\theta),
\]
we have
\[
\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[\Delta R_\theta(\theta)]
=
-\operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(R_\mathcal{G}(\theta),R_\theta(\theta)\right)
-\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)]\,\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_\theta(\theta)].
\]
Substituting into the expression for $\Delta F(k)$ gives
\[
\Delta F(k)
=
-\overline\varepsilon_i(k)\,
\operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(R_\mathcal{G}(\theta),R_\theta(\theta)\right).
\]
Let $k^\ast$ denote the unique maximizer of $\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k)$. Then
\[
F(k^\ast)=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
F'(k^\ast)<0.
\]
A first-order perturbation therefore satisfies
\[
\Delta k^\ast
=
-\frac{\Delta F(k^\ast)}{F'(k^\ast)},
\]
so $\Delta k^\ast$ has the same sign as $\Delta F(k^\ast)$. Hence
\[
\operatorname{sign}(\Delta k^\ast)
=
-\operatorname{sign}\!\left(
\operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(R_\mathcal{G}(\theta),R_\theta(\theta)\right)
\right).
\]
Therefore, a positive covariance shifts $k^\ast$ to the left, while a negative covariance shifts it to the right. Since on the IR-unconstrained region $R_\mathcal{G}=R_\Gamma$, this is exactly the statement of the corollary.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:dkidk}}
\begin{proof}
For each interior category \(i\), the first-order conditions of the outer problem are

V_{k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)=\tilde\lambda^\ast I'(k^\ast)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
V_{I_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)=-\tilde\lambda^\ast.

Differentiating with respect to aggregate capacity \(k\) yields

V_{k_ik_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,dk_i^\ast
+
V_{k_iI_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,dI_i^\ast

I'(k^\ast)\,d\tilde\lambda^\ast
+

and

V_{I_ik_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,dk_i^\ast
+
V_{I_iI_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,dI_i^\ast

Using the expressions established above,

V_{I_iI_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)

and

V_{k_iI_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)

V_{I_ik_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)

\mathbb E_s\!\left[\mathbb E_i^w[uJ\mid s]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]

the second differentiated first-order condition gives

\bar w_i(s)\,

Substituting into the first differentiated first-order condition yields

\left(
V_{k_ik_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)

\qquad\qquad

Multiplying both sides by \(-1\),

\left(
-
V_{k_ik_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)

\mathbb E_s\!\left[\mathbb E_i^w[uJ\mid s]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]

\mathbb E_s\!\left[
\bar w_i(s)\,

\qquad\qquad
=

Now use the identity

V_{k_ik_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)

\paragraph{Across-category problem.}
The outer problem is
\[
\max_{\{k_i,I_i\}_{i\in N}} \sum_{i\in N} V_i(k_i,I_i)
\]
subject to
\[
\sum_{i\in N} k_i = k,
\qquad
\sum_{i\in N} I_i = I,
\qquad
k_i \ge 0,
\qquad
I_i \ge 0
\qquad
\forall i\in N.
\]
Let
\[
\mathcal K
:=
\left\{
(k_i,I_i)_{i\in N}
:\,
\sum_{i\in N} k_i = k,\ 
\sum_{i\in N} I_i = I,\ 
k_i \ge 0,\ 
I_i \ge 0
\right\}.
\]
Since the objective is concave and the feasible set $\mathcal K$ is convex, the KKT conditions are necessary and sufficient.

\paragraph{Lagrangian.}
Let $\mu_k,\mu_I\in\mathbb R$ denote the multipliers on the two aggregate equality constraints, and let $\eta_i\ge 0$ and $\xi_i\ge 0$ denote the multipliers on the nonnegativity constraints $k_i\ge 0$ and $I_i\ge 0$. The Lagrangian is
\[
\mathcal L
=
\sum_{i\in N} V_i(k_i,I_i)
-\mu_k\left(\sum_{i\in N} k_i-k\right)
-\mu_I\left(\sum_{i\in N} I_i-I\right)
+\sum_{i\in N} \eta_i k_i
+\sum_{i\in N} \xi_i I_i .
\]

\paragraph{KKT conditions.}
For each $i\in N$, the stationarity conditions are
\[
\partial_{k_i}V_i(k_i,I_i)-\mu_k+\eta_i=0,
\qquad
\partial_{I_i}V_i(k_i,I_i)-\mu_I+\xi_i=0.
\]
They are supplemented by primal feasibility,
\[
\sum_{i\in N} k_i = k,
\qquad
\sum_{i\in N} I_i = I,
\qquad
k_i \ge 0,
\qquad
I_i \ge 0,
\]
dual feasibility,
\[
\eta_i\ge 0,
\qquad
\xi_i\ge 0,
\]
and complementary slackness,
\[
\eta_i k_i = 0,
\qquad
\xi_i I_i = 0
\qquad
\forall i\in N.
\]

Using the envelope formulas
\[
\partial_{k_i}V_i(k_i,I_i)
=
\mathbb E_s\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right],
\qquad
\partial_{I_i}V_i(k_i,I_i)
=
-\tilde\lambda_i-\beta_i(k_i,I_i),
\]
the stationarity conditions become
\[
\mathbb E_s\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]
=
\mu_k-\eta_i,
\]
\[
\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i(k_i,I_i)
=
-\mu_I+\xi_i.
\]




The object $\Pi_i(k_i)$ arises naturally from the envelope theorem applied to the within-category value function. On the region where $\beta_i=0$, the pointwise first-order condition gives
\[
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s;k_i),s\right)\Gamma_i(\theta)=\varepsilon_i(s;k_i)
\qquad
\text{for every }\theta \text{ and every } s\in T_i(k_i).
\]
Taking expectations over $\theta$ and using the envelope formula yields
\[
\Pi_i(k_i):=\partial_{k_i}V_i(k_i,I_i)
=
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[
\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}\!\left[
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s;k_i),s\right)\Gamma_i(\theta)
\right]
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i(k_i)\}}
\right].
\]
Hence $\Pi_i(k_i)$ is the expected scarcity rent of category $i$ at capacity level $k_i$, and is the object equalized across categories by the outer optimum. Note that on the region $\beta_i=0$, $\mathcal G_i(\theta)=\Gamma_i(\theta)$, so the ratio
\[
R_{\mathcal G}(\theta):=\frac{\Delta \mathcal G_i(\theta)}{\mathcal G_i(\theta)}
\]
defined in Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc} reduces to $\Delta \Gamma_i(\theta)/\Gamma_i(\theta)$: the same proportional reweighting that governs the reallocation of quantities within a category also governs the local ranking of capacities across categories.

\begin{proposition}[Local category ranking under redistributive preferences]\label{prop:cat-order}
Suppose all categories share the same type distribution $G$ on $\Theta$. Fix a baseline category $i$, and let category $j$ be obtained from $i$ through a marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences. Then, to first order in $\Delta\lambda$,
\[
k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s;k_i^\ast)\,
\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s;k_i^\ast\right]
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i(k_i^\ast)\}}
\right]>0.
\]
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the condition reduces to $k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast$ if and only if $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G}(\theta,k_i^\ast)]>0$.
\end{proposition}



\begin{remark}[Continuous preference ordering]
The proposition is local in preference space: it compares a baseline category $i$ to a nearby category $j$ obtained through a marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences, evaluated at the baseline optimum $k_i^\ast$. 

This local characterization becomes globally informative when categories lie on a one-dimensional preference path. Suppose additionally that categories can be embedded in a smooth one-dimensional preference path indexed by $\tau$, and redistributive preferences vary smoothly with $\tau$. Then the proposition characterizes the slope of the optimal capacity schedule $\tau\mapsto k^\ast(\tau)$: the sign of
\[
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[
\varepsilon(s;k^\ast(\tau))\,
\mathbb{E}^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s;k^\ast(\tau)\right]
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T(k^\ast(\tau))\}}
\right]
\]
determines whether optimal capacity rises or falls locally with $\tau$. If this expression is positive for every $\tau$, then $k^\ast(\tau)$ is globally increasing along the preference path; if it is negative for every $\tau$, then $k^\ast(\tau)$ is globally decreasing.

The central economic message is that the capacity ranking is governed by the \textit{proportional} reorientation of IC-adjusted social priorities, not by their absolute change. 
\[
R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)=\frac{\Delta \mathcal G_i(\theta)}{\mathcal G_i(\theta)},
\]
the proportional change in effective social priority at each type. Hence two categories may experience the same absolute shift in welfare weights, yet receive opposite capacity rankings if that shift is proportionally concentrated on different parts of the type distribution. What matters is not the raw magnitude of the preference change, but where that change falls.

In economic terms, a category receives more capacity when the perturbation shifts social priority toward types for whom relaxing capacity is more valuable in the IC-constrained allocation. The state multiplier $\varepsilon(s;k^\ast(\tau))$ then aggregates these effects across binding states according to the marginal social value of scarce capacity. A regulator who ranks categories only by the direction or size of the preference shift, ignoring its proportional and IC-weighted incidence across types, can therefore misrank them.

This connects to the within-category analysis in Section~\ref{sec:within}. There, redistributive effects operate through the IC-weighted sorting of welfare gains across types. Here, they operate through the IC-weighted reorientation of effective social priorities across categories. In both cases, redistributive preferences affect allocation through the same underlying mechanism: incentive constraints distort the relevant welfare weights, and policy responds to those distorted weights rather than to raw social preferences alone.
\end{remark}


\begin{corollary}\label{prop:cat-order-beta0}
Suppose that $\beta_i = 0$ at the corresponding equilibrium for every active category $i\in N$ and that all categories share the same type distribution $G$ on $\Theta$. Pick any two categories $i,j\in N$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
\item Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, $k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast$ if and only if $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)]>0$, without the first-order qualification of Proposition~\ref{prop:cat-order}.
\item If $\tilde\lambda_j>\tilde\lambda_i$ and $\lambda_j(\theta)/\lambda_i(\theta)$ is increasing on $\Theta$, then $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)]>0$ and hence $k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast$.
\item If $\beta_i = 0$ for all $i \in N$, then the revenue allocation satisfies $I_{i^\ast}^\ast = I$ and $I_i^\ast = 0$ for all $i \neq i^\ast$, where $i^\ast := \arg\min_i \tilde\lambda_i$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
\textit{Part (i).} By Proposition~\ref{prop:cat-order}, the local iff holds for any regime. When $\beta_i=0$, $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)=\Gamma_i(\theta)$ does not depend on $k$. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, $w_i(\theta,s)=1/(\alpha\varepsilon_i(s))$ is $\theta$-independent, so $\mathbb{E}^w_i[R_\mathcal{G}\mid s]=\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)]$ for every $s$, independently of $k$. Therefore \[\Delta\Pi_i(k) = \mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)]\cdot\Pi_i(k) \qquad \text{for all } k.\] Since $\Pi_i(k)>0$ for all $k$ in the interior, the sign of $\Delta\Pi_i(k)$ equals the sign of $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)]$ for all $k$, and the local iff of Proposition~\ref{prop:cat-order} holds globally.
\textit{Part (ii).} Since $\lambda_j(\theta)/\lambda_i(\theta)$ is increasing and $\tilde\lambda_j>\tilde\lambda_i$, Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder} gives $\Gamma_j(\theta)\ge\Gamma_i(\theta)$ for all $\theta$, with strict inequality on a set of positive measure. Hence $R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)\ge 0$ everywhere and strictly positive on a set of positive measure, so $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)]>0$ and the conclusion follows from part (i).
\textit{Part (iii).} The envelope theorem gives $\partial_{I_i}V_i(k_i,I_i) = -\tilde\lambda_i - \beta_i(k_i,I_i)$. When $\beta_i = 0$ for all $i$, this reduces to $-\tilde\lambda_i$. Since the welfare cost of raising one additional unit of revenue is strictly lowest in category $i^\ast$, any optimum assigns $I_{i^\ast}^\ast = I$ and $I_i^\ast = 0$ for all $i \neq i^\ast$. \qedhere
\end{proof}

The capacity ranking $k_j^\ast > k_i^\ast$ is governed by the sign of $\mathbb{E}_s[\varepsilon_i(s;k_i^\ast)\mathbb{E}^w_i[R_\mathcal{G}\mid s;k_i^\ast]\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}]$, a weighted average of the proportional difference in social weights $R_\mathcal{G}(\theta) = \Delta\Gamma_i(\theta)/\Gamma_i(\theta)$ across types, with the scarcity rent $\varepsilon_i(s)$ providing state-level weighting. This is a first-order characterization of the capacity ranking: it is exact when categories $i$ and $j$ differ marginally in redistributive preferences, and identifies the proportional shift in $\Gamma_i$ as the sufficient statistic for the direction of the ranking. For finite preference differences, part~(ii) provides a primitive sufficient condition that delivers the same conclusion without requiring the comparison to be marginal. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, $\mathbb{E}^w_i[R_\mathcal{G}\mid s]$ is independent of both $s$ and $k$, so the identity $\Delta\Pi_i(k) = \mathbb{E}^w_i[R_\mathcal{G}]\cdot\Pi_i(k)$ holds exactly for any finite preference difference, and the first-order characterization becomes an exact global iff. The sufficient condition in part~(ii) decomposes the ranking into two independent primitive conditions: the average weight ordering $\tilde\lambda_j > \tilde\lambda_i$ and the within-type monotone ratio $\lambda_j(\theta)/\lambda_i(\theta)$ increasing, which together imply $R_\mathcal{G}(\theta) \geq 0$ pointwise via Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}.

The proposition delivers two non-obvious policy lessons. First, what determines the capacity ranking is not how much more generous a regulator is toward one category in absolute terms, but how that generosity is distributed proportionally across types within the category. Two preference profiles with identical average weights can generate opposite capacity rankings depending on whether the preference increment is concentrated on high or low types. Second, the average weight ordering $\tilde\lambda_j > \tilde\lambda_i$ and the within-type profile $\lambda_j(\theta)/\lambda_i(\theta)$ increasing are genuinely independent conditions: a regulator who observes only average welfare weights across categories cannot determine the capacity ranking without also knowing how preferences vary within categories, and conversely. A regulator who ignores within-type preference heterogeneity and ranks capacity by average generosity alone will systematically misallocate capacity---assigning too little to categories where preferences are concentrated on high types, who generate the most revenue to finance investment, and too much to categories where preferences are flat or favor low types.

\begin{corollary}[Capacity ranking under a regime switch]\label{cor:regime-switch}
Suppose categories $1$ and $2$ share the same type distribution $G$ on $[\underline\theta,\overline\theta]$. If $\lambda_2(\theta)/\lambda_1(\theta)$ is increasing on $[\underline\theta,\overline\theta]$, $\beta_1>0$, $\beta_2=0$, and $\tilde\lambda_2>\tilde\lambda_1+\beta_1$, then $k_2^\ast>k_1^\ast$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Set $c_\Lambda:=\tilde\lambda_2/\tilde\lambda_1$ and $c_{\mathcal G}:=\tilde\lambda_2/(\tilde\lambda_1+\beta_1)$. Since $\beta_1\ge 0$, we have $c_{\mathcal G}\le c_\Lambda$. Since $\tilde\lambda_2>\tilde\lambda_1+\beta_1$, it follows that $c_{\mathcal G}>1$. Because $\lambda_2(\theta)/\lambda_1(\theta)$ is increasing, Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder} implies that $\theta\mapsto \Lambda_2(\theta)/\Lambda_1(\theta)$ is increasing. Evaluating at $\theta=\underline\theta$ yields $\Lambda_2(\underline\theta)/\Lambda_1(\underline\theta)=\tilde\lambda_2/\tilde\lambda_1=c_\Lambda$, so for every $\theta$,
\[
\Lambda_2(\theta)\ge c_\Lambda\Lambda_1(\theta)\ge c_{\mathcal G}\Lambda_1(\theta).
\]
Using $\beta_2=0$ and $c_{\mathcal G}(\tilde\lambda_1+\beta_1)=\tilde\lambda_2$ by definition of $c_{\mathcal G}$,
\[
\mathcal G_2(\theta)=J(\theta)\tilde\lambda_2+\Lambda_2(\theta)=c_{\mathcal G}J(\theta)(\tilde\lambda_1+\beta_1)+\Lambda_2(\theta)\ge c_{\mathcal G}\mathcal G_1(\theta).
\]
Since $c_{\mathcal G}>1$ and $\mathcal G_1(\theta)>0$ for all $\theta$ by Assumption~\ref{ass:interior}, we have $\mathcal G_2(\theta)>\mathcal G_1(\theta)$ for all $\theta$, hence $R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)=(\mathcal G_2(\theta)-\mathcal G_1(\theta))/\mathcal G_1(\theta)>0$ pointwise. Therefore $\mathbb{E}^w_1[R_{\mathcal G}]>0$ and Proposition~\ref{prop:cat-order-beta0} yields $k_2^\ast>k_1^\ast$. \qedhere
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Lagrangian and revenue allocation.}
With interior capacity solutions, the stationarity conditions reduce to equalizing scarcity rents $\Pi_1(k_1^\ast) = \Pi_2(k_2^\ast)$ on the capacity dimension. On the revenue dimension, the effective marginal cost of revenue differs across categories: it equals $\tilde\lambda_1$ for category 1 (IR-unconstrained) and $\tilde\lambda_2 + \beta_2$ for category 2 (IR-constrained). Note that by complementary slackness, $\beta_2 > 0$ requires the IR/budget constraint of category 2 to bind, which is only consistent with $I_2 > 0$. Hence in the mixed regime the revenue allocation admits only two cases:
\begin{itemize}
\item Case A ($I_1 = 0$, $I_2 = I$): holds when $\tilde\lambda_1 \geq \tilde\lambda_2 + \beta_2$;
\item Case C ($I_1, I_2 > 0$): knife-edge, requires $\tilde\lambda_1 = \tilde\lambda_2 + \beta_2$ exactly.
\end{itemize}
The binding IR constraint on category 2 acts as a wedge that raises the effective cost of revenue extraction from category 2 above its primitive welfare weight.

\paragraph{Comparative statics on the knife-edge.}
The knife-edge condition $\tilde\lambda_1 = \tilde\lambda_2 + \beta_2(k_2,I_2)$ defines a locus in $(k_2,I_2)$ space. Its shape is governed by the two partial derivatives established in the within-category analysis:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\partial_{I_2}\beta_2 = 1/\mathcal{V}_2(k_2) > 0$ always: as the revenue burden on category 2 rises, $\beta_2$ rises monotonically, so the mixed regime is self-consistent only for sufficiently low values of $I_2$ such that $\tilde\lambda_1 \geq \tilde\lambda_2 + \beta_2$ remains satisfied.
\item $\partial_{k_2}\beta_2 = -\mathcal{S}_2(k_2)/\mathcal{V}_2(k_2)$: if $\mathcal{S}_2 < 0$, expanding capacity in category 2 tightens its IR constraint and raises the effective cost of revenue; if $\mathcal{S}_2 > 0$, the reverse holds.
\end{itemize}
The sign of $\mathcal{S}_2$ --- the sufficient statistic governing welfare effects and implementation regions within category 2 --- therefore also governs the direction in which capacity expansion in category 2 shifts the effective cost of revenue across categories.

\paragraph{Revenue allocation under single-crossing of $\mathcal{S}_2$.}
Fix $I_2^\ast = I$ and let $k_2^\ast$ vary over $(0,k)$ with $k_1^\ast = k - k_2^\ast$. Under the case where $R_\theta$ is increasing and $R_q$ is log-submodular, $\mathcal{S}_2(k_2)$ is single-crossing from $+$ to $-$, so $\partial_{k_2}\beta_2 = -\mathcal{S}_2/\mathcal{V}_2$ changes sign from $-$ to $+$ at $k^\ast$. Hence $\beta_2$ is single-dipped in $k_2$, attaining its unique minimum at $k^\ast$, and the effective marginal cost of revenue $\tilde\lambda_2 + \beta_2(k_2)$ is also single-dipped.

The mixed regime $\beta_1 = 0$, $\beta_2 > 0$ is therefore most naturally self-consistent for intermediate values of $k_2$ around $k^\ast$, where $\beta_2$ is at its minimum and the IR constraint of category 2 is only mildly binding. The condition $\tilde\lambda_1 \geq \tilde\lambda_2 + \beta_2(k_2)$ required for Case A is most easily satisfied in this intermediate range. At extreme values of $k_2$ --- either very low or very high --- $\beta_2$ is large, the IR constraint of category 2 is severely binding, and maintaining $\beta_1 = 0$ simultaneously becomes harder as revenue pressure on the whole system rises. The mixed regime is therefore a description of an intermediate capacity range, with the fully IR-constrained regime $\beta_1 > 0$, $\beta_2 > 0$ being the more natural description at the extremes.


\begin{lemma}[Revenue allocation across categories]\label{lem:revenue-alloc}
Suppose $N=\{1,2\}$, $k_1,k_2>0$, and $\tilde\lambda_1<\tilde\lambda_2$. There exists a unique threshold $\tilde I \in (0,\infty)$ defined by
\[
\tilde\lambda_1 + \beta_1(k_1,\tilde I) = \tilde\lambda_2,
\]
such that:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
\item For $I \in [0,\tilde I]$: $I_1^\ast = I$ and $I_2^\ast = 0$.
\item For $I \in (\tilde I,\infty)$: $I_1^\ast > 0$ and $I_2^\ast > 0$, uniquely determined by
\[
\tilde\lambda_1 + \beta_1(k_1,I_1^\ast) = \tilde\lambda_2 + \beta_2(k_2,I_2^\ast), \qquad I_1^\ast + I_2^\ast = I.
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\textit{Part (i).} Since $\tilde\lambda_1 < \tilde\lambda_2$ and $\partial_{I_i}V_i = -\tilde\lambda_i - \beta_i$, the marginal welfare cost of revenue is strictly lower in category 1 than in category 2 whenever $\beta_1 \leq \tilde\lambda_2 - \tilde\lambda_1$. The KKT condition for $I_2^\ast = 0$ is $\xi_2 = \tilde\lambda_2 - (\tilde\lambda_1 + \beta_1(k_1,I)) \geq 0$, which holds as long as $\tilde\lambda_1 + \beta_1(k_1,I) \leq \tilde\lambda_2$. Since $\partial_I \beta_1(k_1,I) = 1/\mathcal{V}_1(k_1) > 0$, the effective cost $\tilde\lambda_1 + \beta_1(k_1,I)$ is strictly increasing in $I$ and crosses $\tilde\lambda_2$ at a unique threshold $\tilde I$, defining the corner regime. Note that $\beta_2 = 0$ throughout this region by complementary slackness since $I_2^\ast = 0$.

\textit{Part (ii).} For $I > \tilde I$, maintaining $I_2^\ast = 0$ would require $\xi_2 = \tilde\lambda_2 - (\tilde\lambda_1 + \beta_1(k_1,I)) < 0$, violating dual feasibility. Hence $I_2^\ast > 0$, which by complementary slackness forces $\beta_2 > 0$, and the interior KKT condition $\tilde\lambda_1 + \beta_1(k_1,I_1^\ast) = \tilde\lambda_2 + \beta_2(k_2,I_2^\ast)$ must hold. Existence and uniqueness of the interior solution follow from the intermediate value theorem: the left-hand side is strictly increasing in $I_1$ from $\tilde\lambda_1$ at $I_1=0$ to $\tilde\lambda_1+\beta_1(k_1,I)>\tilde\lambda_2$ at $I_1=I$, while the right-hand side is strictly decreasing in $I_1$ from $\tilde\lambda_2+\beta_2(k_2,I)$ at $I_1=0$ to $\tilde\lambda_2$ at $I_1=I$. Since LHS $<$ RHS at $I_1=0$ and LHS $>$ RHS at $I_1=I$, a unique crossing exists in $(0,I)$. \qedhere
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}[Equal average weights]
When all categories share the same average welfare weight $\tilde\lambda_1 = \tilde\lambda_2 = \dots = \tilde\lambda_n$, the threshold $\tilde I$ collapses to zero and the revenue allocation is interior for any $I > 0$. The equalization condition reduces to $\beta_1(k_1,I_1) = \beta_2(k_2,I_2) = \dots = \beta_n(k_n,I_n)$, so the revenue allocation is determined entirely by the endogenous $\beta_i$ schedules. If $\mathcal{S}_i > 0$ for all categories at the optimum, then $\partial_{k_i}\beta_i < 0$: categories with higher capacity have lower $\beta_i$ at equal revenue, and the equalization condition requires them to bear a larger revenue share. 
\end{remark}


\begin{remark}[Interpretation of the local ranking condition]
Consider an ordered collection of categories such that each adjacent pair \((i,i+1)\) differs only by a marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences. Then part (i) can be read as a local policy rule along the category ordering:
\[
k_{i+1}^\ast > k_i^\ast
\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i\!\left(s;k_i^\ast\right)\,
\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s;k_i^\ast\right]\,
\mathbf{1}_{\left\{s\in T_i\left(k_i^\ast\right)\right\}}
\right] > 0.
\]

The term ``local'' refers to preferences, not to capacity: the result is exact when moving from one category to the next along a sequence of marginal preference perturbations, and it is evaluated at the baseline optimum \(k_i^\ast\). Economically, the condition tells the policymaker whether the optimal capacity schedule should move upward or downward when passing from category \(i\) to category \(i+1\).

The statistic has a natural interpretation. The component \(\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s;k_i^\ast\right]\) measures the proportional reweighting of effective social priorities within category \(i\), while \(\varepsilon_i\!\left(s;k_i^\ast\right)\) weights this reorientation by the marginal value of relaxing capacity in state \(s\). Hence the ranking depends on whether the preference perturbation raises the social value of scarce capacity in binding states.

Under the adjacent-perturbation interpretation, the condition can be applied pairwise along the ordered set of categories and therefore characterizes the ordinal shape of the final optimal policy. In particular, if the expression above is positive for every adjacent pair, then
\[
k_1^\ast < k_2^\ast < \cdots < k_n^\ast.
\]
If it is negative for every adjacent pair, the ordering is reversed. More generally, the sign pattern across adjacent categories determines whether the optimal capacity schedule is increasing, decreasing, or single-peaked.

What this condition does not provide is a closed-form formula for the levels \(k_i^\ast\) or for the distance between categories. Its content is ordinal rather than cardinal: it tells the policymaker who should receive more capacity than whom in the final optimum.
\end{remark}

\begin{itemize}
\item $\gamma_i(\theta) = (1-G_i(\theta))/g_i(\theta)$: the marginal leakage rate at type $\theta$ — allocating one more unit to $\theta$ forces the designer to surrender rent to all types above, and $\gamma_i(\theta)$ measures the local cost of this upward spillover.
\item $J_i(\theta) = \theta - \gamma_i(\theta)$: the virtual surplus of type $\theta$ — the net revenue the designer extracts after accounting for the marginal leakage to higher types. Negative when the leakage cost exceeds the type's own surplus, which arises endogenously when the lower tail of the distribution is heavy and redistribution forces the designer to serve low types.
\item $\Lambda_i(\theta) = \gamma_i(\theta)\,\mathbb{E}[\lambda_i(\tilde\theta)\mid\tilde\theta\ge\theta]$: the socially-weighted marginal leakage — the social value of the rent that flows upward to all types above $\theta$ as a consequence of serving $\theta$. It is large when the designer places high weight on higher types and when the leakage rate $\gamma_i(\theta)$ is large.
\item $\Gamma_i(\theta) = \tilde\lambda_i J_i(\theta) + \Lambda_i(\theta)$: the total social return from allocating to type $\theta$, combining the revenue channel ($\tilde\lambda_i J_i(\theta)$) and the rent channel ($\Lambda_i(\theta)$). Positive even when $J_i(\theta)<0$ if the social value of upward rent flows is sufficiently large.
\item $\beta_i\ge 0$: the shadow cost of the budget constraint — it measures the marginal value of relaxing the revenue requirement by one unit. When positive, it amplifies the attractiveness of revenue-generating types ($J_i>0$) and further discounts revenue-costly types ($J_i<0$).
\item $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) = \Gamma_i(\theta) + J_i(\theta)\beta_i$: the effective social weight on type $\theta$ at the optimum, combining the redistributive objective and the revenue constraint. It collapses to $\Gamma_i(\theta)$ when the budget constraint is slack.
\end{itemize}


\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Case 1: $I_i = 0$, $\beta_i = 0$.} The budget constraint is slack and no revenue is raised from category $i$. Complementary slackness gives $\xi_i \geq 0$, and the outer KKT stationarity condition gives $\tilde\lambda_i = -\mu_I + \xi_i \geq -\mu_I$. The effective weight collapses to $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) = \Gamma_i(\theta)$.
\item \textbf{Case 2: $I_i > 0$, $\beta_i = 0$.} The budget constraint is slack despite positive revenue extraction. Complementary slackness forces $\xi_i = 0$, and the stationarity condition pins down $\tilde\lambda_i = -\mu_I$ exactly. This is the knife-edge marginal category: it bears revenue but its budget constraint does not bind. Generically at most one category satisfies this condition. The effective weight is again $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) = \Gamma_i(\theta)$.
\item \textbf{Case 3: $I_i > 0$, $\beta_i > 0$.} The budget constraint binds. Complementary slackness forces $\xi_i = 0$, and the stationarity condition gives $\tilde\lambda_i + \beta_i = -\mu_I$, so $\beta_i = -\mu_I - \tilde\lambda_i > 0$, which requires $\tilde\lambda_i < -\mu_I$. The effective weight is $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) = \Gamma_i(\theta) + J_i(\theta)\beta_i$, reflecting the additional revenue pressure on the allocation.
\end{itemize}






Proposition~\ref{prop:Elasticity} provides conditions under which a change of preferences unambiguously impacts the trade-off channel. Since $R_q$ depends on the preferences only through $q_i$, and as $R_q(\theta)>0$, the effect of the behavioral change is equivalent to the effect on the optimal allocation of a change in preferences. However, the behavioral channel is not monotonic in $\theta$  so the aggregate effect of a change of preference on the equilibrium cannot be signed in general: in the IR-unconstrained region, Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation} formally establishes that $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s) $is non-monotone; in the IR-constrained region, no analogous characterization is available without conditions on the equilibrium. Corollary~\ref{cor:CARA} provides a clean bypass of this limitation under the Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, where $R_q(\theta)$ is constant, and the total effect reduces to the trade-off channel alone.


\begin{proposition}[Elasticity condition]\label{prop:Elasticity} Assume that $R_\theta(\theta)$ and $R_q(\theta)$ are monotone, that $R_{\G}(\theta)$ has a constant sign, and that, for all $\theta$ such that $R_{\G}(\theta)R_\theta(\theta)\neq 0$,\footnote{At isolated zeros of $R_\theta(\theta)$, the cross-term $-R_\theta(\theta)R_{\G}'(\theta)$ vanishes and the sign of $\Delta R_\theta'(\theta)$ is determined by $-R_{\G}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)$ directly. At isolated zeros of $R_{\G}(\theta)$, the constant-sign hypothesis forces $R_{\G}'(\theta)=0$ by differentiability, so the cross-term vanishes there as well. Monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ is preserved in both cases.} \[ \left|\frac{R_{\G}'(\theta)}{R_{\G}(\theta)}\right| \le \left|\frac{R_\theta'(\theta)}{R_\theta(\theta)}\right|. \] Then $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ is monotone and \[ \mathrm{sign}\!\left(\Cov_{\theta_i}\!\left(\Delta R_\theta(\theta),R_q(\theta)\right)\right) = \mathrm{sign}\!\left(-R_{\G}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)\right)\cdot \mathrm{sign}\!\left(R_q'(\theta)\right). \] If the elasticity condition fails, monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ can no longer be inferred from primitives alone. \end{proposition}

The proposition provides sufficient conditions under which a change in redistributive preferences unambiguously affects the trade-off covariance term, and conversely that a same direction of a change of preference can have opposite effect on the equilibrium. The elasticity condition is a uniform sufficient condition across all achievable sign configurations. It compares two elasticities: (i) $\left|\frac{R_{\G}'(\theta)}{R_{\G}(\theta)}\right|$, which measures how sharply the preference change varies across types, and (ii) $\left|\frac{R_\theta'(\theta)}{R_\theta(\theta)}\right|$, which measures how sharply the existing sorting pattern varies across types. The condition requires that the preference change be smoother in proportional terms than the existing sorting pattern. The sorting condition follows from differentiating $R_\theta(\theta)$:
%##########################################
\[\Delta\!\left(R_\theta'(\theta)\right) = -R_\theta(\theta)\,R_{\G}'(\theta)-R_{\G}(\theta)\,R_\theta'(\theta).\]
%##########################################
The proposition considers all possible case given any sign configuration of $(R_{\G}(\theta), R_\theta'(\theta), R_q'(\theta))$. To interpret its economic meaning, consider the case where lower types are allocated more capacity ($R_q'(\theta)\le 0$), higher types gets higher rents ($R'_\theta(\theta)\ge 0$), the change of preferences is towards higher types ($\Delta R_{\G}\ge 0$), and we are looking to reduce the adverse sorting effect such that the covariance term is positive ($\Delta R_\theta'(\theta) \le 0 $). Then a change of preference ---even in the correct direction---can exacerbate adverse sorting by creating local distortions. More specifically, the sufficient elasticity condition binds in two regions: (a) types contributing negatively to the trade-off ($R_\theta(\theta)<0$) but receiving increasing proportional weights ($R_{\G}'(\theta)\ge 0$), and (b) types contributing positively to the trade-off ($R_\theta(\theta)>0$) but receiving decreasing proportional weights ($R_{\G}'(\theta)\le 0$). In both regions, preference changes that are too concentrated reverse the sign of $\Delta(R_\theta'(\theta))$; the elasticity bound ensures changes remain sufficiently gradual. 

We illustrate the implications of the proposition in Figure \ref{fig:variation_uniform_preference_tradeoff}: even when the direction of a preference shift favors the same consumers, particular concentration of social weights adjustments can exacerbate adverse sorting, worsening rather than improving the trade-off effect. The first plot represents two similar perturbations: from a utilitarian market designer where $\lambda_i(\theta) =1$ to a weight function that favors higher types (in both cases). The middle set of plots shows that the perturbation in the case A leads to a non monotonic change in $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ with respect to $\theta$, compared to case B, which is strictly decreasing (we assume that $R_q$ is decreasing in $\theta$). In case A, this increasing part of the function leads to a negative covariance. The right panel shows that the increasing part is due to the violation of the sufficient conditions in the Proposition.
