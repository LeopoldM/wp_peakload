\begin{abstract}
\noindent This paper examines the challenges of allocating a good subject to capacity constraints such as electricity when considering consumer preferences and investment decisions. A theoretical framework is developed where a market designer sequentially chooses a level of investment and proposes an allocation mechanism to consumers followed by a consumption stage. The market designer uses the allocation to maximize consumer surplus and finance the investment cost. He faces heterogeneous consumers who have private information about their demand level and belong to a publicly observed category, allowing the market designer to distinguish groups of consumers such as households or industries. We show that the optimal allocation implies discriminating against consumers based on their types and categories and that the relative discrimination depends on the level of investment considered. It has significant welfare and distributive implications: an optimal pricing mechanism can minimize the investment cost and lead to a higher aggregate consumer surplus. However, it is not always a Pareto improvement for every consumer, especially for smaller ones. We describe two main environments: the current second-best situation, in which the market designer cannot obtain information about consumers and must choose fixed prices ex-ante, and the optimal theoretical second-best allocation mechanism that considers the incentive and individual rationality constraints and the investment decisions.

\section{Introduction}\label{chap3sec:sec1}
When demand is uncertain and capacity is limited, the standard framework of peak-load pricing answers two interrelated questions: who should receive the scarce good, and who should finance the capacity needed to relieve that scarcity. These questions are especially important for essential goods (in particular electricity, network industries and health services), because inadequate rationing and capacity shortages in these markets impose costs that extend far beyond the consumers directly rationed.\footnote{Misallocation under scarcity can be extremely costly. In electricity markets, \citeP{Hinchberger2024} find a deadweight loss of about \$2.2 billion annually from flat retail pricing over several US markets, while the 2021 Texas blackout caused \$80--130 billion in economic losses \citep{GoldingKumarMertens2021,Busby2021}; the 2025 blackout in Spain reduced household consumption by 15\% in one day, with a quarterly GDP effect of less than \texteuro 400 million \citep{CaixaBank2025}. The 2025 \textit{Urban Mobility Report} reports annual congestion costs of \$269 billion in U.S. urban areas \citep{Schrank2025}, while \cite{Kreindler2024} finds a 23\% deadweight loss from unpriced peak-hour congestion in Bangalore. In vaccine allocation, \cite{Moore2022} showed that vaccine misallocation during COVID-19 led to about 1.3 million direct deaths worldwide by the end of 2021.} The issue is all the more relevant because the same sectors in which short-run scarcity is costly are also those in which relaxing that scarcity requires large and costly investment in new capacity, making the allocation of scarce supply and the financing of expansion part of the same economic problem.\footnote{In electricity generation, the IEA projects almost 4{,}600 GW of additional renewable power capacity worldwide between 2025 and 2030 \citep{IEA2025b}; financing that expansion raises the classic question of whether capacity should be supported primarily through market revenues, such as spot prices and long-term contracts, or through broader public support \citep{borenstein2005,IEA2025a}. For electricity grids, the European Commission estimates investment needs of about \texteuro 730 billion for distribution and \texteuro 477 billion for transmission by 2040; here the issue is typically how regulated network tariffs should recover expansion costs while providing efficient signals for grid use \citep{EuropeanCommission2025,ACER2025}. In urban transport, the OECD estimates average investment needs of about USD 400 per capita per year in high-income cities between 2015 and 2050 \citep{Wagner2018}; because road and public-transport capacity are both substitutes and complements, the financing problem involves the joint design of congestion charges or tolls, taxes, and public-transport fares \citep{ITF2023,ITF2024}.} But these are also precisely the sectors in which distributional concerns are strongest. When capacity is scarce, the allocation rule determines who is served; when capacity is expanded, the financing rule determines who bears the burden of paying for it. This creates a fundamental normative tension. The classical logic of peak-load pricing points toward allocating scarce capacity to the users with the highest marginal willingness to pay and financing new investment through prices that reflect scarcity. For many essential goods, however, policymakers are reluctant to let either access during shortages or the burden of financing expansion be determined solely by willingness to pay.\footnote{Redistributive intervention in practice takes several forms. During the European energy crisis, governments relied on household energy price caps, subsidies, and alternative support schemes to protect consumers from large price increases \citep{Fetzer2024,Amores2025}. In pharmaceuticals, access is also shaped by direct price regulation, including ceiling prices for essential medicines \citep{DPCO2013,WHO2020}. In network industries, redistribution often operates through tariff design: increasing-block and other nonlinear tariffs have frequently been justified as a way to shift part of the revenue burden toward higher-usage consumers \citep{Borenstein2012,Levinson2022,Smith2022,Randriamaro2026}. In transport, the distributional consequences of congestion pricing depend not only on who pays the toll, but also on how the revenues are recycled via direct transfers \citep{Eliasson2006}. In health emergencies, redistribution can also take the form of non-price access rules, such as priority service, reserve categories, or other rationing schemes for scarce vaccines and treatments; these systems typically rely on criteria such as vulnerability, exposure risk, or essential social roles, which cannot be reduced to a single price or priority score \citep{Pathak2024,Wang2023,akbarpour2023economic}.} As a result, the objectives of efficiency, cost recovery, and redistribution need not align: the consumers who should receive the good under an efficient scarcity allocation are not necessarily those whom a redistributive regulator would want to prioritize, and the consumers from whom it is easiest to recover investment costs are not necessarily those whom society wants to make pay.

This paper asks how a market designer should jointly design the optimal allocation mechanism, recover investment costs, and choose the level of scarcity when it has redistributive preferences but cannot observe perfectly the social value of the consumers. In standard peak-load pricing, the investment problem is governed by a comparison between the marginal utility brought from an additional unit of capacity and its marginal cost. Here, the same logic survives, but the content of the marginal value changes. Under private information, an expansion of capacity depends on which consumers receive the additional quantity under the constrained mechanism, how that reallocation interacts with incentive constraints, and how the market design can raise revenue. I consider an environment in which consumers belong to observable categories, differ in privately known marginal utility within each category, and face a common state that makes capacity bind only in high-demand states. The market designer therefore acts along two dimensions at once: within each category, it chooses the allocation mechanism; across categories, it chooses how total capacity and the revenue requirement are distributed. By conditioning on observable categories before screening within them, the designer can target scarcity and raising revenue more efficiently, which helps mitigate the efficiency losses created by private information. As a result, both a change in capacity and a change in redistributive preferences operate through the same object: they reshape the constrained allocation within categories and, through it, the social value of relaxing scarcity. The paper also delivers primitive sufficient conditions and tractable special cases under which these channels admit sharp and interpretable comparative-statics predictions.

The core tension comes from the interaction between incentive constraints, redistributive preferences, and capacity scarcity. Incentive compatibility requires the mechanism to leave rents to higher types, but once the regulator assigns different social values to consumption and transfers across types, those rents are no longer welfare-neutral. Moreover, because the capacity constraint binds only for some states and types, the optimal allocation under the mechanism is endogenous to the investment level, so expanding capacity reshapes the rent structure itself and loops back into the revenue the mechanism can raise to finance that same investment. This interaction generates two endogenous sorting margins that organize the results. When capacity expands, the additional quantity is not distributed uniformly across types but it is reallocated toward types whose demand is most responsive, as determined by demand curvature. Furthermore, its revenue consequence depends on whether serving those types is socially costly or beneficial once the informational rents left to higher types are taken into account. This second component is captured by the ratio between virtual surplus and effective social weight of a given consumer type.\footnote{A consumer’s welfare weight and effective social weight play distinct roles. The welfare weight measures the value of an additional unit of surplus in the market designer’s objective. The effective social weight instead measures the incidence of allocating one additional unit to that consumer, accounting for both the informational rents induced for higher types and the effect on the revenue available to the mechanism.} The correlation between the two forms the \textit{capacity-sorting margin}. The second governs how redistributive preferences reshape the constrained allocation. The key object is the proportional change in effective social weights as it captures how the preference perturbation interacts with the mechanism’s existing sorting of consumption across types. This is the \textit{preference-sorting margin}. These two margins first characterize how the mechanism responds within a category to changes in scarcity and redistributive preferences, and they later determine how capacity and revenue should be allocated across categories.

Conditional on a capacity and a budget, the paper first characterizes the optimal within-category mechanism and then studies how the constrained optimum responds to changes in capacity and redistributive preferences. The key question is whether a capacity expansion relaxes or tightens the designer’s revenue. Under redistributive preferences, the types to whom the designer would like to allocate additional quantity are not necessarily those for whom an expansion is most favorable to the category’s net revenue: serving them may be socially desirable, but it can also increase the informational rents left to higher types. The effect of a capacity expansion is therefore governed by the market designer’s marginal revenue, summarized by the \textit{capacity-sorting margin}. As a result, the welfare incidence of a capacity expansion need not be uniformly progressive or regressive, but depends on the initial investment level. A similar logic governs redistributive perturbations. A change in preferences does not translate mechanically into a monotone shift in quantities and individual surplus. What matters is how a perturbation changes the effective social weights together with the constrained allocation itself, since both objects move jointly under the mechanism. This is captured by the \textit{preference-sorting margin}. I show that the interaction between this margin and the capacity constraint need not be monotonic in types, which generate sets of winners and losers that need not align with the primitive direction of the preference change itself: a tilt in favor of lower or higher types does not mechanically imply a monotone reallocation toward those same types.     

Once the within-category mechanism is characterized, the designer’s problem becomes one of allocating capacity and revenue across categories, along three distinct margins: the capacity ranking, the extensive revenue margin that separates contributing from non-contributing categories, and the intensive revenue margin that ranks revenue requirements across contributing categories. Relative to the inner problem, this outer allocation is more tractable: the distortions created by incentive compatibility and redistribution can be compressed into a small set of category-level objects that aggregate the previous within-category margins. But the resulting rankings are not trivial, and they do not rely on the same primitives. The capacity ranking depends on the preference-sorting margin: categories receive more capacity when redistributive preferences place proportionally more weight on the types for whom scarcity relief is most valuable under the constrained mechanism. Capacity is therefore not necessarily ranked by average welfare weights or by the absolute level of effective social weights. By contrast, the extensive revenue margin, depends only on its average welfare weight, which is sufficient to separate contributing from non-contributing categories. The intensive revenue margin is however different. Conditional on contribution, the ranking depends not only on average weights, but also on the ability of a category to raise revenue given the distribution of capacity and redistributive preferences, which is captured by the interaction between the capacity-sorting and preference-sorting margins.

The final result returns to the canonical logic of peak-load pricing by characterizing the optimal aggregate investment level and the associated welfare under redistributive preferences. The optimality condition equates the marginal investment cost with the incentive-adjusted social value of marginal consumption, aggregated across contributing categories and normalized by the equilibrium marginal cost of funds. This yields a sharp contrast to the utilitarian benchmark case where informational rents are pure transfers in the social objective. Therefore, the complete-information and incomplete-information investment rules coincide at the optimum. Under redistributive preferences, by contrast, informational rents are no longer welfare-neutral, so the informational wedge remains even at the optimum and directly shapes both investment and welfare. The aggregate investment problem  synthesizes the previous within- and across-category analyses, because the value of expanding total capacity depends on how additional capacity is transmitted within categories and on how it is allocated across them. It also yields two comparative results that follow from the same mechanisms. First, redistributive preference changes affect the optimal investment level through two channels: they alter the average marginal value of capacity across categories and they shift the equilibrium marginal cost of funds. Second, the welfare incidence of an aggregate expansion is not mechanical: whether it is progressive or regressive depends on the initial cross-category allocation of capacity, since additional capacity is transmitted through the same within-category and across-category sorting margins.

\textbf{Related Literature}

We build the framework on several strands of literature. The dynamic interaction between investment decisions and the consumption stages stems from the \textit{peak-load pricing theory} that originated from \cite{m1949tarification}. It describes how capacity constraints interact with the provisions of a homogeneous good with time-varying uncertain stochastic demand.  It has mainly been used in recent work to study the role of market power, as in \cite{leautier2016visible}, where producers can increase the price on the spot market beyond marginal cost even though they are not capacity-constrained. The effect of price regulation is also analyzed in \cite{leautier2018long}, where the author demonstrates that short-term inefficiencies can sometimes have long-term and counterintuitive effects. In this paper, the price cap changes the private incentives producers face, hence the final investment decisions. \cite{holmberg2020optimal} study the effect of having inefficient rationing. Consequently, electricity prices do not internalize this additional cost, and the market designer needs to implement an additional stream of revenue for the producers. This work introduces two features in the model: (i) heterogeneous consumers with private information and (ii) inefficiencies due to the schedule commitment by the market designer before the uncertainty is resolved.\footnote{This work mirrors the literature from congestion pricing theory from \cite{vickrey1963pricing,vickrey1969congestion}. For a recent theoretical paper, see, for instance, \cite{DEPALMA2017106}, which also compares different allocation mechanisms but without considering the demand-side.}

This paper is also based on a second stream of papers that is related to the electricity markets and is based on the seminal paper by \cite{chao1987priority} on priority service. The central idea is to provide a mechanism design solution in the form of a contractual arrangement where consumers choose the allocation during the wholesale market at the same time. It is in the same vein as the allocation schedule of this paper and the probability of being disconnected when demand exceeds the level of capacity. This framework has been refined by a series of papers by the same authors, including the comparison with other market arrangements \citep{chao2022priority} and the role of risk aversion \citep{chao2012competitive}. We also relate to a series of papers focusing on implementing the second-best pricing method for consumers with incomplete information in \cite{spulber1992capacity,spulber1992optimal,spulber1993monopoly}. The work in \cite{spulber1992optimal} focuses on an incomplete information framework without endogenous investment decisions. The optimal allocation schedule is non-linear because consumers' type is private information. Therefore, the market designer faces some challenges when implementing such schedules. In \cite{spulber1992capacity}, a regulated firm is introduced to consider its revenue constraint. However, the focus of this paper remains circumscribed to the design of consumers' second-best tariffs. Finally, \cite{spulber1993monopoly} studies the case of a monopoly designing the rates under incomplete information. We depart from this literature by deepening the private incentives consumers might have by behaving strategically from the truthful reporting and by tightening the link with the investment decisions framework developed in the previous paragraph.

Most of the recent empirical works on redistribution study the short-term effect of pricing issues without considering the long-term interactions with the level of investment. This issue has been recently studied in several empirical papers in the context of essential goods. For instance, in electricity markets, \cite{cahana2022distributional} explore the redistributive effects of switching from a flat electricity price to real-time pricing. Depending on the design, low-income households may lose due to specific consumption patterns in the face of available supply.\footnote{\cite{levinson2022electric} have studied the rates implemented by utilities in the U.S. and how they take into account redistribution preferences in their design. Due to the rapid increase in residential rooftop solar photovoltaic, electricity network tariffs have also been studied, notably in the Californian markets. If the tariffs are mostly based on variable parts, then non-adopters tend to cross-subsidies adopters of such technologies. One central issue is that the latter are mostly high-income households \citep{burger2020quantifying}.} Concerning medical goods, the scarcity of vaccines creates a trade-off between protecting the most vulnerable (e.g., elderly), the likely spreader (e.g., students), or the individual bringing the highest economic benefits (e.g., front-line health workers).
Finally, in the case of congestion pricing, \cite{hall2021can} studies the pricing of a lane portion. Using survey and travel time data, the author finds that a fully efficient toll is unnecessary for sufficient welfare and Pareto improvement. 

From a theoretical perspective, the issues related to distributive concerns are borrowed from a growing body of literature using mechanism design.
In particular, \cite{akbarpour2023economic} and \cite{akbarpour2023redistributive} provide a framework with consumers' characteristics, such as the private information and the publicly-observed categories, in line with the current paper. They study the trade-off between allocating certain vaccines on a free but random basis or using prices to discriminate and extract information from consumers. The authors assume that the market designer has distributive and exogenous revenue preferences. Therefore, the model exhibits a tension of allocating the good via prices, which generates some revenue, or via a random free allocation that minimizes distributive issues. This paper endogenizes the revenue preference by implementing investment decisions with a revenue constraint. We also provide results when the market designer can imperfectly implement prices. Finally, a recent paper by \cite{crampespricing} studies the implementation of an optimal Pareto income tax schedule \textit{à la Mirrlees} when considering the interaction between consuming energy services (heating, air conditioning, light) and investing in energy efficiency with incomplete information about consumer utility. This paper has a similar spirit, but the link between consumption and production capacity fundamentally differs.



\section{Environment and Problem Formulation}\label{chap3sec:sec2}

\subsection{Environment}

\paragraph{Consumers.}
There is a unit mass of consumers. Each consumer is characterized by a pair $(i,\theta)$ and is subject to a common shock $s$ unrelated to type. The index $i \in N = \{1,\dots,n\}$ denotes the consumer category (e.g., households or industrial users). Categories are publicly observed, and category $i$ has mass $\mu_i > 0$ with $\sum_i\mu_i=1$. Conditional on category $i$, each consumer has a privately observed type $\theta \in \Theta_i = [\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$, drawn from a distribution $G_i$ with density $g_i > 0$ on $\Theta_i$. Types are independent across consumers and are known to the consumer but not to the market designer. All consumers are subject to a common demand shock $s \in S = [\underline{s},\overline{s}]$, drawn from a distribution $F$ with density $f > 0$. The realization of $s$ is observed by all agents.

\paragraph{Preferences.}
A consumer of type $\theta$ derives value from consuming quantity $q$ in state $s$ given by
\[
\theta \, U(q,s) = \theta \int_0^q u(\tilde q,s)\, d\tilde q,
\]
with $U(0,s)=0$. The marginal willingness to pay $u(q,s)$ satisfies: $u_s > 0$, $u_q < 0$. Thus, marginal utility is higher in high-demand states, and it decreases with quantity. Transfers are separable, and there are no income effects. A consumer receiving allocation $(q_i(\theta,s), t_i(\theta,s))$ obtains expected surplus
\[
\E_s\!\left[\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\right].
\]
Participation requires that this surplus is nonnegative for all types:
\begin{equation}
\E_s\!\left[\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\right] \ge 0,
\qquad \forall \theta \in \Theta_i,
\tag{IR}\label{IR-def}
\end{equation}
where $\E_s[\cdot]$ denotes expectation with respect to the distribution of states $s$.

\paragraph{Technology and feasibility.}
The market designer chooses the allocation of the good subject to capacity constraints. Let $q_i(\theta,s)$ denote the quantity allocated to a consumer of type $\theta$ in category $i$ at state $s$. The total quantity allocated in state $s$ is
\[
Q(s) = \sum_{i \in N} \mu_i \E_{i}[q_i(\theta,s)],
\]
where $\E_i[\cdot]$ denotes expectation with respect to the distribution of types $\theta$. Capacity is determined by an investment level $k \ge 0$, with cost $I(k)$, where $I$ is increasing and convex. Production has zero marginal cost. Feasibility requires that total allocation does not exceed capacity in any state:
\begin{equation}
Q(s) \le k \qquad \forall s \in S.
\tag{K}\label{K-def}
\end{equation}

The designer must also satisfy a revenue requirement:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i
\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[t_i(\theta,s)\right] - I(k) \ge 0.
\tag{R}\label{R-def}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Planner's objective.}
The market designer maximizes a weighted expected sum of consumer surpluses. Let $\lambda_i(\theta) \ge 0$ denote the welfare weight assigned to a consumer of type $\theta$ in category $i$, and let $\tilde\lambda_i := \E_{i}[\lambda_i(\theta)]$ denote the average weight in category $i$. The objective is
\[
\sum_{i \in N} \mu_i \,
\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[
\lambda_i(\theta)\left(\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\right)
\right].
\]

\paragraph{Timing.}
The interaction unfolds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Consumers privately learn their type $\theta$.
    \item The market designer chooses investment $k$.
    \item A mechanism $(q_i,t_i)$ is proposed.
    \item The shock $s$ is realized and allocations are implemented.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Mechanism}\label{sec:PI}

We now consider the allocation problem under private information. The consumer’s category $i$ is publicly observed, while the type $\theta$ is privately known to the consumer. 

By the revelation principle, it is without loss to focus on direct mechanisms. A direct mechanism specifies, for each category $i$, a quantity schedule $q_i(\theta,s)$ and a transfer schedule $t_i(\theta,s)$. For private information, the mechanism is feasible if it satisfies the previous constraints \eqref{R-def}, \eqref{K-def}, and \eqref{IR-def}, as well as an incentive-compatibility constraint. Truthful reporting requires that, for all $\theta,\hat\theta \in \Theta_i$, 
\[
\E_s\!\left[\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\right] \;\ge\; \E_s\!\left[\theta U(q_i(\hat\theta,s),s) - t_i(\hat\theta,s)\right].
\tag{IC$^\times$}\label{IC-def1}
\] 
Constraint \eqref{IC-def1} imposes incentive compatibility in expectation over the common shock $s$. Under \eqref{IC-def1}, transfers can be chosen such that the expected surplus of a consumer of type $\theta$ in category $i$ satisfies 
%##########################################
\[ \tag{$\E CS_i$}\label{ECSi} \E CS_i(\theta):=\E_s\!\left[\theta U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)-t_i(\theta,s)\right] = \E\underline{CS}_i+\int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\theta}\E_s\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\tilde{\theta},s),s\right)\right]\,d\tilde{\theta}. \]
%##########################################
for some boundary term $\E\underline{CS}_i \ge 0$. This representation follows from standard envelope arguments.\footnote{In general, IC in expectation is weaker than pointwise IC: monotonicity of the expected marginal payoff does not, by itself, imply that the allocation is nondecreasing in $\theta$ for every state $s$ separately. We verify ex post that the optimal solution satisfies pointwise IC under Assumption Int. Off-peak, the allocation is independent of $\theta$. On-peak, Assumption~\ref{ass:interior} ensures that the effective social weight is strictly increasing in $\theta$, which implies that the allocation is strictly increasing in $\theta$ in every binding state simultaneously. The optimal allocation, therefore, satisfies pointwise IC, and imposing IC in expectation is without loss at the optimum.} 

\paragraph{Virtual-surplus primitives.} Let $\gamma_i(\theta)$ denote the inverse hazard rate of $G_i$ and $J_i(\theta)$ the virtual surplus function associated with a consumer from category $i$ and type $\theta$
%##########################################
\[
\gamma_i(\theta):= \frac{1-G_i(\theta)}{g_i(\theta)} \quad \text{and} \quad  J_i(\theta):=\theta - \gamma_i(\theta).
\]
%##########################################
We maintain throughout the following regularity condition

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Reg}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:regularity}
For each category $i$, the virtual surplus $J_i(\theta)$ is strictly increasing on $[\underline{\theta}_i, \overline{\theta}_i]$.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Then define $\Lambda_i(\theta) := \gamma_i(\theta)\E[\lambda_i(\tilde\theta) \mid \tilde\theta \ge \theta]$. This term captures the social value of the information rents accruing to all types $\tilde\theta > \theta$ when type $\theta$ receives a marginal unit: incentive compatibility forces the designer to also compensate all higher types, at a rate governed by the inverse hazard rate $\gamma_i(\theta)$, and these rents are evaluated at the average social weight of types above $\theta$.

\begin{lemma}[Virtual surplus representation]\label{lem:Transfer}
The program of the market designer can be expressed as: 
%##########################################
\begin{align*}
\max_{q_i(\theta,s), \, \E\underline{CS}_i}  \quad\quad 
&  \sum_{i \in N}  \mu_i   \; 
\Big\{ \;  \tilde{\lambda}_i \;\E\underline{CS}_i 
      + \E_{(s,i)} \big[\; \Lambda_i(\theta)\;  U(q_i(\theta,s),s)\; \big] \;  \Big\}
\tag{CS}\label{CSmd-def}  \\
\text{s.t.} \quad
& \sum_{i\in N}\mu_i
(\E_{(s,i)}\left[ U(q_i(\theta,s),s) J_i(\theta) \right] - \E\underline{CS}_i)\;-\; I(k)
\;\ge\; 0. \tag{R}\label{Rmd-def}
\end{align*}
%##########################################
with the same capacity constraint \eqref{K-def} given allocation schedule $q_i(\theta,s)$ and the participation constraint for the lower type ($\E\underline{CS}_i\ge 0$).
\end{lemma}

The lemma separates the transfer and allocation problems. When one of the budget or IR constraints binds while the other is slack, a lump-sum transfer can be used to relax the binding constraint without violating the other. This transfer is constrained by IC/IR: the designer can only finance investment from the allocation's utility net of information rents, and the lowest type must weakly prefer to participate.

Because both the objective and the constraints are separable across categories except through the aggregate constraints on capacity and revenue, the problem can be decomposed into:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item an across-category allocation of capacity and revenue requirements $(k_i,I_i)$ satisfying
    \[
    \sum_i \mu_i k_i = k,
    \qquad
    \sum_i \mu_i I_i = I(k),
    \]
    \item and, for each category $i$, a within-category problem that determines the optimal allocation rule $q_i(\theta,s)$ given $(k_i,I_i)$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Optimal allocation}\label{chap3sec:sec5}

\subsection{Within category allocation}\label{sec:within}

This section studies the within-category problem for a given pair $(k_i, I_i)$. The analysis proceeds in two parts. The first asks how capacity expansion reshapes surplus across types: the answer depends on how marginal virtual surplus varies across types, which determines who benefits from relaxing scarcity once incentive constraints and redistributive preferences are accounted for. The second holds capacity fixed and asks how a change in redistributive preferences reallocates quantities and surplus: here both the sorting of virtual surplus and the proportional reweighting of social priorities interact, producing a generically non-monotone surplus pattern regardless of the direction of the preference shift.

\subsubsection{Characterization}

We begin with the basic structure of the optimal within-category allocation. The main takeaway is that the entire allocation can be organized into two regimes: one in which the participation constraint is slack and the allocation is undistorted, and one in which financing the revenue requirement induces an endogenous distortion via the shadow value $\beta_i$.

Following Lemma~\ref{lem:Transfer}, the within-category problem selects an allocation rule $q_i(\theta,s)$ to maximize category $i$'s contribution to the designer's objective, subject to a category-specific capacity level $k_i$ and revenue requirement $I_i$. Since transfers enter consumer surplus with a negative sign, any slack in the revenue requirement can be rebated lump-sum to consumers while preserving incentive compatibility, raising the objective. Therefore the revenue requirement binds at the optimum: $\E_{(s,i)}[t_i(\theta,s)] = I_i$. Substituting the envelope representation \eqref{ECSi} into this equality and integrating by parts with respect to $\theta$,, using the definition of $\gamma_i(\theta)$ and applying Lemma~\ref{lem:Transfer} to separate the lump-sum transfer $\underline{CS}_i$ from the allocation-dependent rent, yields the single combined constraint
%##########################################
\[
\E \underline{CS}_i
=
\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right) J_i(\theta)\right]-I_i.
\tag{$\E \underline{CS}_i$}\label{underlineECSi}
\]
%##########################################
This reformulation combines the participation and budget requirements in a single constraint when the designer maximizes consumer surplus. Substituting $\E \underline{CS}_i$ into the objective, the within-category problem becomes
%##########################################
\begin{align*}
\max_{q_i(\theta,s)} \quad & \E_{(s,i)}\!\left[\Gamma_i(\theta)\,U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)-\tilde\lambda_i I_i\right]
\label{CSmdi-def}\tag{CS} \\
\text{s.t.}\quad &
\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right) J_i(\theta)\right]-I_i \ge 0,
\label{Rmdi-def}\tag{IR-R$_i$} \\
& \E_{i}\!\left[q_i(\theta,s)\right]\le k_i \qquad \forall s.
\label{Kmdi-def}\tag{K$_i$}
\end{align*}
%##########################################
Where $\Gamma_i(\theta) = \tilde\lambda_i J_i(\theta) + \Lambda_i(\theta)$ captures the total social return from allocating a marginal unit to type $\theta$, combining two channels: (i) a revenue channel $\tilde\lambda_i J_i(\theta)$, which is the net revenue extractable from type $\theta$ --- the gross value $\theta$ minus the rent $\gamma_i(\theta)$ that incentive compatibility forces the designer to concede to all higher types --- valued at the average social weight $\tilde\lambda_i$; and (ii) a redistributive channel $\Lambda_i(\theta)$, capturing the social value of those same rents as they accrue to types above $\theta$. Let $\varepsilon_i(s)\ge 0$ and $\beta_i\ge 0$ denote the multipliers on the capacity constraint \eqref{Kmdi-def} and the IR/budget constraint \eqref{Rmdi-def}.

The first-order condition is
%##########################################
\[
\label{KKT-qMD}\tag{FOC$_q$}
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\,\G_i(\theta)-\varepsilon_i(s)=0,
\]
%##########################################
where $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) := \Gamma_i(\theta) + J_i(\theta)\,\beta_i$.\footnote{Equivalently, $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) = (\tilde\lambda_i + \beta_i)J_i(\theta) + \Lambda_i(\theta)$: the effective social weight is therefore an equilibrium object: for instance, when $J_i(\theta) > 0$, a relaxation of the budget constraint ($\beta_i$ falling) reduces the premium placed on revenue-generating types and lowers their effective weight.}. We focus on interior solutions and impose two regularity conditions. The first discipline of the allocation rule: it ensures all types receive a strictly positive allocation and that the monotonicity constraint is automatically satisfied at the optimum.\footnote{If the first set of conditions fails, standard Myerson-style ironing restores monotonicity without affecting the subsequent comparative statics. As discussed by \cite{Martimort2020a}, this condition is equivalent to social weights being small enough compared to inverse price elasticity for the good. In the context of an essential good where redistributive concerns are more likely to exist, this condition might be a weak requirement. This stands in contrast with settings where the supply side imposes additional structure on the allocation: either indivisibility, as in \cite{CONDORELLI2013582}, or a fixed-quality schedule, as in \cite{akbarpour2023redistributive}, which restricts the set of implementable mechanisms.}

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Int}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:interior}
For all $\theta\in\left[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i\right]$, $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)>0$ and $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$ is non-decreasing in $\theta$, so that the monotonicity constraint on $q_i(\theta,s)$ is satisfied at the optimum. Moreover, the marginal utility function satisfies $\lim_{q\rightarrow 0}u(q,s) = +\infty$.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Define $S_i=[\underline s,s_i)$ and $T_i=[s_i,\overline s]$ as the off-peak and on-peak sets under the optimal allocation, where $s_i$ satisfies $\E_{i}\!\left[q_i(\theta,s_i)\right]=k_i$.

\begin{lemma}[Off-peak and on-peak allocation]\label{lem:FOC}
Assume~\ref{ass:interior} holds and that the within-category problem is convex. i) Under the weighted objective with type-dependent weights $\lambda(\theta)$, the allocation is characterized by a unique quantity schedule $q_i(\theta,s)$ for each $\theta$ and $s$:
%##########################################
\[
\label{eq:SB-Smd1}\tag{SB-S}
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)=0
\quad s\in S_i,
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\,\G_i(\theta)=\varepsilon_i(s)
\quad s\in T_i.
\]
%##########################################
ii) If \eqref{Rmdi-def} is slack at $(k_i,I_i)$, then $\beta_i=0$ and $\G_i(\theta)\equiv \Gamma_i(\theta)$.
\end{lemma}

\subsubsection{Sufficient statistics}\label{sec:suffstat}

With the optimality conditions in hand, we know discuss two margins that will significantly drive the result of this analysis. Their interaction delivers a sufficient statistic whose sign will organize the rest of the section. In particular, it simultaneously governs surplus incidence of capacity expansion, the response of the revenue distortion, and the feasibility of the undistorted regime.

To do so, define
\[
R_q(\theta):=-\frac{u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)}{u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)},
\qquad
R_\theta(\theta):=\frac{J_i(\theta)}{\mathcal G_i(\theta)}.
\]
Note that $R_q(\theta)\geq0$ since $u_q<0$. The first object, $R_q(\theta)$, is determined by the curvature of demand and governs how a marginal increase in $k_i$ is distributed across types at the optimal allocation (we also use the term responsiveness). The second, $R_\theta(\theta)$, reflects both the type distribution and the redistributive preferences of the designer, and governs how marginal virtual surplus $u(q_i,s)J_i(\theta)$ varies across types.\footnote{If $R_q(\theta)$ is decreasing in $\theta$, then as capacity expands, lower types receive a higher share than higher types. If $R_\theta(\theta)$ is increasing in $\theta$, then the marginal virtual surplus tends to be lower for lower types. Formally: $\operatorname{sign}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\!\left(\frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s)}{\partial k}\right) = \operatorname{sign}\, R_q'(\theta)$ and $\operatorname{sign}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\!\left(u(q_i(\theta,s),s)J_i(\theta)\right) = \operatorname{sign}\, R_\theta'(\theta)$.} Crucially, $J_i(\theta)$ need not be positive: even when a consumer has negative virtual surplus, their marginal contribution to the objective may remain positive from Assumption~\ref{ass:interior}.

Define the weights
\[
w_i(\theta,s):=-\frac{1}{u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\mathcal G_i(\theta)},
\quad
\overline w_i(s):=\E_{i}\!\left[w_i(\theta,s)\right], \quad \Omega_i:= \E_s\left[\frac{1}{\overline{w}_i(s)}\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]
\]
Those weights represent the departure from the first-best allocation without redistributive preferences as they create systematic differences in marginal utilities across types. Then defined the $w_i$-weighted average of the marginal virtual surplus
\[
\E_i^w[uJ\mid s]
:=
\frac{1}{\overline w_i(s)}
\E_{i}\!\left[w_i(\theta,s)\,u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)J_i(\theta)\right]
=
\frac{1}{\overline w_i(s)}
\E_{i}\!\left[R_\theta(\theta)R_q(\theta)\right].
\]
Its average over binding states yields
\begin{equation}
\E_{(s,i)}^w[uJ\mid s] := \mathbb E_s\!\left[\E_i^w[uJ\mid s]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]. \tag{EuJ}\label{Eujmd}
\end{equation}
This weighted value plays a key role in this analysis: it is the expected marginal revenue for the market designer with respect to the level of capacity, accounting for redistributive preferences and incentive constraints.\footnote{Equivalently, in the context of a market designer that maximizes consumer surplus under a given budget constraint, this is equivalent to the marginal change of available surplus the designer can redistributive to consumer once the budget and informational rent have been covered.} To see this, the statistic \eqref{Eujmd} combines two margins: how extra capacity is allocated across types, through \(R_q\), and how socially valuable those marginal allocations are, through \(R_\theta\). Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
\E_{i}\!\left[R_\theta(\theta)R_q(\theta)\right]
=
\E_{i}[R_\theta(\theta)]\,\E_{i}[R_q(\theta)]
+
\Cov_i\!\left(R_\theta(\theta),R_q(\theta)\right). \tag{signEuJ}\label{signEuJ}
\end{equation}
The first term captures an average effect: increasing capacity frees up surplus for all consumers, thereby increasing the market designer's available revenue. The covariance term captures a sorting effect: it is adverse when additional capacity is tilted toward types with lower marginal virtual surplus.\footnote{For instance, if $R_q(\theta)$ is decreasing and $R_\theta(\theta)$ is increasing, then the lowest marginal virtual surplus is concentrated at the lowest $\theta$. In other words, as $k_i$ increases, a greater weight is placed on types with a potentially negative marginal virtual surplus. A decreasing virtual surplus ($J_i(\theta)<0$) implies that increasing $k_i$ reduces the budget the market designer can raise from those consumers. Therefore, the condition highlights the fundamental trade-off the market designer faces: increasing the allocation of a type, given its social weight, may conflict with revenue maximisation due to the IC constraint on higher types.}

To obtain a sharp characterization, we impose the following regularity condition.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Monot.}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:monotone}
\(R_q(\theta)\) and \(R_\theta(\theta)\) are both monotone on \([\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]\). Moreover, \(R_q(\theta)\) exhibits log-monotone differences in \((\theta,k_i)\).
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

In particular, we show in Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir} of the Appendix that \eqref{Eujmd} is single-crossing in \(k_i\) depending on whether \(R_q(\theta)\) and \(R_\theta(\theta)\) are increasing or decreasing, and on whether \(R_q(\theta)\) exhibits positive or negative log-monotone differences in \((\theta,k_i)\). Hence, the market designer's revenue is either single-peaked or single-dipped.\footnote{The economic interpretation of log-monotone differences in \((\theta,k_i)\) is intuitive. Suppose that, for each \(k_i\), $R_\theta(\theta)$ is increasing in $\theta$, so higher types receive higher effective virtual surplus, and that \(R_q(\theta)\) is log-submodular in \((\theta,k_i)\). Then capacity expansion disproportionately raises \(R_q\) for lower types, in the sense that for \(k_i'>k_i\), the ratio \(R_q(\theta,k_i')/R_q(\theta)\) is decreasing in \(\theta\). Thus, as capacity expands, the weighted average \eqref{Eujmd} shifts monotonically from the regime in which the average effect dominates toward the regime in which the sorting effect dominates.} We discuss in section~\ref{appsec:singlecross} in the Appendix, the conditions for establishing the monotonicity of those ratios. When those assumptions fail, the same sufficient statistic remains valid, but its sign need not vary monotonically with \(k_i\).

Two special cases simplify the analysis by restricting which arguments $R_q$ depends on.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{CARA}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:CARA}
$u(q,s) = v(s)\exp(-\alpha q)$ for some $\alpha > 0$ and some function $v(s) > 0$.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, $R_q = 1/\alpha$ is constant: a consumer's responsiveness is identical regardless of the quantity allocated to her. Additional capacity is therefore distributed across types in proportions determined solely by social weights, and the behavioral channel through which a preference shift distorts the allocation across types vanishes.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Multi}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:mult}
$u(q,s) = \kappa(q)\zeta(s)$ for some functions $\kappa$ and $\zeta$ with $\kappa_q < 0$ and $\zeta_s > 0$.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:mult}, $R_q = -\kappa(q)/\kappa_q(q)$ depends only on $q$ and not on $s$: a consumer's responsiveness is identical regardless of the realization of the common shock. Note that Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA} implies Assumption~\ref{ass:mult}, with $\kappa(q) = \exp(-\alpha q)$ and $\zeta(s) = v(s)$.


\subsubsection{Redistributive effects of capacity expansion}\label{sec:surplus}

We now study the central within-category question: who benefits from a marginal increase in capacity? Even though a larger $k_i$ relaxes scarcity, the surplus gains from this relaxation need not accrue to all types once incentive constraints and revenue extraction are taken into account. This subsection shows that the incidence of capacity expansion is pinned down by the sign of \eqref{Eujmd} together with the ordering of types induced by $R_\theta(\theta)$.

The individual surplus change follows from \eqref{ECSi} and is decomposed into two terms: i) the type-independent component that corresponds to the available surplus after accounting for the budget and the informational rents, and whose directional change is given by \ref{Eujmd}, and ii) the informational rent which depends on the optimal allocation.
%##########################################
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\E CS_i(\theta)}{\partial k_i} = \underbrace{\Bigg.\frac{\partial\E\underline{CS}_i}{\partial k_i}\Bigg.}_{ \text{type-independent transfer} } +\underbrace{\Bigg. \int_{\underline\theta_i}^{\theta} \E_s\!\left[\frac{\partial q_i(\tilde\theta,s)}{\partial k_i} u\!\left(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s\right)\right]d\tilde\theta\Bigg. }_{\text{IC type-dependent transfer}}. \tag{$\partial_k CS$}\label{partialkCS1}
\end{equation}
%##########################################
The derivative of the type-independent component is obtained from \eqref{ECSi} while differentiating the first-order condition \eqref{KKT-qMD} with respect to $k_i$ gives
%##########################################
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s)}{\partial k_i} = w_i(\theta,s)\left( \frac{1}{\overline w_i(s)} -\frac{\partial\beta_i}{\partial k_i} \Bigl(\E^w_i[uJ\mid s] -u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)J_i(\theta)\Bigr) \right). \tag{$\partial_k q_i$}\label{partialkq1}
\end{equation}
%##########################################

Two observations: when \eqref{Rmdi-def} binds (the IR-constrained allocation), the boundary term us null: the entire surplus change is then carried by the IC rent in \eqref{partialkCS1}. On the other hand,  when \eqref{Rmdi-def} is slack (the IR-unconstrained allocation), then the second term in \eqref{partialkq1} is null as $\beta_i = 0$.

\begin{theorem}[Redistributive effects of capacity expansion]\label{thm:surplus}
Fix category \(i\), a budget requirement \(I_i>0\) and assume~\ref{ass:monotone} and~\ref{ass:mult} hold. Define the set of cutoffs 
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\[\tilde\Theta_i := \left\{\tilde\theta_i\in[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i] : \partial\E CS_i(\tilde\theta_i)/\partial k_i=0\right\}.\]
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\noindent\textbf{IR-unconstrained allocation.} If \eqref{Eujmd} is positive, then an increase in \(k_i\) increases expected surplus for every type \(\theta\). If \eqref{Eujmd} is negative, \(\tilde\Theta_i\) contains at most a unique cutoff; and if it exists expected surplus decreases for all \(\theta<\tilde\theta_i\) and increases for all \(\theta>\tilde\theta_i\).

\noindent\textbf{IR-constrained allocation.} The lowest type always lies on the boundary: \(\underline\theta_i\in\tilde\Theta_i\). If \(R_\theta(\theta)\) is increasing, a second cutoff \(\tilde\theta_i\in(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)\) may exist and when it does:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
    \item if \eqref{Eujmd} is positive, expected surplus increases for all \(\theta\in(\underline\theta_i,\tilde\theta_i)\) and decreases for all \(\theta\ge\tilde\theta_i\);
    \item if \eqref{Eujmd} is negative, expected surplus decreases for all \(\theta\in(\underline\theta_i,\tilde\theta_i)\) and increases for all \(\theta\ge\tilde\theta_i\).
\end{enumerate}
If \(R_\theta(\theta)\) is decreasing, the inequalities are reversed.
\end{theorem}

Expanding capacity is not always surplus-improving for all consumers, and the identity of who gains/loses depends on the regime and on the joint sign of \eqref{Eujmd} and \(R_\theta(\theta)\). Importantly, the mechanism through which a capacity expansion determines the progressivity/regressivity of the capacity expansion is not the same across regimes. Under the IR-unconstrained regime, it operates through changes in the boundary term \eqref{underlineECSi}, while under the IR-constrained regime it operates through changes in the optimal allocation \(q_i(\theta,s)\).

When the participation constraint is slack (\(\beta_i=0\)), the optimal allocation is increasing in \(k_i\) for all types in binding states. Hence, the IC component is always increasing. However, the sign of the type-independent component in \eqref{partialkCS1} depends entirely on the sign of \eqref{Eujmd}, which therefore summarizes the marginal redistributive effect of expanding capacity. If \eqref{Eujmd}>0, both the common surplus component and the induced IC rents increase with \(k_i\), so all types gain. If \eqref{Eujmd}<0, lower types lose while higher types gain, with a unique cutoff as stated in Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus}.\footnote{We show in the proof that the type-dependent part in \eqref{partialkCS1} (the marginal IC rent) is increasing in \(\theta\). This monotonicity property yields the uniqueness of the cutoff in part~(ii), as well as the ordering of the types that experience a decrease in surplus.}

When the participation constraint binds, the type-independent component is null, so the surplus effect of a capacity expansion is driven by the quantity margin. However, the monotonicity of quantities in $k_i$ may fail because a capacity expansion also changes the tightness of the financing constraint through the endogenous response of \(\beta_i\), as shown in \eqref{partialkq1}. The bracketed term in that expression is the deviation of a type's marginal virtual surplus \(u(q_i(\theta,s),s)J_i(\theta)\) from its \(w_i\)-weighted average; its sign is therefore determined by the relative ranking of types according to marginal virtual surplus, which is captured by \(R_\theta(\theta)\).  The sign of \(\partial \beta_i/\partial k_i\) is again governed by \eqref{Eujmd}, which captures how capacity expansion tightens or loosens the budget constraint. Therefore, the condition on \ref{Eujmd} and $R_\theta(\theta)$ has a direct interpretation: suppose that expanding \(k_i\) makes the budget less tight, so that \(\beta_i\) decreases in \(k_i\). The need for revenue extraction then falls, and the designer lowers the marginal weight on types that generate higher marginal virtual surplus.

Assumption~\ref{ass:monotone} allows us to characterize cleanly the set of winners and losers from a capacity expansion. Without this requirement, the model may simply exhibit a richer set of cutoffs. Assumption~\ref{ass:mult} is required only for the IR-constrained case so that the single-crossing structure is preserved after aggregation over states. In Appendix Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation}, we show that \eqref{Eujmd} also governs the transition between the IR-unconstrained and IR-constrained regimes. This transition depends on whether the available revenue is single-peaked or single-dipped. Figure~\ref{fig:impl_surplus} summarizes this regime geometry by illustrating how the distributional incidence of a capacity expansion changes across regimes and across capacity levels, while the section~\ref{appsec:implementation-region} in the Appendix provides the full characterization. A direct implication is that the redistributive effect of additional capacity is not invariant to the installed capacity level: the same marginal expansion can be progressive at low \(k_i\) and regressive at high \(k_i\). We also provide a closed-form numerical simulation in the Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_redistribution_marg_k_uniform} illustrating how the identity of the winners and losers from a capacity expansion depends on the current level of capacity.

\subsubsection{Redistributive preferences and optimal allocation}\label{sec:redist}

The previous section asked how a given redistributive objective shapes the surplus consequences of capacity expansion. We now reverse the question: holding $k_i$ fixed, how does a change of redistributive preferences reshape the optimal allocation? This allows us to study how incentive constraints shape the incidence of redistributive preference changes for a given capacity constraint, via the effective social weights.\footnote{This comparative static is also the building block of the across-category analysis in Section~\ref{sec:across}: comparing the optimal allocations of two categories that differ marginally in preferences is equivalent to evaluating the local effect of a preference perturbation at fixed capacity.} 

Let $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta)$ denote the marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences. The induced change in the effective welfare weight is
%##########################################
\[
\Delta\G_i(\theta):= J_i(\theta) (\Delta\tilde\lambda_i+\Delta\beta_i)+\Delta\Lambda_i(\theta)
\quad \text{with} \quad  
\Delta\Lambda_i(\theta):=\gamma_i(\theta)\E_{i}\!\left[\Delta\lambda_i(\tilde\theta)\mid \tilde\theta\ge \theta\right].
\]
%##########################################
In a similar spirit to the previous section, the effect of a marginal perturbation of preferences is captured by an average and a sorting effect. The key statistic is  the proportional change in the effective social weight of a type $\theta$:
\[
R_{\G}(\theta):=\frac{\Delta\G_i(\theta)}{\G_i(\theta)},
\]
which measures how the perturbation tilts the effective weight of type $\theta$ relative to the baseline allocation. What matters for the reallocation is therefore not solely the absolute change of $\G_i(\theta)$. Importantly, the IC structure implies that a change of $\lambda_i(\theta)$ of a single type $\theta$ propagates to other types. Therefore, we also define the effect at the category level, the aggregate term  $\E^w_i\!\left[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s\right]$ which is the average of $R_\G(\theta)$ weights by $w_i(\theta,s)$ as in \eqref{Eujmd}.

Appendix Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder} provides primitive conditions on preference perturbations under which $R_{\G}(\theta)$ has a constant sign across types. The first part of the Lemma states that a proportional tilt of welfare weights toward higher types shifts the social value of the information rent given to types above $\theta$ (i.e., $\Lambda_i(\theta)$) upward relative to the baseline across the type space.\footnote{The first result of the Lemma has a direct implication for the sign of $R'_\theta(\theta)$. From expression \eqref{signRt}, only the IC term depends on preferences; the revenue term is determined solely by the type distribution. As a maintained example, assume $\Lambda_i^{1\,\prime}(\theta)<0$, $\Lambda_i^{2\,\prime}(\theta)<0$, and $J_i'(\theta)>0$. In that case, a preference shift toward higher types favors $R_\theta'(\theta)>0$. For types generating revenue ($J_i(\theta)>0$), $R_\theta'(\theta)>0$ holds under both weight functions. For types that do not generate revenue ($J_i(\theta)<0$), the condition in \eqref{signRt} is easier to satisfy under a preference shift toward higher types, since $\Lambda_i^{\Delta\,\prime}/\Lambda_i^\Delta \ge \Lambda_i^{\prime}/\Lambda_i$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}.} The second part connects the direction of a preference shift to the sign of $R_{\G}(\theta)$, but requires two conditions: the effective weight must not decrease on average, and the average preference shift must be at least as large as the effective-weight shift after accounting for the budget shadow cost. These conditions simplify under two special cases on the equilibrium object $\beta_i$, which will be used later for the across-category results: tilting welfare weights proportionally toward higher types, while not decreasing the average weight, yields $R_{\G}(\theta)>0$ for all $\theta$; and conversely, shifting proportionally toward lower types while not increasing the average weight yields $R_{\G}(\theta)<0$ for all $\theta$. A standard perturbation in the redistribution literature is a (monotonic) mean-preserving rotation of $\lambda_i(\theta)$, i.e.\ $\Delta\tilde\lambda_i=0$. Under such a rotation, $R_{\G}(\theta)$ has a constant sign throughout the type space: positive when the rotation favors higher types, and negative when it favors lower types.

We now turn to the characterization of how such perturbations affect the optimal allocation.

\begin{lemma}[Reallocation under preference perturbations]\label{lem:realloc}
Fix category \(i\) and an across-category allocation \((k_i,I_i)\). For any local perturbation $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta)$ and any allocation regime,
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s) = R_q(\theta)\!\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta) - \E^w_i\!\left[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s\right]\right), \qquad s\in T_i.
\]
\end{lemma}

To interpret Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}, type $\theta$ receives more quantity if and only if its proportional change in effective social weight exceeds the proportional average. The intuition is the following. Linearizing the pointwise first-order condition yields
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s)
=
w_i(\theta,s)\bigl(u(q_i,s)\,\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)-\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)\bigr)
=
R_q(\theta)\!\left(R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)-\mathbb E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]\right).
\label{Deltaq}\tag{$\Delta$q}\]
That is the condition of receiving more or less allocation is determined by whether the preference change raises the marginal utility valued at the effect social weights of type $\theta$ by more or less than the equilibrium adjustment in the scarcity rent.\footnote{ The equivalence between the two equations stems from the observation that the aggregate proportional change in the effective social weight is also the proportional change in the scarcity rent
\[
\frac{\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)}{\varepsilon_i(s)}
=
\E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right].
\]
} Since capacity is fixed, the reallocation is zero-sum across types within each binding state: $\mathbb E_i\!\left[\Delta q_i(\theta,s)\right]=0$ for each $s\in T_i$.



The sorting effect of the optimal allocation induced by a preference perturbation is, however, different than the direction of the perturbation itself. Appendix Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation} shows that the behavioral response $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$ can be non-monotone in types, so a tilt of preferences toward higher or lower types does not mechanically translate into a monotone quantity reallocation.\footnote{Take the covariance term in \eqref{signEuJ}, then a perturbation in preference yields $\Cov_i\!\left(R_\theta(\theta),\Delta R_q(\theta)\right) + \Cov_i\!\left(\Delta R_\theta(\theta),R_q(\theta)\right)$. The non-monotonicity in $\Delta q$ prevents signing the first term and, by extension, the effect of a perturbation on the sign of \eqref{Eujmd}. Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA} avoids these limits by shutting down the behavioral effect of a perturbation on the final equilibrium as $\Delta R_q(\theta)=0$.} This non-monotonicity of the optimal allocation stems from the fact that the element that drives its response is the proportional change $R_{\G}(\theta)$ and not the absolute change $\Delta G_i(\theta)$. While a change in preferences may induce a monotonic variation in the absolute change $\Delta \mathcal G_i(\theta)$ along types, this need not be the case for the proportional change $R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)$. To see this, consider the extreme types $\underline{\theta}_i$ and $\overline{\theta}_i$ and a perturbation of an optimal allocation in the IR-unconstrained regime. Using the definition of $J_i(\theta)$ and $\Lambda_i(\theta)$, the effective weight is $\G_i(\theta)= \theta  \tilde\lambda_i - \gamma_i(\theta) \tilde\lambda_i + \gamma_i(\theta)\E_{i}\!\left[\lambda_i(\tilde\theta)\mid \tilde\theta\ge \theta\right]$. At $\underline{\theta}_i$, the social value of the information rent is the information rent valued at the expected social weights: $\gamma_i(\theta)\tilde\lambda_i$, so the second and third terms cancel: $\G_i( \underline{\theta}_i)= \underline{\theta}_i  \tilde\lambda_i$. At $\overline{\theta}_i$, the information rent is absent: $\gamma_i(\theta)=0$, so the second and third terms are null: $\G_i(\overline{\theta}_i)= \overline{\theta}_i  \tilde\lambda_i$. The perturbation yeilds similar values:  $\Delta \G_i( \underline{\theta}_i)= \underline{\theta}_i  \Delta \tilde\lambda_i$ and $\Delta \G_i(\overline{\theta}_i)= \overline{\theta}_i  \Delta \tilde\lambda_i$. Therefore, while we can have $\Delta \G_i(\underline{\theta}_i) \neq \Delta \G_i(\overline{\theta}_i)$, we always have $R_\G(\underline{\theta}_i) = R_\G(\overline{\theta}_i)$ for any perturbation of redistributive preferences. In particular, the second part of Appendix Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation} further shows that, under mean-preserving perturbations ($\Delta \tilde\lambda_i=0$), the induced gains and losses must necessarily occur at the type boundaries in the opposite direction from the interior reallocation, whatever the direction of preference changes.

We are now ready to characterize the surplus effect of a change in preferences. Similarly to the capacity extension expression \eqref{partialkCS1}, the individual surplus change follows from \eqref{ECSi}
%##########################################
\begin{equation*}
  \Delta CS_i(\theta) = \Delta \E\underline{CS}_i + \int_{\underline\theta_i}^{\theta} \E_s\!\left[\Delta q_i(\tilde\theta,s) u\!\left(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s\right)\right]d\tilde\theta
\end{equation*}
Using lemma \ref{lem:realloc}, the marginal change with respect to types yields
\[\frac{\partial\Delta\E CS_i(\theta)}{\partial\theta} = \E_s\!\left[\frac{\varepsilon_i(s)}{\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)}R_q(\theta)\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s\right]\right)\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right],\]
where $\varepsilon_i(s)/\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)>0$ and $R_q(\theta)>0$. Similarly, a perturbation of preference changes the available revenue \eqref{underlineECSi} via the optimal allocation:
\[
\Delta\E\underline{CS}_i =\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\,R_\theta(\theta)\,R_q(\theta)\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s\right]\right)\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right].
\]


\begin{proposition}[Surplus effects of redistributive preference perturbations]\label{prop:surplus-redist}
Assume Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA} and unimodality of $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$. Define the set of cutoffs
\[
\tilde\Theta_i^\Delta := \left\{\tilde\theta_i\in[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i] : \Delta\E CS_i(\tilde\theta_i)=0\right\}.
\]
\noindent\textbf{IR-unconstrained allocation.} There exist at most three cutoffs in $(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)$. The pattern of $\Delta\E CS_i(\theta)$ is jointly determined by two signs: $\mathrm{Cov}_{i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})$, which governs the starting value,
\[\sign\!\left(\Delta\E CS_i(\underline\theta_i)\right)=\sign\!\left(\mathrm{Cov}_{i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})\right),\]
and $R_{\G}$, which governs the interior shape. If $R_{\G}\ge 0$ for all $\theta$: expected surplus follows the pattern $+,-,+,-$ when $\mathrm{Cov}_{i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})>0$, and $-,+,-$ when $\mathrm{Cov}_{i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})<0$. If $R_{\G}\le 0$ for all $\theta$, the starting value and all interior inequalities are reversed.

\noindent\textbf{IR-constrained allocation.} The lowest type always lies on the boundary: $\underline\theta_i\in\tilde\Theta_i^\Delta$. There exist at most two additional cutoffs $\theta_1<\theta_2$ in $(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)$. If $R_{\G}\ge 0$ for all $\theta$, expected surplus decreases for $\theta\in(\underline\theta_i,\theta_1)$, increases for $\theta\in(\theta_1,\theta_2)$, and decreases for $\theta\in(\theta_2,\overline\theta_i)$. If $R_{\G}\le 0$ for all $\theta$, the inequalities are reversed.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:surplus-redist} delivers a sharp redistributive policy result. Even when the direction of a preference shift is unambiguous (as in Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}), the resulting surplus changes are generically non-monotone across types.\footnote{As in Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus}, Assumption~\ref{ass:mult} is sufficient to show that the individual surplus effect is generally non-monotone across types.  Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA} is used only to sharpen this result by delivering a closed-form expression for the boundary term \eqref{underlineECSi}. The unimodality of \(R_{\mathcal G}\) is used only to limit the number of sign changes of \(\Delta \E CS_i(\theta)\). If \(R_{\mathcal G}\) is not unimodal, additional crossings may arise, so the proposition should be read as a conservative characterization of the possible alternating surplus regions.}

In both regimes, the origin of the non-monotonicity in the change in surplus is the same: the individual surplus change combines an IC-rent channel and a level channel, and neither of them follows the direction of the preference perturbation mechanically. Figure~\ref{fig:surplus-redist} provides a simple geometry illustration of the proposition.

The first channel is the IC-rent channel. By construction, informational rents are built from the allocation received by lower types. As a result, when the perturbation changes the quantity assigned to some type \(\tilde\theta\), this change is inherited by all higher types through the rent formula. The shape of the IC-rent term is therefore determined by the shape of \(\Delta q_i(\theta,s)\) across types. Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc} shows that this behavioral response is itself governed by the gap between the proportional change in effective social weight, \(R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)\), and its equilibrium average. Since \(R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)\) is non-monotone, the induced quantity response is also generically non-monotone. Hence, even when the underlying preference shift is monotone, the associated IC-rent effect on surplus need not be monotone across types.

The second channel is a level effect, and its source depends on the regime. When participation is slack, the surplus change contains both the fixed component \(\Delta \mathbb E\underline{CS}_i\) and the IC-rent term. The fixed component is common across types and depends on how the surplus available to the designer moves under the perturbation; this is what gives rise to the covariance term in Proposition~\ref{prop:surplus-redist}. This covariance has a natural interpretation: if a consumer who receives the greatest proportional change is also the one who participates the most in the revenue, then the available surplus for all consumers increases. When participation binds, the lowest type remains at the boundary, so that \(\Delta \mathbb E CS_i(\underline\theta_i)=0\). In that case, the level effect is pinned down by the endpoint condition\footnote{The sign of the surplus change for the highest type is governed by the weighted integral $\E_i\!\left[\frac{R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)-\E_i[R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)]}{\mathcal G_i(\theta)}\right]$.}, and the entire surplus profile is shaped by the accumulation of the non-monotone quantity response through the IC formula.

Taken together, these two channels explain why the surplus effect of a redistributive preference perturbation is generically non-monotone across types. A shift in preferences toward higher or lower types does not translate mechanically into a monotone pattern of winners and losers, because the perturbation operates through the endogenous reallocation of quantities and through the way informational rents accumulate along the type space.

\begin{remark}[Link between $R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)$ and $R_\theta(\theta)$]
The identity from a local perturbation
\[\Delta R_\theta(\theta) = -R_\mathcal{G}(\theta) R_\theta(\theta)\]
provides a formal bridge between the two sufficient statistics that govern both the within- and across-category results. A preference perturbation reshapes who gains from capacity expansion precisely by changing how virtual surplus is sorted across types. We discuss in Appendix Proposition~\ref{prop:Elasticity} the conditions under which a change in preferences can clearly affect how virtual surplus is sorted across types (notably the monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$). When it fails, their joint effect on sorting can no longer be signed from primitives alone, and sharp results are no longer available.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Across category allocation}\label{sec:across}
Having characterized the within-category problem, we now turn to the designer's outer allocation problem: how to distribute total capacity and total revenue requirements across categories?

The previous section showed how redistributive preferences affect a category's internal allocation through the designer marginal revenue, the sorting of virtual surplus, and the proportional reweighting of effective social weights. The question here is how these within-category distortions translate into differences in categories' marginal values of capacity and revenue.

\subsubsection{Characterization}

We now turn to the across-category problem. Given the within-category value functions \(V_i(k_i,I_i)\), the designer chooses \(\{k_i,I_i\}_{i\in N}\) to solve
\[
\max_{\{k_i,I_i\}_{i\in N}} \sum_{i\in N}\mu_i\,V_i(k_i,I_i)
\]
subject to
\[
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i k_i = k,
\qquad
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i I_i = I(k),
\qquad
k_i\ge 0,\quad I_i\ge 0 \quad \forall i\in N.
\]
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
where $V_i(k_i, I_i)$ is the within-category value function defined as
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\[
V_i(k_i, I_i) \;:=\; \max_{q_i(\theta,s)} \;\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[\Gamma_i(\theta)\,U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\right] - \tilde\lambda_i I_i
\]
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
subject to incentive compatibility and the category-specific constraints. By the envelope theorem applied to the within-category problem, the two partial derivatives of $V_i$ are
\[
\partial_{k_i} V_i(k_i,I_i) \;=\; \overline \varepsilon_i \; , \qquad \partial_{I_i} V_i(k_i,I_i) \;=\; -\tilde\lambda_i - \beta_i(k_i,I_i).
\]
where $\overline \varepsilon_i =\E_s\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]$.

The envelope decomposition shows that the across-category problem is governed by only two marginal objects. First,
\[
\Pi_i(k_i,I_i) \;:=\; \partial_{k_i} V_i(k_i,I_i) \;=\; \E_s\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right] \;=\; \E_{(s,i)}\!\left[ u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\G_i(\theta)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right], \]
is the expected shadow value of relaxing the category-specific capacity constraint. As in canonical peak-load pricing, the marginal value of an additional unit of capacity is equal to the expected scarcity rent. Equivalently, using the within-category first-order condition, it can be written as the expected aggregate marginal utility generated by extra capacity. The difference relative to the canonical model is that this marginal utility is here weighted by the effective social weight \(\G_i(\theta)\), rather than by $\theta$. This IC-adjusted weighting will be crucial for the comparative statics below.\footnote{Note that \(\Pi_i(k_i,I_i)\) is strictly positive whenever category \(i\) is capacity-constrained with positive probability, since \(\varepsilon_i(s)>0\) in every on-peak state.}

Second,
\[
\partial_{I_i}V_i(k_i,I_i)
=
-\tilde\lambda_i-\beta_i(k_i,I_i),
\]
is the effective marginal social cost of requiring one additional unit of revenue from category \(i\). The term \(\beta_i\) captures the canonical marginal cost of tightening the budget, while \(\tilde\lambda_i\) captures the additional social cost induced by redistributive concerns.

\begin{lemma}[Across-category allocation]\label{lem:outer}
The outer problem admits at least one solution, and the set of optimal allocations is convex. The optimality conditions are, for each $i \in N$,
\[
\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast) \;=\; \zeta_k, \qquad \tilde\lambda_i + \beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast) \;=\; -\zeta_I + \xi_i,
\]
with complementary slackness $\xi_i I_i^\ast = 0$ for all $i$ and where $\zeta_k, \zeta_I \in \mathbb{R}$ denote the multipliers on the capacity and revenue equality constraints, and $\xi_i \ge 0$ the multiplier on $I_i \ge 0$.\footnote{The Inada conditions on $u(\cdot)$ ensure that $k_i>0$ at the optimum, so the multiplier on $k_i \ge 0$ is zero.}
\end{lemma}

The two optimality conditions have the usual economic interpretation. The designer allocates capacity until the marginal value of every categories, measure by $\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$, is the same across all categories. He equalizes the effective marginal social cost of revenue extraction $\tilde\lambda_i + \beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$ across categories that contribute to the budget.



\subsubsection{Ranking of the optimal allocation}\label{sec:rankingall}

We now study how redistributive preferences shape the allocation of capacity and revenue across categories. Categories differ through their social weight functions \(\lambda_i(\theta)\), and the question is how these differences translate into optimal capacity choices. We begin with a local comparison between two nearby categories, representing category \(j\) as a marginal variation of category \(i\), and then use this comparison to derive a global ordering along a path of redistributive preferences. Throughout, all categories share the same type support \(\Theta\).

Using the first-order conditions in Lemma~\ref{lem:outer}, consider the induced difference in the marginal value of capacity around category \(i\):
\[
\Delta \Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)
=
\E_{(s,i)}\![
(
\underbrace{u_q(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\G_i(\theta)\,\Delta q_i(\theta,s)
}_{\text{behavioral effect}}+
\underbrace{u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\Delta \G_i(\theta)}_{\text{weight effect}}
)
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}
].
\]
This expression separates the effect of a preference variation into two margins: a behavioral effect, through the induced change in the optimal allocation \(q_i(\theta,s)\), and a direct reweighting effect, through the change in effective social weights \(\Delta \G_i(\theta)\). By Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc} and \eqref{Deltaq}, the allocation response has the same decomposition. At the aggregate level, however, the two direct reweighting terms offset each other. What remains is the average proportional difference in effective social weights $\E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]$.

The next theorem provides a necessary and sufficient local condition for the following question: when do redistributive preference differences across categories translate into different capacity allocations?

\begin{theorem}[Category ranking]\label{thm:cat-order}
Suppose category \(j\) differs from a baseline category \(i\) only through a marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences. Fix the baseline optimum \((k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\). Then, to first order in \(\Delta\lambda\), the following capacity ranking holds
\[
k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\E_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s)\,
\E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}
\right]>0.
\]
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the condition reduces to $k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast$ if and only if $\E_i[R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)]>0$. 
\end{theorem}

The theorem delivers a direct outer-margin counterpart to the within-category analysis: the same object \(R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)\) that governed the reallocation of quantities and the surplus effects of preference differences within a category now determines which category receives more capacity at the outer optimum.

The key implication of the theorem is the following. The relevant comparison is not which category has the larger average effective weight, but whether category \(j\)'s advantage in effective weights is concentrated on types for which category \(i\)'s effective weight is low. Categories should therefore be ranked not by the absolute size of the difference in redistributive preferences, but by the relative distribution of their effective social weights across types. As a result, two categories may exhibit the same absolute difference in welfare weights and nevertheless receive opposite capacity rankings if that difference is distributed differently across the type space. A designer who focuses only on the sign and magnitude of the absolute difference in preferences, while ignoring how effective weights are sorted across types, may therefore misrank categories.

We provide, in Figure \ref{fig:appendix_two_scenarios_six_panels} in the Appendix, simple numerical examples of redistributive preferences around the utilitarian benchmark, that is, \(\lambda_i(\theta)=1\). These examples show that the ranking induced by the absolute effect, \(\E_{i}\!\left[\Delta \G_i(\theta)\right]\), and the ranking induced by the proportional effect, \(\E_{i}\!\left[R_{\G,i}(\theta)\right]\), can be non-trivial and may even go in opposite directions. In two cases, we consider pairs of categories \(i\) and \(j\) whose redistributive weights \(\lambda_i(\theta)\) and \(\lambda_j(\theta)\) both deviate from the constant benchmark \(1\), while all other parameters are held fixed. We show that even when category \(i\) has a higher average effective weight relative to the utilitarian benchmark whereas category \(j\) has a lower one, so that \(\E_{i}\!\left[\Delta \G_i(\theta)\right] > 0 > \E_{\theta_j}\!\left[\Delta \G_j(\theta)\right]\), it is still possible to have \(\E_{i}\!\left[R_{\G,i}(\theta)\right] < 0 < \E_{\theta_j}\!\left[R_{\G,j}(\theta)\right]\).

We now derive global implications of Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order} by imposing additional structure on redistributive preferences. The theorem provides a local comparison between nearby categories; the role of the next assumption is to place all categories along a common ordered path, so that these local comparisons can be aggregated into a global ranking.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Order}
\begin{assumption}[Ordered preference path]\label{ass:ordered-path}
For each adjacent pair \(i-1\) and \(i\), there exists a positive \(C^1\) path $(t,\theta)\mapsto \lambda(t,\theta) \text{ on }[0,1]\times\Theta$ such that \(\lambda(0,\theta)=\lambda_{i-1}(\theta)\) and \(\lambda(1,\theta)=\lambda_i(\theta)\). Along this path, two conditions hold for every \(t\in[0,1]\):

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
\item the average redistributive weight is weakly increasing, $\partial_t \tilde\lambda(t)\ge 0$, where $\tilde\lambda(t):=\E_{\theta}[\lambda(t,\theta)]$,
\item the relative tilt of redistributive weights is toward higher types, in the sense that $\partial_t \log \lambda(t,\theta)$ is weakly increasing in \(\theta\) on \(\Theta\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path} orders categories along a common redistributive path. Condition~(i) requires average welfare weights to be weakly increasing along the ordering. Condition~(ii) requires this increase to be proportionally stronger for higher types. Together, these two restrictions allow the local comparison in Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order} to be iterated across adjacent categories. By Appendix Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}, they imply a monotone ordering of effective social weights that carries over to the equilibrium capacity profile.

\begin{corollary}[Capacity ordering along an ordered preference path]\label{cor:ordered-path}
Consider an equilibrium allocation \(\{k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast,\beta_i^\ast\}_{i\in N}\). Under Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path}, the equilibrium capacity profile is globally weakly monotone:
\[
k_1^\ast\le\cdots\le k_n^\ast.
\]
\end{corollary}


The corollary provides a sharp economic ranking of categories by capacity allocation. A higher average social weight \(\tilde\lambda_i\) alone is not enough for a category to receive more capacity: what also matters is how redistributive preferences are tilted across types. Categories that place proportionally more weight on higher types have a larger $\G_i(\theta)$, not only through the average term \(\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i\), but also through the informational-rent component \(\Lambda_i(\theta)\). Under Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path}, these two forces move in the same direction. As a result, categories that are higher in the redistributive ordering optimally receive a larger capacity.

In Figure \ref{fig:appendix_N_categories_six_panels} in the Appendix, we provide a numerical illustration of the sufficient condition. We compute the optimal allocation of \(k_i\) across 50 categories for a given aggregate capacity \(k\). We consider two cases. In both, categories are ordered so that, between two adjacent categories \(i\) and \(i+1\), the average social weight is strictly increasing. In the first case, redistributive preferences are gradually tilted toward higher types, whereas in the second they are gradually tilted toward lower types. For comparability, we assume that, for a given category \(i\), the average social weight is the same across the two cases. We show that, in the second case, capacity may be increasing across some initial categories, but that the ranking eventually reverses. In the first case, which satisfies the condition of the corollary, the optimal allocation \(k_i^\ast\) is clearly strictly increasing.



To rank categories in terms of revenue requirements, we again use the first-order condition from Lemma~\ref{lem:outer}. For a local comparison around category \(i\), this condition implies
\[
0 = \Delta\tilde\lambda + \frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,\Delta k + \frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial I_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,\Delta I.
\]
This expression shows that the comparison of equilibrium revenue requirements across categories depends on two objects: the difference in average social weights, \(\Delta\tilde\lambda\), and the endogenous response of the category-specific shadow cost \(\beta_i\). The within-category analysis provides the sign of the derivative of \(\beta_i\), and therefore yields the following result.

\begin{proposition}[Revenue ordering]\label{prop:revenueordering}
Consider an equilibrium allocation $\{k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast\}_{i\in N}$. Define the two endogenous subsets 
\[
N^+:=\{i\in N:\beta_i^\ast>0\},
\qquad
N^0:=\{i\in N:\beta_i^\ast=0\},
\]
which partition \(N\) into categories that contribute to the budget and categories that do not contribute to the budget (either may be empty). Then:

\noindent\textbf{(i)} At any equilibrium, $\max_{i \in N^+} \tilde\lambda_i < \min_{i \in N^0} \tilde\lambda_i$, so \(N^+\) and \(N^0\) are strictly separated in terms of average welfare weights, regardless of the within-type structure of preferences.

\noindent\textbf{(ii)} Fix a baseline category $i \in N^+$, and let category $j \in N^+$ be obtained from $i$ through a marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences with $\Delta\tilde\lambda:=\tilde\lambda_j-\tilde\lambda_i$ and $\Delta k:=k_j^\ast-k_i^\ast>0$. Then to the first order
\[
I_j^\ast<I_i^\ast \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad -\underbrace{\Bigg.\left(\Delta k\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)+\Delta_{\lambda}\beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\right)\Bigg.}_{\text{revenue effect}}<\underbrace{\Bigg.\Delta\tilde\lambda\Bigg.}_{\text{preference effect}}.
\]
where the sign of $\Delta k \frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$ is determined by the sign of \eqref{Eujmd} and $\Delta_{\lambda}\beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$ is the  first-order effect of the preference perturbation on the $\beta_i$. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA},  and using the first-order expression for $\Delta k$,this reduces to
\[
I_j^\ast<I_i^\ast \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \underbrace{\Bigg.\frac{\E_i[R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)]}{\E_i[R_\theta^2(\theta)]}\Bigg.}_{\Delta k} \underbrace{\Bigg.\E_i[R_\theta(\theta)]\Bigg. }_{\partial_k\beta}+ 
\underbrace{\Bigg.\frac{
\Cov_i\!\left(
R_{\theta}(\theta),R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)
\right)
}{
\Var_i\!\left(
R_{\theta}(\theta)
\right)
}\Bigg.}_{\Delta_\lambda \beta} < \Delta\tilde\lambda.
\]
\end{proposition}

The proposition completes the across-category characterization by describing the revenue side of the allocation. It does so in two steps. First, it identifies which categories contribute to the budget. This extensive margin is entirely governed by average social weights: categories with sufficiently low average redistributive weight are the ones from which the designer extracts revenue, whereas categories with sufficiently high average weight do not contribute.\footnote{Part (i) follows directly from the optimality conditions and requires no additional structure on preferences. The contribute/not-contribute margin is therefore simpler than the ranking of revenue requirements within the contributing set.} Second, conditional on belonging to the contributing set, the proposition ranks equilibrium revenue requirements \(I_i^\ast\). This intensive margin relies on a different mechanism from the capacity ranking. A category may be more favored on average by redistributive preferences without necessarily facing a lower equilibrium revenue requirement once the equilibrium allocation of capacity is taken into account. 

The preference effect term measures the difference in average redistributive concern across the two categories. By contrast, the revenue effect term captures how the two categories differ in their ability to raise revenue. The first component reflects the indirect effect operating through the difference in equilibrium capacity, while the second captures the direct effect of the preference perturbation on the shadow cost of revenue, holding the allocation fixed. The ranking of equilibrium revenue requirements is therefore not pinned down by average preferences alone, but by the interaction between redistributive preferences and the category’s ability to generate revenue. This differs from environments in which the concern for revenue is exogenous where average welfare weights determine both the extensive margin of contribution, and the intensive margin of revenue allocation.

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, this tradeoff becomes especially transparent, because the revenue-ordering condition can be expressed entirely in terms of the same sufficient statistics that govern the previous sorting results. The capacity difference is summarized by marginal capacity value and captured by the proportional change $\E_i[R_\G(\theta)]$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order}). The effect of a larger capacity on the shadow cost of revenue is summarized by the distribution of virtual surplus across types  $\E_i[R_\theta(\theta)]$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus}). Finally, the direct effect of the preference perturbation on the ability to raise revenue is captured by the covariance term, as in Proposition~\ref{prop:surplus-redist}. Under CARA, the revenue ranking is therefore entirely governed by the sorting of consumers along these two objects.

\subsection{Optimal investment}

We now endogenize total investment. The previous sections characterized the optimal within-category allocation and the optimal allocation of capacity and revenue across categories for a given aggregate capacity \(k\). We now let \(k\) itself be a choice variable. The designer solves
\[
\max_{k\geq 0} W(k),
\]
where \(W(k)\) denotes the outer value function once total revenue is tied to the endogenous investment cost \(I(k)\), with \(I\) strictly increasing and convex.

Increasing \(k\) relaxes scarcity in the states in which capacity binds, but it also requires additional revenue to finance the larger investment. The optimal investment level is therefore pinned down by equating the marginal value of relaxing the capacity constraint with the marginal financing cost of additional capacity. As shown in Proposition~\ref{lem:investment}, this condition is governed by the same objects, \(\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\), \(\Pi_i\), and \(\tilde\lambda^\ast\), that organize the within- and across-category analysis.

\begin{lemma}[Optimal investment and the incomplete-information peak-load rule]\label{lem:investment}
The investment problem \(\max_{k\geq 0} W(k)\) admits a unique interior solution \(k^\ast > 0\). The optimal investment level satisfies
\[
I'(k^\ast)
= \frac{1}{\mu^+\tilde\lambda^\ast}\sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\,\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)=
\frac{1}{\mu^+}
\sum_{i\in N^+} \mu_i\,
\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[
u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,
\frac{\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}\,
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}
\right],
\]
where \(\mu^+ := \sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\) denotes the mass of contributing categories and \(\tilde\lambda^\ast := -\zeta_I > 0\) is the equilibrium marginal cost of funds. Moreover,
\[
\max_{i \in N^+} \tilde\lambda_i < \tilde\lambda^\ast \leq \min_{i \in N^0} \tilde\lambda_i.
\]
\end{lemma}

Lemma~\ref{lem:investment} is the incomplete-information counterpart of the classic peak-load investment rule. In the complete-information benchmark with redistributive preferences, incentive constraints play no role. There is no information rent, so \(J_i(\theta)=\theta\) and \(\Lambda_i(\theta)=0\). Hence the normalized incomplete-information object collapses to its complete-information counterpart:
\[
\frac{\mathcal G_i(\theta)}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}
\; \longrightarrow \;
\theta,
\]
which yields equation~\eqref{eq:FB-k1}.

Without redistributive concerns, the social value of a transfer is the same for all consumers, so the distinction between contributing and non-contributing categories disappears, and the marginal utility of consumption is equated with the marginal investment cost. Under incomplete information, by contrast, the relevant marginal value is no longer marginal utility alone. It is the incentive-adjusted object \(u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\mathcal G_i(\theta)\) for contributing categories, which combines the direct surplus gain from relaxing scarcity with the informational distortions embedded in the mechanism.

This stands in contrast with the literature on peak-load pricing under incomplete information but without redistributive concerns. Under utilitarian preferences, incomplete information distorts welfare away from the complete-information benchmark when investment is off the optimum, but this distortion disappears at the optimal investment level \(k^\ast\) (see XXXX). In our framework, by contrast, the gap with complete information remains even at the optimum.

In Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_OptimalFBSB} in the Appendix, we provide a numerical illustration of welfare \(W(k)\) under four scenarios: utilitarian versus redistributive preferences, and complete versus incomplete information, holding the primitives fixed. The figure shows that, under redistributive preferences, the complete- and incomplete-information welfare profiles differ even at the optimum. By contrast, under utilitarian preferences, the two profiles coincide at the optimal investment level \(k^\ast\).


Using the first-order condition from Lemma~\ref{lem:investment}, we conclude by characterizing how redistributive preference perturbations affect both the optimal investment level and the incidence of a global capacity expansion across categories.

\begin{proposition}[Investment response to redistributive preference perturbations]\label{prop:investment}
Assume Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}. Suppose each category \(i\) becomes a new category \(j\) through a marginal and monotonic mean-preserving perturbation of \(\lambda_i(\theta)\), and let \(\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast\) denote the first-order change in the equilibrium marginal cost of funds. Then the induced change in optimal investment satisfies
\[
W''(k^\ast)\,\Delta k^\ast
=
-\underbrace{\Bigg.\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i\,\Pi_i\,\mathbb E_i[R_{\mathcal G,i}]\Bigg.}_{\text{average effect}}
\qquad +  \qquad \underbrace{ \Bigg.I'(k^\ast)\,\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast\Bigg.}_{\text{sorting effect}}
\]
where
\[
\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast
=
-
\frac{
\sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\,\overline\varepsilon_i\,
\Cov_i\!\left(
R_{\theta,i}(\theta),R_{\mathcal G,i}(\theta)
\right)
}{
\sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\,\overline\varepsilon_i\,
\Var_i\!\left(
R_{\theta,i}(\theta)
\right)
}.
\]
Here \(W''(k^\ast)<0\) and \(\overline\varepsilon_i \geq 0\).
\end{proposition}

The proposition shows that the investment response depends on two distinct forces. The first term captures the average effect of redistributive preferences on the marginal value of capacity, while the second term captures a sorting effect through the induced change in the equilibrium marginal cost of funds. For optimal investment to increase, the preference perturbation must therefore not only raise scarcity rents on average, but also must align with the distribution of marginal virtual utility $u(q_i(\theta,s),s)J_i(\theta)$ across types within all contributing categories.

A preference perturbation affects the optimal investment level through two channels. First, it changes the equilibrium marginal cost of funds \(\tilde\lambda^\ast\). As in the within-category analysis, the sign of this effect is governed by the covariance between the redistributive shift and the type profile (Proposition~\ref{prop:surplus-redist}). Second, the perturbation changes the category-level marginal value of capacity, summarized by \(\Pi_i\). Within categories, redistributive perturbations reshape allocations through the effective-weight object \(R_{\mathcal G,i}\); across categories, they alter the ranking of scarcity rents and therefore the optimal distribution of capacity (Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order}). At the aggregate level, these two effects jointly determine whether the designer increases or decreases total investment.

We illustrate this result in Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_exampleoptimallevel} in the Appendix, where we simulate how the optimal investment level \(k^\ast\) responds to redistributive preference changes. We start from a benchmark with two utilitarian categories, \(i\) and \(i+1\). We then iteratively perturb preferences and recompute the optimal investment level for each successive pair of categories: in the first step, category \(i\) remains utilitarian while category \(i+1\) is a marginal perturbation of \(i\); in the next step, category \(i+1\) becomes the new baseline and category \(i+2\) is constructed as a perturbation of it; and so on. We consider two cases. In the first, redistributive preferences are increasingly tilted toward higher types; in the second, they are increasingly tilted toward lower types. In the numerical examples we consider, the sorting and average effects move in the same direction under monotone mean-preserving perturbations, producing a clear opposite shift in optimal investment.

Finally, we connect a marginal increase in aggregate capacity \(k\) to its redistributive incidence across categories and types. While we do not provide a formal characterization here, the previous results already identify the two objects that govern this effect: the within-category incidence of \(k_i\), and the response of equilibrium category capacities \(k_i^\ast\) to a change in aggregate capacity \(k\). This yields a simple condition that captures the main economic mechanism at work. Consider a neighborhood of an interior optimum in which the set of active and contributing categories remains unchanged. Then
\[
\frac{d\,\E CS_i(\theta)}{dk}
=
\frac{\partial \E CS_i(\theta)}{\partial k_i}\,
\frac{dk_i^\ast}{dk}.
\]
The first term is the within-category incidence characterized in Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus}. The second term captures how a marginal increase in aggregate capacity is redistributed across categories. As shown in Appendix Lemma~\ref{lem:dkidk}, this term depends on two objects. The first is the category-specific expression
\[
\Omega_i^{-1}\left(
\mathbb \E_{(s,i)}^w[uJ\mid s]
-I'(k^\ast)
\right),
\]
which measures whether the marginal revenue \eqref{Eujmd} for category \(i\) is high or low relative to the marginal investment cost. The second is the corresponding aggregate expression,
\[\tag{$d\lambda^\ast$}\label{dlambda}
\sum_{j\in N } \mu_j\,\Omega_j^{-1}
\left(
\E_{(s,j)}^w[uJ\mid s]
-I'(k^\ast)
\right),
\]
which governs how the equilibrium marginal cost of funds \(\tilde\lambda^\ast\) responds to a change in aggregate capacity.\footnote{In this sense, the aggregate response mirrors the previous proposition on \(\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast\): what matters, again, is how the marginal revenue compares with the relevant aggregate benchmark.}

This yields a simple interpretation of the redistributive effect of a global capacity expansion. The allocation of additional capacity depends on both the category-level comparison between \eqref{Eujmd} and \(I'(k^\ast)\), and the corresponding aggregate object \eqref{dlambda}. The sign of \eqref{dlambda} determines the direction of the reallocation: if \eqref{dlambda} is positive, categories with relatively high marginal revenue receive more of the expansion; if \eqref{dlambda} is negative, categories with relatively low marginal revenue receive more. Moreover, as shown in the Appendix and used in Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus}, \eqref{Eujmd} moves monotonically with \(k_i\): depending on \(R_\theta\) and \(R_q\), it may switch from positive to negative, or from negative to positive. This delivers a sharp qualitative prediction based only on these two ratios and on the sign of \eqref{dlambda}.

To illustrate, take two categories \(i\) and \(j\), and assume \(R'_\theta(\theta)>0\), so that in both categories the virtual term \(u(q_i(\theta,s),s)J_i(\theta)\) is increasing in \(\theta\). Suppose that, at the initial allocation, \(k_j^\ast \gg k_i^\ast\), and that \eqref{Eujmd} is positive for the low-capacity category \(i\) but negative for the high-capacity category \(j\). If \eqref{dlambda} is positive, the global expansion reallocates relatively more capacity toward category \(i\), compressing category-level allocations and generating convergence. If \eqref{dlambda} is negative, the opposite occurs: the expansion reallocates relatively more capacity toward category \(j\), widening category-level allocations and generating divergence. Figure~\ref{fig:general_surplus} illustrates this mechanism.

\subsection{Complete Information Benchmark}\label{chap3sec:sec3}

Under complete information, the market designer observes each consumer's category $i$ and type $\theta$, so allocation rules and transfers can condition directly on true types. Since there are no incentive constraints, redistributive preferences enter the allocation problem through the pointwise weights $\lambda_i(\theta)$. Relative to the standard peak-load benchmark, this changes both the optimal marginal condition and the way revenue is extracted.

Let $S^*=[\underline{s},s^*)$ and $T^*=[s^*,\overline{s}]$ denote the off-peak and on-peak regions, where $s^*$ is the unique threshold such that
\[
\sum_i \mu_i\,\E_{i}\!\left[q_i^*(\theta,s^*)\right]=k.
\]
Define $\lambda^*$ as the marginal welfare weight such that extracting full surplus from all types with $\lambda_i(\theta)\leq \lambda^*$ is just sufficient to finance investment. The section~\ref{appsec:CI} in the Appendix gives the formal construction and the associated cutoff types.

\begin{proposition}[Complete-information allocation with redistribution]\label{prop:FBalloc}
The optimal allocation is characterized by a unique quantity schedule $q_i^*(\theta,s)$ satisfying
\[\tag{FOC$_S^{fb}$}\label{FB-S}
\theta\,u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)=0,
\qquad s\in S^*,
\]
and
\[\tag{FOC$_T^{fb}$}\label{FB-T}
\begin{aligned}   
& u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\,\beta \theta= \varepsilon(s), & \text{if } \lambda_i(\theta)\leq \lambda^*,\\[6pt]
& u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\,\lambda_i(\theta)\theta=\varepsilon(s), & \text{if } \lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*,
\end{aligned}
\qquad s\in T^*.
\]
Transfers are independent of $s$ and satisfy $t_i^*(\theta)=\E_s\!\left[\theta\,U\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\right]$ for strictly contributing types $\lambda_i(\theta)<\lambda^*$, $t_i^*(\theta)=0$ for non-contributing types $\lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*$, and the participation constraint may be slack only at the marginal type $\lambda_i(\theta)=\lambda^*$.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:FBalloc} isolates the two dimensions along which redistribution departs from the traditional peak-load benchmark. First, it changes the planner's marginal condition. Off-peak, the allocation remains efficient.\footnote{We abstract from marginal production costs in order to isolate the interaction between scarcity and redistribution. This comes at the cost of shutting down an additional pricing margin: with positive marginal cost, the planner would also trade off allocative efficiency in non-scarcity states. Incorporating that margin would extend the standard analysis without changing the main mechanism emphasized here, namely how redistribution alters the scarcity component of peak-load pricing. Moreover, since the relevant objects vary continuously across states, no additional boundary term arises at the transition between off-peak and on-peak states.} On-peak, however, the marginal condition becomes type-specific for non-contributing consumers.  Without redistributive concerns, the optimal allocation is simply $\theta\,u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)=\varepsilon(s)$.

Therefore, a uniform spot price fails to implement the first best whenever the non-contributing set has positive mass, since the type-specific marginal condition cannot be replicated by a single price. Among contributing types alone, however, a spot price implements the optimal allocation, as they all face the same marginal condition.


The complete-information benchmark highlights two features that disappear under private information. First, with observable types, the planner can separate contributors from non-contributors directly, so redistribution operates through a sharp partition of consumers. Under incomplete information, this within-type partition collapses: IC constraints force contribution to be category-based, so if a category is required to raise revenue, every type within it bears the contribution. Second, under complete information redistribution and revenue extraction are largely separable, whereas under incomplete information they conflict directly because changing the allocation of one type affects the information rents of others. As a result, the key question is no longer only who contributes, but how capacity and redistributive weights are sorted across the type distribution, which generates non-trivial winners and losers from both capacity expansions and preference shifts.\footnote{Additional details on the investment margin under complete information are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:CI}.}
