\begin{abstract}
\noindent This paper examines the challenges of allocating a good subject to capacity constraints such as electricity when considering consumer preferences and investment decisions. A theoretical framework is developed where a market designer sequentially chooses a level of investment and proposes an allocation mechanism to consumers followed by a consumption stage. The market designer uses the allocation to maximize consumer surplus and finance the investment cost. He faces heterogeneous consumers who have private information about their demand level and belong to a publicly observed category, allowing the market designer to distinguish groups of consumers such as households or industries. We show that the optimal allocation implies discriminating against consumers based on their types and categories and that the relative discrimination depends on the level of investment considered. It has significant welfare and distributive implications: an optimal pricing mechanism can minimize the investment cost and lead to a higher aggregate consumer surplus. However, it is not always a Pareto improvement for every consumer, especially for smaller ones. We describe two main environments: the current second-best situation, in which the market designer cannot obtain information about consumers and must choose fixed prices ex-ante, and the optimal theoretical second-best allocation mechanism that considers the incentive and individual rationality constraints and the investment decisions.

\section{Introduction}\label{chap3sec:sec1}

Economists have long advocated that pricing mechanisms should be carefully designed to allow the coverage of investment costs and promote efficient resource use. This is particularly true when providing \textit{essential goods} characterized by the public-good nature of investment availability when supply is scarce, such as electricity, public transport, or medical goods. In those sectors, demand and supply fluctuate unpredictably, and if any demand exceeds the available capacity and cannot be efficiently rationed, it generates significant welfare losses.
For instance, without sufficient investment, the reliability of the electricity supply can be compromised, leading to frequent outages and power interruptions \citep{IEA2020}. It is particularly important in the energy transition context. Indeed, it is crucial to lower the production from fossil fuels but reliable technology and invest massively in carbon-free but intermittent renewables. Moreover, the electrification of end-use consumption also implies that periods of scarcity may occur more often. Therefore, we must carefully design electricity markets by choosing the most efficient pricing mechanisms, allowing for sufficient investment and ensuring demand reacts to scarcity \citep{IEA2021}.
The COVID crisis has also shown that the lack of production capacity for medical goods, especially vaccines, has severe consequences. The absence of sufficient capacity to produce vaccines led to a worldwide lockdown and border closures, increasing contagions and hospital congestion.
Finally, congestion in transportation systems continues to generate substantial costs \citep{schrank2021} and poses challenges for the much-needed modal shift to low-carbon means \citep{ITDP2021}.

In this paper, we provide a framework highlighting the inherent tensions that arise when implementing an allocation mechanism that dictates how agents consume the goods, and generates revenue to finance new investments in an incomplete information framework with heterogeneous consumers.  Numerous theoretical contributions have been made to understand the importance of having sufficient investment.\footnote{Namely, how to implement mechanisms to procure sufficient investment at the least cost and consider the private incentives producers face, which may differ from the optimum. Those mechanisms can range from direct subsidies to the design of more complex competitive markets. An important stream of literature in electricity markets is focused on studying long-term markets in which producers offer either future production via long-term contracts \citep{ausubel2010using} or their future availability through, for instance, capacity remuneration mechanisms \citep{leautier2016visible,holmberg2020optimal}.} Nonetheless, they are mainly centered around the supply-side of the problem and consider the demand as given. Therefore, the first contribution of this paper is to discuss the implications of considering the demand-side when it comes to ensuring an efficient level of investment. We provide a model to analyze the interaction between a set of heterogeneous consumers, the choice of the pricing mechanism, and the use of the revenue generated through this mechanism to increase available capacity. The use of consumer heterogeneity allows us to raise the issues of the redistribution generated by an allocation mechanism that is considered more efficient. We find that reaching a certain level of investment that maximizes aggregate consumer surplus and implementing the corresponding allocation mechanism to finance the investment can lead to different welfare levels depending on the consumer types. This means the most efficient mechanism is not always Pareto-improving for every consumer, even considering an increase in available capacity.\footnote{For clarity, we do not discuss in this current paper version the optimal level of investment. While this provides both technical and policy results, it mainly boils down to comparing the different, and sometimes opposite, effects that are described in this paper.}

The second contribution lies in the assumption that the utility buyers derive from consuming the goods is private information. The consumers are characterized by a linear marginal utility function, which is uncertain when the market designer makes investment decisions and proposes an allocation mechanism. This uncertainty has two additive components: (i) a common shock that is identical across all consumers, and (ii) a private shock only observed by consumers before the consumption stage and the realization of the common shock. We also embedded each consumer with a category for which the market designer is publicly informed.\footnote{The support of the distribution of a consumer type depends on the category to which he belongs.} The existence of private information with respect to their consumption implies that consumers' private incentives might also differ from the market designer's objective. Therefore, the allocation mechanism in the framework can be used simultaneously to generate revenue to cover investment costs and screen for unobservable characteristics to ensure efficient consumption. 

The supply side is represented by a market designer, which can be interpreted as a public authority or a regulated monopoly, that (i) determines the allocation in prices and quantities of a homogeneous good and (ii) chooses the level of investment that maximizes consumer surplus. The allocation mechanism defines the monetary transfer and the quantity for a set of consumers during the consumption stage subject to capacity constraint. Therefore, when the market designer chooses the mechanism, he must consider that demand may exceed the level of available capacity and that specific actions need to be taken to reduce aggregate consumption. This creates an asymmetric effect of the optimal allocation when the capacity is binding or not. Hence, the consideration of the capacity constraint significantly impacts the design of the efficient mechanism and the revenue generated by the mechanism.\footnote{In this paper, the capacity constraint is hard in the sense that we do not represent the costs associated with demand exceeding available supply. Therefore, the market designer can always reduce demand but at the cost of misallocation due to imperfect information. Several papers have described those costs in more detail, such as rolling blackouts in electricity \citep{fabra2018primer,llobet2018conventional} or congestion costs in transports \citep{YOSHIDA2008228,DEPALMA2017106}.}  We also describe the (potential) incompleteness of the mechanism proposed by the market designer due to implementation constraints.\footnote{We mean by implementation constraints an environment in which the market designer cannot set the optimal allocation for every demand realization.} Finally, note that as the market designer uses the allocation to maximize consumer surplus and finance the investment cost, he is also under a revenue constraint. 

We analyze several market design environments to highlight the range of mechanisms at play in this framework. In section XXXX, we start with the first-best, in which the market designer perfectly observes the consumer type when choosing the investment level and the allocation. Including heterogeneity in the canonical model does not alter the fundamentals of the pricing decision: the optimal mechanism can be directly implemented by a spot market, with a unique (unitary) monetary transfer from each consumer. Section XXXX analyzes the \textit{current} second-best implemented across many markets. The market designer faces private information about the level of consumption and is constrained in the monetary transfer he can implement. Namely, the price is unique for every state of the world, and it can vary based on the category of consumers. One of the main results is the non-monotonous relation between the price of the smaller consumers' category and the level of investment. For small levels of investment, the price decreases in the level of investment, and then beyond a specific value, it increases similarly to the price of the biggers' category. The cause lies in the relation between the market designer's objective and the constraints he faces. Increasing the level of investment changes the capacity constraint. In turn, it modifies the utility marginal rates of substitution between the different categories of consumers in favor of the category of smaller consumers. This effect fades away for high levels of investment as the gain from a marginal increase in the level of investment is lower. On the other hand, the financing cost is convex in the investment level; that is, the budget constraint is increasingly tightening. It explains why, for small levels of investment, the consumer surplus effect dominates, while the revenue effect dominates for high levels. Finally, in sectionXXX, we look at the theoretical second-best case under incomplete information and we implement a mechanism design approach. In that environment, the market designer proposes an individual contract that needs to satisfy the incentive compatibility constraint in addition to the previous ones. The main results are that the market designer discriminates against consumers based on their type and it can be observed both in terms of quantity and individual welfare. For consumers with smaller consumption, an increase in the level of investment can lead to a decrease in the allocated quantity. On the other hand, only sufficiently big consumers have both an increase in quantity and in their welfare when the level of investment increases. The relation between the quantity allocated and the level of investment is based on the interaction between the revenue and consumer surplus effect described in Section XXXX and the consumers' virtual marginal utility, which takes into account the cost associated with truthful behavior. For a given consumer type, the individual welfare effect is based on the information rent, which itself relies on the quantity allocated to smaller consumers. The remainder of this section discusses the related literature. Section \ref{chap3sec:sec2} presents the environment.

\textbf{Related Literature}

We build the framework on several strands of literature. The dynamic interaction between investment decisions and the consumption stages stems from the \textit{peak-load pricing theory} that originated from \cite{m1949tarification}. It describes how capacity constraints interact with the provisions of a homogeneous good with time-varying uncertain stochastic demand.  It has mainly been used in recent work to study the role of market power, as in \cite{leautier2016visible}, where producers can increase the price on the spot market beyond marginal cost even though they are not capacity-constrained. The effect of price regulation is also analyzed in \cite{leautier2018long}, where the author demonstrates that short-term inefficiencies can sometimes have long-term and counterintuitive effects. In this paper, the price cap changes the private incentives producers face, hence the final investment decisions. \cite{holmberg2020optimal} study the effect of having inefficient rationing. Consequently, electricity prices do not internalize this additional cost, and the market designer needs to implement an additional stream of revenue for the producers. This work introduces two features in the model: (i) heterogeneous consumers with private information and (ii) inefficiencies due to the schedule commitment by the market designer before the uncertainty is resolved.\footnote{This work mirrors the literature from congestion pricing theory from \cite{vickrey1963pricing,vickrey1969congestion}. For a recent theoretical paper, see, for instance, \cite{DEPALMA2017106}, which also compares different allocation mechanisms but without considering the demand-side.}

This paper is also based on a second stream of papers that is related to the electricity markets and is based on the seminal paper by \cite{chao1987priority} on priority service. The central idea is to provide a mechanism design solution in the form of a contractual arrangement where consumers choose the allocation during the wholesale market at the same time. It is in the same vein as the allocation schedule of this paper and the probability of being disconnected when demand exceeds the level of capacity. This framework has been refined by a series of papers by the same authors, including the comparison with other market arrangements \citep{chao2022priority} and the role of risk aversion \citep{chao2012competitive}. We also relate to a series of papers focusing on implementing the second-best pricing method for consumers with incomplete information in \cite{spulber1992capacity,spulber1992optimal,spulber1993monopoly}. The work in \cite{spulber1992optimal} focuses on an incomplete information framework without endogenous investment decisions. The optimal allocation schedule is non-linear because consumers' type is private information. Therefore, the market designer faces some challenges when implementing such schedules. In \cite{spulber1992capacity}, a regulated firm is introduced to consider its revenue constraint. However, the focus of this paper remains circumscribed to the design of consumers' second-best tariffs. Finally, \cite{spulber1993monopoly} studies the case of a monopoly designing the rates under incomplete information. We depart from this literature by deepening the private incentives consumers might have by behaving strategically from the truthful reporting and by tightening the link with the investment decisions framework developed in the previous paragraph.

Most of the recent empirical works on redistribution study the short-term effect of pricing issues without considering the long-term interactions with the level of investment. This issue has been recently studied in several empirical papers in the context of essential goods. For instance, in electricity markets, \cite{cahana2022distributional} explore the redistributive effects of switching from a flat electricity price to real-time pricing. Depending on the design, low-income households may lose due to specific consumption patterns in the face of available supply.\footnote{\cite{levinson2022electric} have studied the rates implemented by utilities in the U.S. and how they take into account redistribution preferences in their design. Due to the rapid increase in residential rooftop solar photovoltaic, electricity network tariffs have also been studied, notably in the Californian markets. If the tariffs are mostly based on variable parts, then non-adopters tend to cross-subsidies adopters of such technologies. One central issue is that the latter are mostly high-income households \citep{burger2020quantifying}.} Concerning medical goods, the scarcity of vaccines creates a trade-off between protecting the most vulnerable (e.g., elderly), the likely spreader (e.g., students), or the individual bringing the highest economic benefits (e.g., front-line health workers).
Finally, in the case of congestion pricing, \cite{hall2021can} studies the pricing of a lane portion. Using survey and travel time data, the author finds that a fully efficient toll is unnecessary for sufficient welfare and Pareto improvement. 

From a theoretical perspective, the issues related to distributive concerns are borrowed from a growing body of literature using mechanism design.
In particular, \cite{akbarpour2023economic} and \cite{akbarpour2023redistributive} provide a framework with consumers' characteristics, such as the private information and the publicly-observed categories, in line with the current paper. They study the trade-off between allocating certain vaccines on a free but random basis or using prices to discriminate and extract information from consumers. The authors assume that the market designer has distributive and exogenous revenue preferences. Therefore, the model exhibits a tension of allocating the good via prices, which generates some revenue, or via a random free allocation that minimizes distributive issues. This paper endogenizes the revenue preference by implementing investment decisions with a revenue constraint. We also provide results when the market designer can imperfectly implement prices. Finally, a recent paper by \cite{crampespricing} studies the implementation of an optimal Pareto income tax schedule \textit{à la Mirrlees} when considering the interaction between consuming energy services (heating, air conditioning, light) and investing in energy efficiency with incomplete information about consumer utility. This paper has a similar spirit, but the link between consumption and production capacity fundamentally differs.


\section{Environment}\label{chap3sec:sec2}

Although the terminology is tailored to electricity, the results can, with some modifications, be applied to other essential goods, as noted in the introduction. 

Our framework consists of five elements: 
(i) the demand side, which may represent households, industrial consumers, or retailers; 
(ii) the allocation mechanism, which specifies how the market designer allocates electricity in terms of both quantity and monetary transfer; 
(iii) the supply side, which captures investment and production decisions;
(iv) the objective function of the market designer; and 
(v) the timing of decisions.

\noindent \textbf{Consumers Preferences} 

\noindent There is a unit mass of consumers for electricity. Each consumer is characterized by a type vector $(i,\theta,s,\lambda)$. We assume that the three dimensions of the vector $(i,\theta,\lambda)$ have a joint distribution among consumers and are known by the market designer. The first characteristic refers to the consumer category, e.g., household or industry. There is a finite set of categories such that $i \in N = \{1,...,n\}$. It is publicly observed, and the size of each category, i.e., the number of agents, is denoted by $\mu_i>0$ for each group. The second characteristic is consumer private preference $\theta$, which, under an incomplete information framework, is assumed to be observed only by the consumer. Conditional on belonging to a category $i$, this value is drawn from a common-knowledge cumulative distribution function $G_i$ whose continuous density is $g_i>0$ has full support on $\Theta_i$ and is strictly positive on $[\ubar{\theta}_i, \overline{\theta}_i]$. With households, $\theta$ represents the revenue shocks, the lowest type of consumer being the poor household, and the highest type of consumer being the more prosperous household. Industrial consumers could also be modeled with this framework, where $\theta$ represents their buyers' orders. The characteristic $s \in S = [\underline{s},\overline{s}]$ represents the state of the world and introduces demand uncertainty. We assume that all consumers are subject to the same shock $s$ and that the market designer's redistributive preferences do not depend on the realization of $s$. It is common knowledge among all agents. Economically, $s$ can capture, for example, a weather condition or a macroeconomic factor. The shock is drawn from a continuous distribution $F$ with density $f>0$, both of which are common knowledge before the shock is realized, and has full support on $S$. Each consumer type is known before the demand shocks are realized. Therefore, this framework encompasses two interpretations of the demand shocks: (i) a static model with a single shock and there is uncertainty concerning its realization, or a (ii) a repetition of multiple shocks over a given period (for example, one year), which are drawn from the distribution $F(.)$ \citep{leautier2016visible}. In the last interpretation, we assume that the consumer type does not change across shocks and is determined before this period. Finally, $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}_+$ are the market designer welfare weights for a given individual. As for the type $\theta$, we assume that this information is private and unobserved by the market designer. All agents in the game are considered to be risk-neutral.

We define a consumer's utility belonging to a category $i$ of a type $\theta_i$. The value for electricity consumption for each consumer is denoted: 
%##########################################
\begin{equation*}
    \theta \; U(q,s) = \theta\int_0^{q} u(\tilde{q},s) d\tilde{q}
\end{equation*}
%##########################################
With $q$ the quantity of electricity allocated to the consumer. If a consumer does not receive electricity, its value is null: $U(0,s) = 0$. $u$ is the marginal willingness to pay for a given quantity of electricity. $u$ specification satisfies the Spence-Mirrlees condition that marginal willingness to pay is strictly increasing in type: higher types prefer larger allocations for any given quantity. All consumers should be allocated at least some quantity $\lim_{q\downarrow0}\theta \; U(q,s)= \infty$, that higher $s$ leads to higher willingness to pay and the marginal utility is decreasing in $q$: $u_{\theta}>0,u_{s}>0,u_{q}<0$.  A consumer receives a quantity $q$ in exchange for a monetary transfer $t$. We assume transfers are separable, and there are no income effects. Each consumer participates in the market if its expected surplus is nonnegative;
%##########################################
\[
\; \E_{s}\!\big[\,\theta \; U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\,\big]
\;\ge\; 0,
\qquad \forall\, \theta\in\Theta_i. \tag{IR}\label{IR-def}
\]
%##########################################

One key element in the model is the quantity responsiveness of a consumer of type $\theta$ at allocation $q$ in state $s$; defined as $R_q(q) := -u(q,s)/u_q(q,s) > 0$. It measures how much a marginal increase in $q$ raises willingness to pay relative to its rate of decline. Two special cases simplify the analysis by restricting which arguments $R_q$ depends on.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{CARA}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:CARA}
$u(q,s) = v(s)\exp(-\alpha q)$ for some $\alpha > 0$ and some function $v(s) > 0$.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, $R_q = 1/\alpha$ is constant: a consumer's responsiveness is identical regardless of the quantity allocated to her. Additional capacity is therefore distributed across types in proportions determined solely by social weights, and the behavioral channel through which a preference shift distorts the allocation across types vanishes.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Multi}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:mult}
$u(q,s) = \kappa(q)\zeta(s)$ for some functions $\kappa$ and $\zeta$ with $\kappa' < 0$ and $\zeta > 0$.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:mult}, $R_q = -\kappa(q)/\kappa'(q)$ depends only on $q$ and not on $s$: a consumer's responsiveness is identical regardless of the realization of the common shock. Note that Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA} implies Assumption~\ref{ass:mult}, with $\kappa(q) = \exp(-\alpha q)$ and $\zeta(s) = v(s)$, so CARA is the leading example of both simultaneously.

\noindent \textbf{Allocation Design} 

\noindent We derive different mechanisms to allocate electricity. We distinguish between a \textit{general mechanism} and some \textit{market mechanisms}. In the former, the market designer independently selects the quantity and the monetary transfer, which serves as our benchmark allocation. In the latter, the market designer offers a transfer schedule, and consumers choose their preferred quantity. We now define these formally.

\noindent \textbf{General Mechanism}. Given a total quantity $Q(s)$ of electricity in state $s$, a general allocation mechanism $\mathcal{M}$  consists of collection of functions $q_i: [\ubar{\theta}_i, \overline{\theta}_i]\times S \rightarrow [0,Q(s)] $ where $q_i(\theta,s)$ is the quantity of electricity allocated to a consumer of type $\theta$ in category $i$ at state $s$. The aggregate quantity allocated to a group  $i$ is
%##########################################
\begin{equation*}
    Q_i(s) = \mu_i \E_{\theta_i} [q_i(\theta,s)].
\end{equation*}
%##########################################
Feasibility requires that $Q(s) = \sum_{i} Q_i(s)$. Transfers are described by the functions $t_i(\theta,s)$ assigning a monetary payment to each consumer of type $\theta$ in category $i$ at state $s$. To study the optimal second-best mechanism with incomplete information in section \ref{chap3sec:sec5}, we rely on the Revelation Principle. In a direct mechanism $(q_i,t_i)_{i = \{1,2\}}$, consumers report their type $\theta$, receive an allocation $q_i(\theta,s)$, and pays $t_i(\theta,s)$ to the market designer. 

\noindent \textbf{Market mechanism}. In addition to the general mechanism, we define a \textit{market allocation}, which corresponds to an indirect mechanism. Given a transfer schedule $t(q,\theta,s)$, a consumer chooses a quantity schedule $d(\theta,s)$ as the solution to
%##########################################
\[
d(\theta,s) := \arg\max_q \{ \theta \; U(q,s) - t(q,\theta,s) \}.
\]
%##########################################
Hence, a market allocation is one in which consumers optimally select their allocation given the schedule offered by the designer. We use the notation $d(\theta,s)$ to distinguish this individual demand function from the direct allocation rule $q(\theta,s)$. The comparison between the mechanism design and the market allocation will become handy for different reasons. First, market allocations are the primary mechanism used to allocate electricity in practice. They encompass different pricing schemes, such as spot pricing (where $t(q,\theta,s)$ is a unit price resulting from a market-clearing condition) and two-part tariffs (where $t(q,\theta,s)$ consists of a unit price and a lump-sum transfer). Second, having the general mechanism as a benchmark allows for comparing various forms of market allocations, particularly in terms of their implications for consumer surplus. 

\noindent \textbf{Supply side} 

\noindent We assume the most straightforward formulation for the supply-side represented by a single agent, the market designer. It can be interpreted in several ways: as a public firm, as a regulator directly controlling a private monopoly, or as an intermediary between consumers and producers. In the latter case, the mechanism between consumers and the designer can be viewed as a theoretical retail market, while the mechanism between producers and the designer corresponds to a wholesale market. We remain agnostic about the institutional form of this interaction. Still, we assume it is fully efficient: production and investment decisions are identical to those that would be chosen under optimal regulation (for instance, if the market designer acts as a single buyer facing perfectly competitive producers). 

For tractability, we model the market designer as directly choosing production and investment. The level of investment is denoted by $k_i$. We assume an increasing and convex investment cost $I(k)$ and a unit marginal production cost normalized to 0. We require that the profit of the market designer $\Pi$ is nonnegative
%##########################################
\[
\sum_{i\in N}\mu_i
\E_{(s,\theta_i)}\left[ t_i(\theta,s) \right]\;-\; I(k)
\;\ge\; 0. \tag{R}\label{R-def}
\]
%##########################################
The investment level $k_i$ determines capacity, which imposes the feasibility constraint
%##########################################
\[
Q(s) \leq k \quad \quad \text{for each\;} s.\label{K-def}\tag{K}
\]
%##########################################
where $Q(s)$ is the total quantity allocated in state $s$.

The market designer maximizes a weighted expected sum of consumer surpluses across types and states. We define the expectation of $\lambda$ of an individual conditional of its type $\theta$ and belonging to a category $i$ as $\lambda_i(\theta) = \E_{\theta_i}[\lambda|i,\theta]$. Those Pareto weights are assumed to be continuous in $\theta$ for each category $i$. Let $\tilde\lambda_i:= \E_{\theta_i}[\lambda_i]$ be the average weight for a category $i$. The market designer's objective function is
%##########################################
\begin{equation*}
    \text{Obj} := \;  \sum_{i}  \; \mu_i\; \E_{(s,\theta_i)} \left[\; \lambda_i(\theta)\; ( \; \theta \; U(q_i(\theta,s),s)  -  t_i(\theta,s)\; )\; \right]
\end{equation*}
%##########################################
This formulation of the objective function has several limitations as described in \cite{akbarpour2023redistributive}. XXXX How to endogenoize welfare weights.

\noindent \textbf{Timing} 

\noindent We consider a multi-period game with four stages:  

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Information stage.} Consumers privately learn their type $\theta$. Under complete information, the market designer also observes types; under incomplete information, the designer only knows their distribution. 
    \item \textbf{Investment decision.} The market designer chooses the level of investment $k_i$. 
    \item \textbf{Allocation proposal.} 
    \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
        \item The market designer proposes an allocation schedule $\mathcal{M}$ (which may be market-based or mechanism-based). The schedule can be fully contingent on the realization of $s$ or restricted if some constraints prevent complete state-contingency.  
        \item Consumers then accept or reject the proposal. If a consumer rejects the offers, we assume no electricity consumption, and we normalize the outside utility to zero. 
    \end{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Short-term allocation.} 
    \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
        \item The common shock $s$ is realized and observed by all agents. 
        \item Allocations are implemented according to the offer made in Stage 3. 
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\section{Complete Information Benchmark}\label{chap3sec:sec3}

The first regime we study is the complete information case: the market designer observes each consumer's category $i$ and type $\theta$, so the individual welfare weight $\lambda_i(\theta)$ is known, and allocation rules can condition on true types without incentive issues. The category label $i$ remains relevant as it indexes the population mass $\mu_i$ and the type distribution $G_i(\theta)$, but the average weight $\tilde{\lambda}_i$ plays no allocative role: what matters for redistribution is the pointwise value $\lambda_i(\theta)$. The market designer solves
%##########################################
\[
\tag{P$^*$}\label{P-CI}
\max_{\substack{ t_i(\theta,s) \geq 0, \\ q_i(\theta,s) \geq 0}} \quad \sum_{i} \mu_i \; \E_{(s,\theta_i)} \left[\lambda_i(\theta)\;(\theta \; U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s))\right]
\]
%##########################################
subject to the budget constraint \eqref{R-def}, the capacity constraint \eqref{K-def}, and the participation constraint \eqref{IR-def}. With multipliers $\varepsilon(s) \geq 0$, $\beta\geq0$, $\rho_i(\theta)\geq0$, and $\phi_i(\theta,s)\geq 0$ on the capacity, budget, participation, and transfer-positivity constraints respectively. 

Let $S^*=[\underline{s},s^*)$ and $T^*=[s^*,\overline{s}]$ be the off-peak and on-peak sets, where $s^*$ is the unique threshold at which $\sum_{i} \mu_i \E_{\theta_i} [q_i^*(\theta,s^*)] = k$. Define the marginal welfare weight $\lambda^*$ as the lowest weight such that the transfers collected from all types with $\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*$ suffice to cover the investment cost $I(k)$. As shown in Proposition~\ref{prop:FBalloc}, the participation constraint \eqref{IR-def} binds for all strictly contributing types, so their transfer equals their expected gross utility and the condition reduces to:
%##########################################
\[
\sum_i \mu_i \E_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[\theta\, U(q_i^*(\theta,s),s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\lambda_i(\theta) < \lambda^*}\right] < I(k) \leq \sum_i \mu_i \E_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[\theta\, U(q_i^*(\theta,s),s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\lambda_i(\theta) \leq \lambda^*}\right].
\]
For each category $i$, whenever the equation $\lambda_i(\theta)=\lambda^\ast$ admits a solution, let $\theta_i^\ast(k)$ denote the corresponding cutoff type.
%Under the maintained assumption that $\lambda_i(\theta)$ is monotone, this cutoff is unique whenever it exists.
%##########################################

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:FBalloc}
The optimal allocation is characterized by a unique quantity schedule $q_i^*(\theta,s)$:
%##########################################
\[
\label{eq:FB-S}\tag{FB-S}
\theta\, u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)=0, \qquad s\in S^{\ast},
\]
%##########################################
%##########################################
\[
\label{eq:FB-T}\tag{FB-T}
\theta\, u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)=
\begin{cases}
\varepsilon(s)/\beta, & \text{if } \lambda_i(\theta)\leq \lambda^{\ast},\\[6pt]
\varepsilon(s)/\lambda_i(\theta), & \text{if } \lambda_i(\theta)> \lambda^{\ast},
\end{cases}
\qquad s\in T^{\ast}.
\]
%##########################################
The optimal transfers are independent of $s$ and satisfy $t_i^*(\theta) > 0$ if $\lambda_i(\theta) \leq \lambda^*$ and $t_i^*(\theta) = 0$ if $\lambda_i(\theta) > \lambda^*$. Moreover, the participation constraint \eqref{IR-def} binds for all strictly contributing types $\lambda_i(\theta)<\lambda^*$, so that $t_i^*(\theta)=\E_s[\theta\, U(q_i^*(\theta,s),s)]$ for those types; it is potentially slack only at the marginal type $\lambda_i(\theta)=\lambda^*$.
\end{proposition}

The result generalizes the Boiteux-Steiner framework (see \citealp{crew1995theory}) to redistributive weights and relates to the recent literature on redistributive mechanism design, in which the optimal allocation depends on the relationship between social weights and the exogenous preference for revenue of the market designer \citep{akbarpour2023redistributive}.\footnote{In that case $\beta$ is exogenous and when $\lambda_i(\theta)>\beta$, a positive transfer to type $\theta$ increase the marginal designer objective. See, for instance, \cite{Kang2024} for the design of optimal subsidies.} Off-peak, the optimal allocation reproduces marginal-cost pricing. On-peak, the threshold $\lambda^*=\beta$ partitions types into two groups. Contributing types ($\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*$) have a binding IR constraint: the market designer extracts their full expected surplus and equalizes weighted marginal willingness to pay at $\varepsilon(s)/\beta$ across all of them. Non-contributing types ($\lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*$) receive a zero transfer and are allocated capacity according to $\varepsilon(s)/\lambda_i(\theta)$, so higher-weight types receive strictly more capacity for a given $\theta$. A spot market fails to implement this allocation: a uniform price $p(s)$ cannot replicate the type-specific condition $\varepsilon(s)/\lambda_i(\theta)$ for non-contributing types, except in the knife-edge uniform-weight case where $\lambda_i(\theta)=\lambda$ for all $(i,\theta)$.

We now turn to the investment margin. The FOC \eqref{eq:FB-T} implies that differentiating the allocation with respect to $k$ naturally produces the following weights. Define for contributing and non-contributing types respectively:
%##########################################
\[
w_i^c(\theta,s) := -\frac{1}{\beta\,\theta\,u_q(q_i^*(\theta,s),s)} > 0 \qquad \text{and} \qquad w_i^{nc}(\theta,s) := -\frac{1}{\lambda_i(\theta)\,\theta\,u_q(q_i^*(\theta,s),s)} > 0,
\]
%##########################################
with aggregate weights $\overline w^c(s) := \sum_i\mu_i\,\E_{\theta_i}[w_i^c(\theta,s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*}]$, $\overline w^{nc}(s) := \sum_i\mu_i\,\E_{\theta_i}[w_i^{nc}(\theta,s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*}]$, and $\overline w(s) := \overline w^c(s)+\overline w^{nc}(s)>0$. These weights are positive since $u_q<0$ and $\theta>0$. Define the total marginal contributor surplus as
%##########################################
\[\tag{Euj$^\ast$}\label{EujFB}
\begin{aligned}   
\E^w_i[u^c|s] := \frac{1}{\overline w(s)}\,\E_{\theta_i}\!\left[w_i^c(\theta,s)\,\theta\,u(q_i^*(\theta,s),s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*}\right] \\+ \theta^*_i(k)\,U\!\left(q_i(\theta_i^*(k),s),s\right)g_i\!\left(\theta_i^*(k)\right)\frac{d\theta_i^*(k)}{dk},
\end{aligned}
\]
%##########################################
This expression combines an inframarginal term — the weighted expected on-peak marginal utility of contributing types — and a boundary term capturing the surplus of types crossing into the contributing set, whenever such a boundary exists. The second term is specific to the complete information setting, where the designer observes $\theta$ and runs a sharp extraction algorithm: full surplus is extracted from all $\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*$ and nothing from types above. Under incomplete information, IC constraints link adjacent types through the rent profile and this structure breaks down entirely: information rents are spread smoothly across the full type distribution, and the investment margin is governed instead by a covariance condition between the rent profile and the capacity allocation - the central object of Section~\ref{chap3sec:sec5}.\footnote{Equivalently, under incomplete information, the individual IR constraints are replaced by a single aggregate condition per category.}

\begin{proposition}[individual welfare and optimal investment]\label{prop:CI-welfare} i) Assume that both the contributing set and the non-contributing set have positive mass. Then, for every type such that \(\lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*\), individual surplus is weakly increasing in \(k\) if
\[ \E_s\left[\sum_i \mu_i \E^w_i[u^c|s]\mathbf{1}_{s\in T^*}\right] \geq I'(k). \]
ii) Suppose the optimal investment $k^*>0$ is interior. Then 
\[\label{eq:FB-k1}
\frac{1}{\mu^c}\,\E_s\!\left[\sum_i \mu_i\, \E_{\theta_i}\!\left[\theta\, u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\mathbf{1}_{\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*}\right]\mathbf{1}_{s\in T^*}\right] = I'(k^*),
\]
where $\mu^c := \sum_i \mu_i \Pr_i(\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*)$ denote the total mass of contributing types. 
\end{proposition}

A key feature of Proposition~\ref{prop:CI-welfare} is that welfare effects are uniform within each group: all non-contributing types move in the same direction simultaneously. There is no within-group cutoff $\tilde\theta_i$ separating winners from losers. This uniformity reflects the absence of IC rents: the market designer observes $\theta$ directly, extracts full surplus from contributing types, and the only margin that matters for investment is the aggregate budget condition in $i)$. This condition requires that the weighted marginal benefit of capacity expansion across contributing types exceeds the marginal investment cost; when it holds, the market designer self-finances the increase in $k$ without tightening non-contributing types' allocations.

The condition in ii) equates the marginal investment cost to the average on-peak marginal utility across contributing types, with no social weights appearing directly. Redistribution enters only indirectly, through the determination of $\lambda^\ast$ and hence of who belongs to the contributing set $\{\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*\}$: the market designer chooses how large the contributing set must be to finance the investment, but conditional on that partition, the investment rule is purely utilitarian over contributors. In the uniform-weight case, all types contribute and $\mu^c=1$
so the condition collapses to the canonical conditions
\[
 \E_s\!\left[\sum_i\mu_i\,\E_{\theta_i}\!\left[\theta\,u(q_i^*(\theta,s),s)\right]\mathbf{1}_{s\in T^*}\right] = I'(k^*) ,
\]
which is the standard peak-load pricing result: invest until the expected on-peak marginal utility across all types equals the marginal cost. Figure~\ref{fig:CI} illustrates the optimal allocation and rent structure under complete information for two consumer categories, contrasting the standard peak-load pricing benchmark with the redistributive mechanism.

\begin{remark}[Implementation with a two-part tariff]
The first-best allocation can be implemented by a tariff $T_i(x,s\mid\theta)=A_i(\theta)+p_i(\theta,s)\,x$, where the unit price replicates the designer's marginal condition: contributing types across all categories faces a unique unit price, while non-contributing categories faces a type dependent unit price:
\[
p(s) = \begin{cases} 0, & s\in S^*, \\ \varepsilon(s)/\beta, & s\in T^*,\ \lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*, \end{cases} \qquad \text{and} \qquad p_i(\theta,s) = \begin{cases} 0, & s\in S^*, \\  \varepsilon(s)/\lambda_i(\theta), & s\in T^*,\ \lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*, \end{cases}
\]
Moreover, $p(s)>p_i(\theta,s)$ for all $(\theta,s)$. The fixed fee $A_i(\theta)$ is set to extract the full expected surplus of contributing types, and to exactly offset the expected variable payment of non-contributing types so that $t_i^*(\theta)=0$.
\end{remark}


This benchmark will be modified along two precise dimensions under incomplete information. First, the market designer can no longer extract full surplus from contributing types: IC constraints force him to leave information rents, replacing full surplus with \textit{virtual surplus} in condition \eqref{EujFB}. Second, and more fundamentally, within-group heterogeneity reappears: the distribution of information rents across types creates a cutoff $\tilde\theta_i$ such that low types may lose from capacity expansion even when high types gain. The covariance condition governing this cutoff is the central object of Section~\ref{chap3sec:sec5}.

\section{Optimal allocation with private information}\label{chap3sec:sec5}

\subsection{Characterization}

We now assume that the consumer type is private information, while the consumer's category remains known to the market designer. In this framework, the direct mechanism consists of consumers reporting their type $\theta$. The mechanism assigns to each consumer of each category a quantity schedule $q_i(\theta,s)$ and a transfer $t_i(\theta,s)$. For private information, the mechanism is feasible if it satisfies the previous constraints \eqref{R-def}, \eqref{K-def}, and \eqref{IR-def}, as well as an incentive-compatibility constraint. That is, each agent reports its type truthfully if the following constraint is satisfied:
%##########################################
\[
\E_s[\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s)-t_i(\theta,s)] \geq \E_s[\theta U(q_i(\hat{\theta},s),s)-t_i(\hat{\theta},s)]  \quad \quad \forall i,\theta,\hat{\theta}  \tag{IC}\label{IC-def}
\]
%##########################################
Constraint \eqref{IC-def} imposes incentive compatibility in expectation over $s$. In general, this only requires monotonicity of the expected marginal payoff from reporting truthfully, and it does not, by itself, imply that $\theta\mapsto q_i(\theta,s)$ is nondecreasing for every state $s$. In our environment, however, the off-peak allocation will be independent of $\theta$, and on-peak allocations will be characterized by a first-order condition in which the dependence on $\theta$ enters through a term that does not vary with $s$. As a result, any potential failure of monotonicity (and any associated ironing) occurs across all peak states. Hence, for the purpose of the characterization, it is without loss to state monotonicity directly at the level of $q_i(\theta,s)$ (rather than at the level of its expectation over $s$): if the allocation is monotone in $\theta$ in one peak state, it is monotone in all peak states, and the IC constraint \eqref{IC-def} is then satisfied. Additionally, under \eqref{IC-def}, transfers $t_i(\theta,s)$ can be chosen so that, for each category $i$ and each type $\theta$, the  expected surplus satisfies, for some boundary term $\E\underline{CS}_i \in \Big[0,\E_s\big[\underline{\theta}_i U(q_i(\underline{\theta}_i,s),s)\big]\Big]$,
%##########################################
\[
\tag{IC}\label{ICmd-def}
\E CS_i(\theta):=\E_s\!\left[\theta U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)-t_i(\theta,s)\right]
= \E\underline{CS}_i+\int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\theta}\E_s\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\tilde{\theta},s),s\right)\right]\,d\tilde{\theta}.
\]
%##########################################
This representation follows from standard envelope arguments; see \cite{milgrom2002envelope}. Let $\gamma_i(\theta)$ denote the inverse hazard rate of $G_i$ and $J_i(\theta)$ the virtual surplus function associated with a consumer from category $i$ and type $\theta$
%##########################################
\[
\gamma_i(\theta):= \frac{1-G_i(\theta)}{g_i(\theta)} \quad \text{and} \quad  J_i(\theta):=\theta - \gamma_i(\theta).
\]
%##########################################
We maintain throughout the standard regularity condition that $J_i(\theta)$  is increasing in $\theta$. Then define $\Lambda_i(\theta) := \gamma_i(\theta)\E[\lambda_i(\tilde\theta) \mid \tilde\theta \ge \theta]$. This term captures the social value of the information rents accruing to all types $\tilde\theta > \theta$ when type $\theta$ receives a marginal unit: incentive compatibility forces the designer to also compensate all higher types, at a rate governed by the inverse hazard rate $\gamma_i(\theta)$, and these rents are evaluated at the average social weight of types above $\theta$.

\begin{lemma}[Virtual surplus representation]\label{lem:Transfer}
The program of the market designer can be expressed as: 
%##########################################
\begin{align*}
\max_{q_i(\theta,s), \, \E\underline{CS}_i}  \quad\quad 
&  \sum_{i \in N}  \mu_i   \; 
\Big\{ \;  \tilde{\lambda}_i \;\E\underline{CS}_i 
      + \E_{(s,\theta_i)} \big[\; \Lambda_i(\theta)\;  U(q_i(\theta,s),s)\; \big] \;  \Big\}
\tag{CS}\label{CSmd-def}  \\
\text{s.t.} \quad
& \sum_{i\in N}\mu_i
(\E_{(s,\theta_i)}\left[ U(q_i(\theta,s),s) J_i(\theta) \right] - \E\underline{CS}_i)\;-\; I(k)
\;\ge\; 0. \tag{R}\label{Rmd-def}
\end{align*}
%##########################################
with the same capacity constraint \eqref{K-def} given allocation schedule $q_i(\theta,s)$ and the participation constraint for the lower type, $\E\underline{CS}_i \in \Big[0,\E_s\big[\underline{\theta}_i U(q_i(\underline{\theta}_i,s),s)\big]\Big]$ for all $i$.
\end{lemma}

The formulation of this new program stems from the separation of the transfer problem (the choice of $t_i(\theta,s)$) from the allocation problem (the choice of $q_i(\theta,s)$). Consider the case in which either the budget constraint or the IR constraint is binding while the other constraint is slack; then, a feasible lump-sum transfer from the non-binding constraint exists that allows relaxing the binding constraint while maintaining the other constraint slack. Such lump-sum transfers are defined in Lemma 2. They are conditioned by the IC/IR constraint, such that high types do not mimic low types, i.e., the market designer is constrained to finance the investment on the utility of the allocation net of the informational rent a consumer receives, and the lowest type derives non-negative utility from participating in the mechanism (see  \cite{Myerson1981,Ledyard2007})

Because the objective and all constraints are separable across categories $i$ except through the aggregate constraints $\sum_i \mu_i k_i = k$ and $\sum_i \mu_i I_i = I(k)$, the designer problem can be decomposed into: (i) an across-category allocation of $(k_i,I_i)$ subject to the two aggregate constraints, and (ii) for each $i$, a within-category mechanism that chooses $\{q_i(\theta,s)\}_{(\theta,s)}$ to respect capacity $k_i$ and raise revenue $I_i$.

\subsection{Optimal within-category allocation}\label{sec:within}

This section studies the within-category problem for a given pair $(k_i, I_i)$. The analysis proceeds in two parts. The first asks how capacity expansion reshapes welfare across types: the answer depends on how marginal virtual surplus varies across types, which determines who benefits from relaxing scarcity once incentive constraints and redistributive preferences are accounted for. The second holds capacity fixed and asks how a change in redistributive preferences reallocates quantities and welfare: here both the sorting of virtual surplus and the proportional reweighting of social priorities interact, producing a generically non-monotone welfare pattern regardless of the direction of the preference shift.

\subsubsection{Characterization}

We begin with the basic structure of the optimal within-category allocation. The main takeaway is that the entire allocation can be organized into two regimes: one in which the participation constraint is slack and the allocation is undistorted, and one in which financing the revenue requirement induces an endogenous distortion via the shadow value $\beta_i$.

Following Lemma~\ref{CSmd-def}, the within-category problem selects an allocation rule $q_i(\theta,s)$ to maximize category $i$'s contribution to the designer's objective, subject to a category-specific capacity level $k_i$ and revenue requirement $I_i$. Since transfers enter consumer surplus with a negative sign, any slack in the revenue requirement $(R_i)$ could be rebated lump-sum to consumers while preserving incentive compatibility, raising the objective. Therefore the revenue requirement binds at the optimum: $\E_{(s,\theta_i)}[t_i(\theta,s)] = I_i$. Substituting the envelope representation $(IC)$ into this equality and integrating by parts with respect to $\theta$ --- using the definition of $\gamma_i(\theta)$ and applying Lemma~\ref{CSmd-def} to separate the lump-sum transfer $\underline{CS}_i$ from the allocation-dependent rent --- yields the single combined constraint
%##########################################
\[
\E \underline{CS}_i
=
\E_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right) J_i(\theta)\right]-I_i.
\tag{$\E \underline{CS}_i$}\label{ECSi}
\]
%##########################################
This reformulation combines the participation and budget requirements in a single constraint when the designer maximizes consumer surplus. Substituting $\E \underline{CS}_i$ into the objective, the within-category problem becomes
%##########################################
\begin{align*}
\max_{q_i(\theta,s)} \quad & \E_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[\Gamma_i(\theta)\,U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)-\tilde\lambda_i I_i\right]
\label{CSmdi-def}\tag{CS} \\
\text{s.t.}\quad &
\E_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right) J_i(\theta)\right]-I_i \ge 0,
\label{Rmdi-def}\tag{IR-R$_i$} \\
& \E_{\theta_i}\!\left[q_i(\theta,s)\right]\le k_i \qquad \forall s.
\label{Kmdi-def}\tag{K$_i$}
\end{align*}
%##########################################
Where $\Gamma_i(\theta) = \tilde\lambda_i J_i(\theta) + \Lambda_i(\theta)$ captures the total social return from allocating a marginal unit to type $\theta$, combining two channels: (i) a revenue channel $\tilde\lambda_i J_i(\theta)$, which is the net revenue extractable from type $\theta$ --- the gross value $\theta$ minus the rent $\gamma_i(\theta)$ that incentive compatibility forces the designer to concede to all higher types --- valued at the average social weight $\tilde\lambda_i$; and (ii) a redistributive channel $\Lambda_i(\theta)$, capturing the social value of those same rents as they accrue to types above $\theta$. Let $\varepsilon_i(s)\ge 0$ and $\beta_i\ge 0$ denote the multipliers on the capacity constraint \eqref{Kmdi-def} and the IR/budget constraint \eqref{Rmdi-def}.

The first-order condition is
%##########################################
\[
\label{KKT-qMD}\tag{FOC$_q$}
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\,\G_i(\theta)-\varepsilon_i(s)=0,
\]
%##########################################
where $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) := \Gamma_i(\theta) + J_i(\theta)\,\beta_i$.\footnote{Equivalently, $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) = (\tilde\lambda_i + \beta_i)J_i(\theta) + \Lambda_i(\theta)$: the effective social weight is therefore an equilibrium object: for instance, when $J_i(\theta) > 0$, a relaxation of the budget constraint ($\beta_i$ falling) reduces the premium placed on revenue-generating types and lowers their effective weight.}. We focus on interior solutions and impose two regularity conditions. The first discipline of the allocation rule: it ensures all types receive a strictly positive allocation and that the monotonicity constraint is automatically satisfied at the optimum.\footnote{If the first set of conditions fails, standard Myerson-style ironing restores monotonicity without affecting the subsequent comparative statics, since the ironed weight $\overline{G}_i(\theta)$ enters the FOC in place of $\G_i(\theta)$ but does not alter the structure of \eqref{Eujmd}. This stands in contrast with settings where the supply side imposes additional structure on the allocation: either indivisibility, as in \cite{CONDORELLI2013582}, or a fixed-quality schedule, as in \cite{akbarpour2023redistributive}, which restricts the set of implementable mechanisms.}

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Int}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:interior}
For all $\theta\in\left[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i\right]$, $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)>0$ and $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$ is non-decreasing in $\theta$, so that the monotonicity constraint on $q_i(\theta,s)$ is satisfied at the optimum.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

The second ensures the within-category revenue constraint defines a convex feasibility set, which is needed for the uniqueness and comparative statics results that follow.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Cvx}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:convex}
For every category $i$ and every $I_i\ge 0$, the set $\left\{q_i(\theta,s):\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i,s}\!\left[J_i(\theta)\,U(q_i(\theta,s),s)\right]\ge I_i\right\}$ is convex.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Define $S_i=[\underline s,s_i)$ and $T_i=[s_i,\overline s]$ as the off-peak and on-peak sets under the optimal allocation, where $s_i$ satisfies $\E_{\theta_i}\!\left[q_i(\theta,s_i)\right]=k_i$.

\begin{lemma}[Off-peak and on-peak allocation]\label{lem:FOC}
i) Under the weighted objective with type-dependent weights $\lambda(\theta)$, the allocation is characterized by a unique quantity schedule $q_i(\theta,s)$ for each $\theta$ and $s$:
%##########################################
\[
\label{eq:SB-Smd1}\tag{SB-S}
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)=0
\quad s\in S_i,
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\,\G_i(\theta)=\varepsilon_i(s)
\quad s\in T_i.
\]
%##########################################
ii) If \eqref{Rmdi-def} is slack at $(k_i,I_i)$, then $\beta_i=0$ and $\G_i(\theta)\equiv \Gamma_i(\theta)$.
\end{lemma}

\subsubsection{Sufficient statistics}\label{sec:suffstat}

With the optimality conditions in hand, the next step is to compress the comparative statics into a small set of economically meaningful objects. Rather than tracking the full allocation schedule, we isolate the two margins that matter: how additional capacity is distributed across types, and how marginal virtual surplus varies along the type distribution. Their interaction delivers a sufficient statistic that will organize the rest of the section, because it simultaneously governs welfare incidence, the response of the revenue distortion, and the feasibility of the undistorted regime.

To do so, define
\[
R_q(\theta) := -\frac{u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)}{u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)}, \qquad R_\theta(\theta) := \frac{J_i(\theta)}{\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)}.
\]
Note that $R_q(\theta)\geq0$ since $u_q<0$. The first object, $R_q(\theta)$, is determined by the curvature of demand and governs how a marginal increase in $k_i$ is distributed across types at the optimal allocation: if $R_q(\theta)$ is decreasing in $\theta$, then as capacity expands, lower types receive a higher share than higher types. The second, $R_\theta(\theta)$, reflects both the type distribution and the redistributive preferences of the designer, and governs how marginal virtual surplus $u(q_i,s)J_i(\theta)$ varies across types: if $R_\theta(\theta)$ is increasing in $\theta$, then the marginal virtual surplus tends to be lower for lower types.\footnote{Formally: $\operatorname{sign}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\!\left(\frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s)}{\partial k}\right) = \operatorname{sign}\, R_q'(\theta)$ and $\operatorname{sign}\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\!\left(u(q_i(\theta,s),s)J_i(\theta)\right) = \operatorname{sign}\, R_\theta'(\theta)$.} Crucially, $J_i(\theta)$ need not be positive: even when a consumer has negative virtual surplus, their marginal contribution to the objective may remain positive.\footnote{Assumption~\ref{ass:interior} imposes exactly this: all consumers have a strictly positive contribution to the surplus, which is equivalent to requiring $\Lambda_i(\theta)>-J_i(\theta)(\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i)$.}

Next, define the weights
%##########################################
\[
  w_i(\theta,s):=-\frac{1}{u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\G_i(\theta)}, \qquad \bar w_i(s):=\E_{\theta_i}\!\left[w_i(\theta,s)\right],
\]
%##########################################
Those weights represent the departure from the first-best allocation without redistributive preferences. As previously discussed, in this context, the marginal utility is equalized across types by a scalar. On the other hand, redistributive preferences create systematic differences in unweighted marginal utilities across types. Then defined the $w_i$-weighted average of the marginal virtual surplus
%##########################################
\[
  \E^w_i[uJ\mid s] :=\frac{1}{\bar w_i(s)} \E_{\theta_i}\!\left[w_i(\theta,s)\, u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)J_i(\theta)\right] =\frac{1}{\bar w_i(s)} \E_{\theta_i}\!\left[R_\theta(\theta)R_q(\theta)\right]. 
\]
%##########################################
And in particular, its average over the on-peak period
%##########################################
\begin{equation}
 \mathbb E_s\!\left[
\E_i^w[uJ\mid s]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\,\right]. \tag{EuJ}\label{Eujmd}
\end{equation}
%##########################################

\eqref{Eujmd} depends on two dimensions: (i)~how the additional capacity is shared between types, captured by $R_q(\theta)$, and (ii)~how the marginal virtual surplus changes across types, captured by $R_\theta(\theta)$. The interaction between these two dimensions is formalised by the following condition:
%##########################################
\begin{equation}
  \E_{\theta_i}\!\left[R_\theta(\theta)R_q(\theta)\right] = \E_{\theta_i}[R_\theta(\theta)]\,\E_{\theta_i}[R_q(\theta)] + \operatorname{Cov}_{\theta_i}\!\left(R_\theta(\theta),R_q(\theta)\right) \geq 0. \tag{signEuJ}\label{signEuJ}
\end{equation}
%##########################################
The covariance term captures the \textit{capacity sorting effect}: it is adverse when the covariance in \eqref{signEuJ} is negative. For instance, if $R_q(\theta)$ is decreasing and $R_\theta(\theta)$ is increasing, then the lowest marginal virtual surplus is concentrated at the lowest $\theta$. In other words, as $k_i$ increases, a greater weight is placed on types with a potentially negative marginal virtual surplus. A decreasing virtual surplus ($J_i(\theta)<0$) implies that increasing $k_i$ reduces the budget the market designer can raise from those consumers. Therefore, the condition highlights the fundamental trade-off the market designer faces: increasing the allocation of a type, given its social weight, may conflict with revenue maximisation due to the IC constraint on higher types.

The sign of \eqref{Eujmd} simultaneously governs three comparative-statics objects: $\partial\E\underline{CS}_i/\partial k_i$, the sign of $\partial\beta_i/\partial k_i$ on the IR-constrained region, and the reallocation component of $\partial q_i(\theta,s)/\partial k_i$; the derivation is shown in the preliminary step of the proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir}. These objects in turn govern the marginal welfare and redistributive aspect of a capacity extension (Theorem~\ref{thm:welfare}) and the feasibility of the optimal allocation (Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation}). To obtain a sharp characterization of the sign of \eqref{Eujmd}, we impose the following regularity condition.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Monot.}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:monotone}
$R_q(\theta)$ and $R_\theta(\theta)$ are both monotone on $[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$. Moreover, $R_q(\theta)$ exhibits log-monotone differences in \((\theta,k_i)\)
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

The primitive foundations and implications of Assumption~\ref{ass:monotone} are discussed in Appendix~XXXX. Assumption~\ref{ass:monotone} is used only to obtain a sharp threshold characterization: Appendix~XXXX shows that it implies that \eqref{Eujmd} is single-crossing in \(k_i\). When the assumption fails, the same sufficient statistic remains valid, but its sign need not vary monotonically with \(k_i\), so multiple crossings may arise.\footnote{This contrasts with Assumptions~\ref{ass:CARA} and~\ref{ass:mult}, which are introduced only as simplifying restrictions in later results. Their role is to simplify the dependence of \(R_q\) on the allocation and the state, thereby yielding sharper closed-form implications.}

\subsubsection{Redistributive effects of capacity expansion}\label{sec:welfare}

We now use this sufficient statistic to study the central within-category question: who benefits from a marginal increase in capacity? The answer is not mechanical. Even though a larger $k_i$ relaxes scarcity, the welfare gains from this relaxation need not accrue to all types once incentive constraints and revenue extraction are taken into account. This subsection shows that the incidence of capacity expansion is pinned down by the sign of \eqref{Eujmd} together with the ordering of types induced by $R_\theta(\theta)$.

The individual surplus change follows from \eqref{ICmd-def}:
%##########################################
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\E CS_i(\theta)}{\partial k_i} = \underbrace{\frac{\partial\E\underline{CS}_i}{\partial k_i}}_{\text{boundary term}} +\underbrace{\int_{\underline\theta_i}^{\theta} \E_s\!\left[\frac{\partial q_i(\tilde\theta,s)}{\partial k_i} u\!\left(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s\right)\right]d\tilde\theta}_{\text{IC rent}}. \tag{$\partial_k CS$}\label{partialkCS1}
\end{equation}
%##########################################
To interpret both terms, we first record how the optimal allocation responds to a change in $k_i$. Differentiating the first-order condition \eqref{KKT-qMD} with respect to $k_i$ gives
%##########################################
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s)}{\partial k_i} = w_i(\theta,s)\left( \frac{1}{\bar w_i(s)} -\frac{\partial\beta_i}{\partial k_i} \Bigl(\E^w_i[uJ\mid s] -u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)J_i(\theta)\Bigr) \right). \tag{$\partial_k q_i$}\label{partialkq1}
\end{equation}
%##########################################

Two observations: when \eqref{Rmdi-def} binds (the IR-constrained allocation), the boundary term equals zero: the entire welfare change is then carried by the IC rent in \eqref{partialkCS1}. On the other hand,  when \eqref{Rmdi-def} is slack (the IR-unconstrained allocation), then the second term in \eqref{partialkq1} is null as $\beta_i = 0$.


\begin{theorem}[Redistributive effects of capacity expansion]\label{thm:welfare}
Fix category \(i\) and a budget requirement \(I_i>0\). Define the set of cutoffs 
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\[\tilde\Theta_i := \left\{\tilde\theta_i\in[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i] : \partial\E CS_i(\tilde\theta_i)/\partial k_i=0\right\}.\]
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\noindent\textbf{IR-unconstrained allocation.} If \eqref{Eujmd} is positive, then an increase in \(k_i\) increases expected surplus for every type \(\theta\). If \eqref{Eujmd} is negative, \(\tilde\Theta_i\) contains at most a unique cutoff; and if it exists expected surplus decreases for all \(\theta<\tilde\theta_i\) and increases for all \(\theta>\tilde\theta_i\).

\noindent\textbf{IR-constrained allocation.} Assume \(R_\theta(\theta)\) is monotone and Assumption~\ref{ass:mult} holds. The lowest type always lies on the boundary: \(\underline\theta_i\in\tilde\Theta_i\). If \(R_\theta(\theta)\) is increasing, a second cutoff \(\tilde\theta_i\in(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)\) may exist and when it does:\footnote{When no interior cutoff exists, either all types lose from a capacity expansion or all types gain.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
    \item if \eqref{Eujmd} is positive, expected surplus increases for all \(\theta\in(\underline\theta_i,\tilde\theta_i)\) and decreases for all \(\theta\ge\tilde\theta_i\);
    \item if \eqref{Eujmd} is negative, expected surplus decreases for all \(\theta\in(\underline\theta_i,\tilde\theta_i)\) and increases for all \(\theta\ge\tilde\theta_i\).
\end{enumerate}
If \(R_\theta(\theta)\) is decreasing, the inequalities are reversed.
\end{theorem}

Expanding capacity is therefore not always surplus-improving for all consumers, and which types gain depends on the regime and on the joint sign configuration of \eqref{Eujmd} and $R_\theta(\theta)$, as we now detail.

When the participation constraint is slack ($\beta_i=0$), the second term in parentheses in \eqref{partialkq1} vanishes, so the optimal allocation is increasing in $k_i$ for all types during on-peak states and constant off-peak. However, the monotonicity of the allocation in $k_i$ is not sufficient for the capacity expansion to benefit all consumers. Indeed, the boundary term in \eqref{partialkCS1} then corresponds to an equal change in surplus across all types, while the IC rent captures the additional incentive rents induced by the change in $q_i$. If \eqref{Eujmd} is positive, both terms are positive: the common component and the IC rent both increase with $k_i$, so an increase in $k_i$ improves the welfare of all consumers. If \eqref{Eujmd} is negative, lower types are harmed. Indeed, the cross-partial derivative satisfies
%##########################################
\[
  \frac{\partial^2\E CS_i(\theta)}{\partial k_i\,\partial\theta} =\E_s\!\left[\frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s)}{\partial k_i} u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\right],
\]
%##########################################
which is strictly positive in the IR-unconstrained allocation: the boundary term does not depend on $\theta$, while the marginal IC rent is increasing in $\theta$. This monotonicity property yields uniqueness of the cutoff in part~(ii), as well as the ordering of types that experience a decrease in surplus.

Under the IR-constrained allocation (i.e., when \eqref{Rmdi-def} binds), the allocation must be distorted so that expected revenue equals the budget. This distortion is captured by the second term in parentheses in \eqref{partialkq1}. We show in the proof that $\beta_i$ is differentiable in $k_i$ on the IR-constrained region, and that the sign of $\partial\beta_i/\partial k_i$ is governed by the sign of \eqref{Eujmd}. The bracketed term in \eqref{partialkq1} is the deviation of a type's marginal virtual surplus $u(q_i(\theta,s),s)J_i(\theta)$ from its $w_i$-weighted average; its sign is therefore determined by the relative ranking of types according to marginal virtual surplus, which is captured by $R_\theta(\theta)$. If $R_\theta(\theta)$ is monotone, this deviation changes sign at most once, yielding a unique cutoff and the type-ranking described in Theorem~\ref{thm:welfare}. To interpret the condition, suppose expanding $k_i$ makes the budget less tight, so that $\beta_i$ decreases in $k_i$. The need for revenue extraction then falls, and the designer lowers the marginal weight on types that generate higher marginal virtual surplus. The sign of $R_\theta(\theta)$ pins down which types those are. For a given $s$, individual surplus is single-crossing in types under monotone $R_\theta$. However, this need not survive aggregation over $s$, since different states may have crossing points at different $\theta$. Multiplicative separability ensures that the allocation is $s$-independent on the binding region, so the crossing point is common across states and single-crossing is preserved in expectation.

The sufficient statistic in \eqref{Eujmd} does more than characterize the welfare incidence of capacity expansion. It also determines whether the IR-unconstrained allocation remains feasible as capacity increases, and therefore how the economy moves between the undistorted regime \(\beta_i=0\) and the distorted regime \(\beta_i>0\). This is important because the same increase in capacity may either relax the financing problem or make it more severe, depending on how the composition of marginal beneficiaries evolves with \(k_i\).

\begin{proposition}[Implementation feasibility under capacity expansion]\label{prop:implementation}
Fix category \(i\) and a budget requirement \(I_i>0\). Let \(k_i^+\) be the capacity level at which the capacity constraint never binds. Assume that \eqref{Eujmd} is \emph{single-crossing in \(k_i\) from \(+\) to \(-\)} on \([0,k_i^+]\). Then \(\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k_i)\) is single-peaked on \([0,k_i^+]\) and the set of capacities for which the IR-unconstrained allocation is feasible (equivalently, \(\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k_i)\ge 0\)) has the following form:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
    \item If \(\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k_i^+)\ge 0\), there exists a unique cutoff \(\tilde k_i\in(0,k_i^+]\) such that the IR-unconstrained allocation is optimal for all \(k_i\in[\tilde k_i,k_i^+]\).
    \item If \(\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k_i^+)<0\), the IR-unconstrained allocation is either never optimal on \([0,k_i^+]\) or optimal only on an intermediate interval \([\tilde k_i^-,\tilde k_i^+]\subset(0,k_i^+)\).
\end{enumerate}

If instead \eqref{Eujmd} crosses from \(-\) to \(+\): then \(\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k_i)\) is single-dipped on \([0,k_i^+]\) and either \(\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k_i)<0\) for all \(k_i\in[0,k_i^+]\), or there exists a unique \(\tilde k_i\in(0,k_i^+]\) such that \(\mathbb E\underline{CS}_i(k_i)\ge 0\) for all \(k_i\in[\tilde k_i,k_i^+]\).
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation} identifies the geometry of the IR-unconstrained region. The threshold \(\tilde k_i\), when it exists, is the capacity level above which the revenue requirement \(I_i\) can be financed without introducing the additional distortion associated with \(\beta_i>0\). The result follows from the single-crossing of \eqref{Eujmd} in \(k_i\), which determines whether capacity expansion improves or weakens the mechanism's ability to generate revenue.

The economic intuition is straightforward. A larger \(k_i\) always relaxes physical scarcity, but it also changes which types are at the margin of the expansion. If the additional capacity is increasingly directed toward types with low or negative virtual surplus, the expected revenue the mechanism can sustain may fall, so that further expansion tightens the revenue constraint rather than relaxing it. In that case, the designer eventually leaves the IR-unconstrained region and must rely on a positive shadow cost \(\beta_i\). By contrast, when marginal expansions continue to increase the mechanism's revenue, the undistorted region expands as capacity increases.

This distinction matters for the across-category problem studied next. Even categories with similar primitives may differ sharply in whether an additional unit of capacity relaxes or tightens the financing constraint, and therefore in whether their marginal value of capacity is governed purely by scarcity or also by the endogenous response of \(\beta_i\).

Figure~\ref{fig:impl_welfare} synthesizes the joint implications of Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir}, Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation}, and Theorem~\ref{thm:welfare}. Two features are worth emphasizing. First, the welfare effect of a capacity expansion is governed entirely by the sign of \eqref{Eujmd}, which switches at $k^\ast$, and by IR-regime boundaries. Second, under case~(ii) of Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation}, the single-peakedness of \eqref{Eujmd} and its negative value at $k_i=0$ jointly guarantee the ordering $\tilde k_i^- < k^\ast < \tilde k_i^+$, so all four regions are generically non-empty, with degenerate cases arising only when extreme segments collapse. A direct implication is that the redistributive consequences of capacity expansion are not invariant to the existing level of installed capacity: the same incremental investment can be progressive at low capacity levels and regressive at high ones.

\subsubsection{Redistributive preferences and optimal allocation}\label{sec:redist}

The previous subsection asked how a given redistributive objective shapes the welfare consequences of capacity expansion. We now reverse the question: holding $k_i$ fixed, how does a change of redistributive preferences reshape the optimal allocation? This allows us to study how incentive constraints shape the incidence of redistributive preference changes for a given capacity constraint. This comparative static is also the building block of the across-category analysis in Section~\ref{sec:across}: comparing the optimal allocations of two categories that differ marginally in preferences is equivalent to evaluating the local effect of a preference perturbation at fixed capacity.

Let $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta)$ denote the marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences. The induced change in the welfare weight is
%##########################################
\[
\Delta\G_i(\theta):=\Delta\Gamma_i(\theta) + J_i(\theta) \Delta\beta_i,
\]
%##########################################
%##########################################
\[
\text{with} \qquad  \Delta\Gamma_i(\theta): = J_i(\theta) \Delta\tilde\lambda_i+\Delta\Lambda_i(\theta) \qquad \text{and} \qquad
\Delta\Lambda_i(\theta):=\gamma_i(\theta)\E\!\left[\Delta\lambda_i(\tilde\theta)\mid \tilde\theta\ge \theta\right].
\]
%##########################################
An important object for the analysis is the following ratio that measures the proportional change in the social weight of a type $\theta$:
%##########################################
\[R_{\G}(\theta):=\frac{\Delta\G_i(\theta)}{\G_i(\theta)} \]
%##########################################
The following Lemma establishes the link between a particular change of redistributive preferences $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta)$ and the sign of $\Delta\G_i(\theta)$.\footnote{Due to the sorting effects that play a central role in the model, sharp comparative statics require $\Delta\G_i(\theta)$ to have a constant sign across all types. The Lemma provides primitive assumptions on preferences that deliver this condition.}

\begin{lemma}[Preference shifts and effective social weights]\label{lem:preferenceorder}
Consider a weight function $\lambda_i(\theta)$ and its perturbation $\lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)$, with $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta):=\lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)-\lambda_i(\theta)$ and $\Delta\G_i(\theta):=\G_i^\Delta(\theta)-\G_i(\theta)$. Suppose $\G_i(\theta),\G_i^\Delta(\theta)>0$ for all $\theta\in[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$. If $\lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)$ shifts preferences toward higher (resp. lower) types proportionally more than $\lambda_i(\theta)$, in the sense that $\frac{\lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)}{\lambda_i(\theta)}$ is increasing on $[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$. Then for a.e. $\theta\in[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$, %##########################################
\[ \frac{\Lambda_i^{\Delta\,\prime}(\theta)}{\Lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)} \;\ge\; (\text{resp.} \le) \; \frac{\Lambda_i^{\prime}(\theta)}{\Lambda_i(\theta)}, \] %##########################################
Moreover, define
\[
c_\Lambda:=\frac{\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta}{\tilde\lambda_i},
\qquad
c_{\G}:=\frac{\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta+\beta_i^\Delta}
{\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i}.
\]
%##########################################
if
$1\le c_{\G}\le c_\Lambda$ and 
(resp. $c_\Lambda\le c_{\G}\le 1)$, then for all $\theta\in[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$, $\Delta\G_i(\theta)\;\ge\;(\text{resp. }\le)\;0$. In particular, if either $\beta_i=\beta_i^\Delta=0$ or $\beta_i^\Delta+\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta=\beta_i+\tilde\lambda_i$, then the condition in part ii) reduces to $\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta\ge (\text{resp. }\le)\tilde\lambda_i$.
\end{lemma}

Recall that the function $\Lambda_i(\theta)$ measures the social value of the information rent given to types above $\theta$, evaluated at the welfare weights. The first part of the Lemma states that a proportional tilt of welfare weights toward higher types shifts the perturbed term $\Lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)$ upward relative to the baseline $\Lambda_i(\theta)$ across the type space.\footnote{The first result of the Lemma has a direct implication for the sign of $R'_\theta(\theta)$. From expression \eqref{signRt}, only the IC term depends on preferences; the revenue term is determined solely by the type distribution. As a maintained example, assume $\Lambda_i^{1\,\prime}(\theta)<0$, $\Lambda_i^{2\,\prime}(\theta)<0$, and $J_i'(\theta)>0$. In that case, a preference shift toward higher types favors $R_\theta'(\theta)>0$. For types generating revenue ($J_i(\theta)>0$), $R_\theta'(\theta)>0$ holds under both weight functions. For types that do not generate revenue ($J_i(\theta)<0$), the condition in \eqref{signRt} is easier to satisfy under a preference shift toward higher types, since $\Lambda_i^{\Delta\,\prime}/\Lambda_i^\Delta \ge \Lambda_i^{\prime}/\Lambda_i$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}.
} The second part connects the direction of a preference shift to the sign of $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$, but requires two conditions: the effective weight must not decrease on average ($1 \le c_{\mathcal{G}}$), and the average preference shift must be at least as large as the effective-weight shift after accounting for the budget shadow cost ($c_{\mathcal{G}} \le c_\Lambda$). The ``in particular'' part simplifies these conditions under two special cases on the equilibrium object $\beta_i$, which will be used later for the across-category results: tilting welfare weights proportionally toward higher types, while not decreasing the average weight, raises the effective social weight pointwise; and conversely, shifting proportionally toward lower types while not increasing the average weight lowers it. A standard perturbation in the redistribution literature is a mean-preserving rotation of $\lambda_i(\theta)$: $\Delta\tilde\lambda_i=0$. Under such a rotation, $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$ has a constant sign throughout the type space: positive when the rotation favors higher types, negative when it favors lower types.

\begin{lemma}[Reallocation under preference perturbations]\label{lem:realloc}
Fix category \(i\) and a across allocation \((k_i,I_i)\) For any local perturbation $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta)$ and any allocation regime,
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s) = R_q(\theta)\!\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta) - \mathbb{E}^w_i\!\left[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s\right]\right), \qquad s\in T_i.
\]
\end{lemma}

To interpret Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}, note that the sign of $\Delta q_i(\theta,s)$ is determined by whether the preference change raises the marginal social valuation of type $\theta$ by more or less than the equilibrium adjustment in the scarcity rent. Linearizing the pointwise first-order condition makes this precise:
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s) = w_i(\theta,s)\bigl(u(q_i,s)\,\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) - \Delta\varepsilon_i(s)\bigr) = R_q(\theta)\!\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta) - \mathbb{E}^w_i\!\left[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s\right]\right).
\]
Type $\theta$ receives more capacity if and only if its marginal social valuation rises by more than the scarcity rent. The second expression refines this condition in terms of the sufficient statistic $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$: the designer reallocates toward types whose proportional change in effective weight exceeds the capacity-weighted average. Since capacity is fixed, the reallocation is zero-sum across types within each binding state: $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[\Delta q_i(\theta,s)]=0$ for each $s\in T_i$, so any type that gains necessarily comes at the expense of another. This zero-sum condition is precisely what pins the mean-correction to $\mathbb{E}^w_i[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s]$: the capacity-weighted average of $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$ rather than a uniform one, because the weights $w_i(\theta,s)$ capture the heterogeneity in unweighted marginal utilities induced by redistributive preferences.

\begin{proposition}[Non-monotone reallocation under preference perturbations]\label{prop:rprefreallocation}
Under the IR-unconstrained allocation, if $R_\G(\theta)$ is not (a.e.) constant, then $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$ is not monotone on $\Theta_i$. Moreover, assume that $\beta_i^\Delta+\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta=\beta_i+\tilde\lambda_i$, then  $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$ is also not monotone on $\Theta_i$ under the IR-constrained allocation. 
\end{proposition}

The non-monotonicity of $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$ follows from a structural property of $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$ at the type boundaries, and reflects a fundamental tension between the revenue motive, anchored to the virtual surplus $J_i(\theta)$, and the redistributive motive, which operates through the social valuation of informational rents $\Lambda_i(\theta)$. For instance, under IR-unconstrained case, the revenue and redistributive motives cancel and $R_{\mathcal{G}}$ is pinned to the same value: at $\underline\theta_i$, the social value of informational rents $\Lambda_i(\underline\theta_i) = \gamma_i(\underline\theta_i)\tilde\lambda_i$ exactly offsets the revenue cost in $\tilde\lambda_i J_i(\underline\theta_i)$, leaving only the gross valuation so that $\Gamma_i(\underline\theta_i) = \tilde\lambda_i\underline\theta_i$; at $\overline\theta_i$, both terms vanish simultaneously following the no distortion at the top principle ($\Lambda_i(\overline\theta_i) = \gamma_i(\overline\theta_i) = 0$), again leaving only $\Gamma_i(\overline\theta_i) = \tilde\lambda_i\overline\theta_i$. The same logic applies to $\Delta\Gamma_i$, so $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\underline\theta_i) = R_{\mathcal{G}}(\overline\theta_i) = \Delta\tilde\lambda_i/\tilde\lambda_i$. Since $R_{\mathcal{G}}$ takes the same value at both endpoints but deviates in the interior whenever preferences change non-uniformly, it cannot be monotone, and by Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}, neither can $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$. A similar argument applies under the IR-constrained allocation. \footnote{Under the IR-constrained allocation, $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$ involves the endogenous adjustment $\Delta\beta_i$ and cannot be characterized further without solving for the equilibrium multiplier. We use the previous assumptions on $\beta_i$ from Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}, which pins the sum of average weight and shadow cost across the perturbation, to recover the same conclusion.}

Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation} has strong consequences for the welfare analysis of preference shifts. Since the aggregate effect combines an average component and a sorting component, and since the behavioral response $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$ is non-monotone in types, it is not possible, without further restrictions on preferences and demand curvature, to sign the overall effect on the equilibrium. 

\begin{corollary}[Reallocation under mean-preserving preference shifts]\label{cor:rprefreallocation} 
Assume that $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta)$ is mean-preserving and for any allocation regime, the set of cutoffs $\{\theta: \Delta q_i(\theta,s)=0\}$ contains at least two elements. Let $\theta_-$ and $\theta_+$ denote its minimum and maximum elements. Then: 
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
\item  if $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\ge 0$ for all $\theta$, then $\Delta q_i(\theta)\le 0$ for $\theta\in[\underline\theta_i,\theta_-)\cup(\theta_+,\overline\theta_i]$
\item  if $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\le 0$ for all $\theta$, then $\Delta q_i(\theta)\ge 0$ for $\theta\in[\underline\theta_i,\theta_-)\cup(\theta_+,\overline\theta_i]$
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

The mean-preserving assumption yields a stronger result than Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation}: the corollary characterizes the exact pattern of winners and losers at the type boundaries regardless of the interior. To illustrate this in terms of primitives, recall that Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder} establishes that a proportional tilt of preferences toward higher types implies $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) \geq 0$ for all $\theta$. The corollary then delivers a non-trivial consequence: even under this unambiguously favorable shift, boundary types necessarily lose in allocation. A designer who tilts preferences toward higher types cannot, therefore, expect those types to receive more capacity. The IC structure forces the reallocation to concentrate on interior types, regardless of where the preference change is directed.

We conclude by characterizing the welfare effect of a change in preferences. 

\begin{proposition}[Welfare effects of redistributive preference perturbations]\label{prop:welfare-redist}
Assume Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA} and unimodality of $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$. Define the set of cutoffs
\[
\tilde\Theta_i^\Delta := \left\{\tilde\theta_i\in[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i] : \Delta\mathbb{E}CS_i(\tilde\theta_i)=0\right\}.
\]
\noindent\textbf{IR-constrained allocation.} The lowest type always lies on the boundary: $\underline\theta_i\in\tilde\Theta_i^\Delta$. There exist at most two additional cutoffs $\theta_1<\theta_2$ in $(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)$. If $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\ge 0$ for all $\theta$, expected surplus decreases for $\theta\in(\underline\theta_i,\theta_1)$, increases for $\theta\in(\theta_1,\theta_2)$, and decreases for $\theta\in(\theta_2,\overline\theta_i)$. If $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\le 0$ for all $\theta$, the inequalities are reversed.

\noindent\textbf{IR-unconstrained allocation.} There exist at most three cutoffs in $(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)$. The pattern of $\Delta\mathbb{E}CS_i(\theta)$ is jointly determined by two signs: $\mathrm{Cov}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})$, which governs the starting value,
\[\sign\!\left(\Delta\mathbb{E}CS_i(\underline\theta_i)\right)=\sign\!\left(\mathrm{Cov}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})\right),\]
and $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$, which governs the interior shape. If $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\ge 0$ for all $\theta$: expected surplus follows the pattern $+,-,+,-$ when $\mathrm{Cov}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})>0$, and $-,+,-$ when $\mathrm{Cov}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})<0$. If $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\le 0$ for all $\theta$, the starting value and all interior inequalities are reversed.
\end{proposition}


Proposition~\ref{prop:welfare-redist} delivers a sharp lesson for redistributive policy. Even when the direction of a preference shift is unambiguous (see  Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}), the resulting welfare changes are generically non-monotone across types. The reason is that two forces operate simultaneously and in opposite directions for different types. First, the capacity constraint makes reallocation zero-sum. Second, the IC structure means that the boundary surplus $\Delta\mathbb{E}\underline{CS}_i$ shifts uniformly across all types, independently of where the preference change is concentrated. Together, these two forces create interior reversals in the welfare profile regardless of the direction of the preference shift, generating up to four alternating regions of winners and losers. A policymaker who observes only that preferences have shifted toward, say, lower types cannot therefore infer that lower types will benefit: whether they do depends on the curvature of demand and the IC structure of the allocation, not just the direction of the shift.\footnote{Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the precise number of regions depends on the individual surplus of the highest type, whose sign is governed by the weighted integral of $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$ around its mean: $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}\!\left[(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)])/\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\right]$.}

The result stems directly from the analysis of the change in the optimal allocation. Indeed, the individual welfare change for type $\theta$ satisfies
\[\frac{\partial\Delta\mathbb{E}CS_i(\theta)}{\partial\theta} = \mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\frac{\varepsilon_i(s)}{\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)}R_q(\theta)\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s\right]\right)\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right],\]
whose sign follows directly from $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\mathbb{E}_i^w[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s]$ since $\varepsilon_i(s)/\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)>0$ and $R_q(\theta)>0$. Unimodality of $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$ — equivalently, single-crossing of the derivative in $\theta$ — ensures that $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$ crosses its capacity-weighted mean at most twice, generating at most two turning points in the welfare profile.\footnote{Note that the unimodality assumption is used only to bound the number of alternating regions. The result should therefore be interpreted as a lower bound on possible alternating regions.} Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, $\mathbb{E}_i^w[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s]=\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal{G}}]$ is $s$-independent, which allows the sign of the boundary term $\Delta\mathbb{E}\underline{CS}_i$ to have the sign of the covariance $\bar\varepsilon_i\,\mathrm{Cov}_{\theta_i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})$; without CARA, both the derivative and the boundary term involve $s$-dependent weighted averages that cannot be signed without additional structure.

\begin{remark}[Link between $R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)$ and $R_\theta(\theta)$]
The identity from a local perturbation
\[\Delta R_\theta(\theta) = -R_\mathcal{G}(\theta) R_\theta(\theta)\]
provides a formal bridge between the two sufficient statistics that govern both the within- and across-category results. A preference perturbation reshapes who gains from capacity expansion precisely by changing how virtual surplus is sorted across types. We discuss in Proposition~\ref{prop:Elasticity} in the Appendix the conditions under which a change in preferences can clearly affect how virtual surplus is sorted across types (notably the monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$). When it fails, their joint effect on sorting can no longer be signed from primitives alone, and sharp results are no longer available.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Across category allocation}\label{sec:across}
Having characterized the within-category problem, we now return to the designer's outer allocation problem. The question is no longer how a given category adjusts to $(k_i,I_i)$, but how total capacity and total revenue requirements should be distributed across categories. The key idea is that the same objects governing welfare incidence and preference reallocation within a category — the sorting of virtual surplus and the proportional reweighting of social priorities — also drive the across-category comparison, now reducing it to a ranking of marginal capacity values and effective marginal costs of revenue extraction across groups.

\subsubsection{Characterization}

Because the objective and all constraints in Lemma~\ref{lem:Transfer} are separable across categories except through the two aggregate constraints $\sum_{i \in N} \mu_i k_i = k$ and $\sum_{i \in N} \mu_i I_i = I(k)$, the designer problem decomposes into: (i) an outer allocation of $(k_i, I_i)$ across categories subject to the two aggregate constraints, and (ii) for each $i$, an inner optimization over $q_i(\theta,s)$ given $(k_i, I_i)$. The outer problem is to choose $\{k_i, I_i\}_{i \in N}$ to solve
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\[
\max_{\{k_i,I_i\}_{i\in N}} \sum_{i\in N}\mu_i\,V_i(k_i,I_i),
\]
\[
\text{s.t.}\quad \sum_{i\in N} \mu_i k_i = k, \qquad \sum_{i\in N} \mu_i I_i = I(k), \qquad k_i\ge 0, \qquad I_i\ge 0 \quad \forall i\in N.
\]
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
where $V_i(k_i, I_i)$ is the within-category value function defined as
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\[
V_i(k_i, I_i) \;:=\; \max_{q_i(\theta,s)} \;\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[\Gamma_i(\theta)\,U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\right] - \tilde\lambda_i I_i
\]
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
subject to incentive compatibility and the category-specific constraints. This representation follows from Lemma~\ref{lem:Transfer} and the binding revenue constraint established in Section~\ref{sec:within}: $V_i(k_i,I_i)$ captures the total weighted consumer surplus the designer can achieve in category $i$ given capacity allocation $k_i$ and revenue requirement $I_i$, after optimizing over the incentive-compatible allocation rule.

By the envelope theorem applied to the within-category problem, the two partial derivatives of $V_i$ are
\[
\partial_{k_i} V_i(k_i,I_i) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right], \qquad \partial_{I_i} V_i(k_i,I_i) \;=\; -\tilde\lambda_i - \beta_i(k_i,I_i).
\]
The decomposition highlights that the across-category problem is governed by two marginal objects. First,
\[
\Pi_i(k_i,I_i) \;:=\; \partial_{k_i} V_i(k_i,I_i) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_s\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right] \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[ u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\G_i(\theta)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right], \]
is the expected shadow value of relaxing the category-specific capacity constraint. It measures the social gain from assigning one more unit of system capacity to category \(i\). This marginal effect is much simpler than the individual welfare effects studied in Section~\ref{sec:welfare} and in particular the result of Theorem~\ref{thm:welfare}: while the latter depend on the full response of the optimal allocation and transfers, the envelope theorem implies that, at the category level, an additional unit of capacity is valued exactly at the expected scarcity rent. In particular, \(\Pi_i(k_i,I_i)\) is strictly positive whenever category \(i\) is capacity-constrained with positive probability, since \(\varepsilon_i(s)>0\) in every on-peak state. Equivalently, \(\Pi_i(k_i,I_i)\) can be interpreted as the expected marginal utility generated by extra capacity, weighted by the effective social weight \(\G_i(\theta)\).

Second,
\[
\partial_{I_i}V_i(k_i,I_i)
=
-\tilde\lambda_i-\beta_i(k_i,I_i),
\]
is the effective marginal social cost of requiring one additional unit of revenue from category \(i\). This cost combines the primitive redistributive weight \(\tilde\lambda_i\) with the endogenous shadow cost \(\beta_i\), which captures the additional welfare loss created when the IR-budget tradeoff binds. Hence, the outer problem allocates capacity toward categories with the highest marginal social value of capacity, while allocating the revenue burden toward categories with the lowest effective marginal cost of funds.



\begin{proposition}[Across-category allocation]\label{prop:outer}
The outer problem admits at least one solution, and the set of optimal allocations is convex. Moreover, the KKT conditions are necessary and sufficient. The stationarity conditions are, for each $i \in N$,
\[
\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast) \;=\; \zeta_k, \qquad \tilde\lambda_i + \beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast) \;=\; -\zeta_I + \xi_i,
\]
with complementary slackness $\xi_i I_i^\ast = 0$ for all $i$ and where $\zeta_k, \zeta_I \in \mathbb{R}$ denote the multipliers on the capacity and revenue equality constraints, and $\xi_i \ge 0$ the multiplier on $I_i \ge 0$.\footnote{The Inada conditions on $u(\cdot)$ ensure that $k_i>0$ at the optimum, so the multiplier on $k_i \ge 0$ is zero.}
\end{proposition}

If the outer optimum is not unique, the set of optimal allocations is convex, hence connected. The two stationarity conditions have a direct economic interpretation. The first condition equalizes marginal capacity values across categories: the designer allocates capacity until the expected shadow value of relaxing the category-specific capacity constraint, $\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$ is the same across all active categories. 

The second condition equalizes the effective marginal social cost of revenue extraction across categories that contribute to the budget. For each category \(i\), this marginal cost is $\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$,
which combines the primitive redistributive weight \(\tilde\lambda_i\) with the endogenous shadow cost \(\beta_i\) generated by the IR-budget tradeoff. Equivalently, this object is the coefficient on the virtual-surplus term \(J_i(\theta)\) in the effective weight$\mathcal G_i(\theta)= (\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i)J_i(\theta)+\Lambda_i(\theta)$.



\subsubsection{Ranking of the optimal allocation}\label{sec:rankingall}

We now turn to the across-category implications of redistributive preferences. Categories differ through their social weight functions \(\lambda_i(\theta)\), and the question is how these differences translate into optimal capacity allocations. To study this comparison in a tractable way, we proceed locally: we compare two nearby categories by representing category \(j\) as a marginal variation of category \(i\), and later use this local comparison to order categories along a path of successive preference shifts. To obtain sharp results, we assume that all categories share the same type support.

The following theorem provides a necessary and sufficient local condition for comparing the optimal capacities of two nearby categories.

\begin{theorem}[Category ranking]\label{thm:cat-order}
Suppose category \(j\) differs from a baseline category \(i\) only through a marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences. Fix the baseline optimum \((k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\). Then, to first order in \(\Delta\lambda\),
\[
k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s)\,
\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}
\right]>0.
\]
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the condition reduces to $k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast$ if and only if $\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)]>0$. 
\end{theorem}

The result connects directly with Section~\ref{sec:within}: the same object $R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)$ that governed the reallocation of quantities and the welfare pattern of preference shifts within a category now determines which category receives more capacity at the outer optimum.

Since \(\Pi_i(k_i,I_i)\) is weakly decreasing in \(k_i\), the category with the larger marginal capacity value is the one that receives more capacity at the optimum. Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order} provides the exact necessary and sufficient local condition for this comparison. The key implication is that categories are ranked not by the absolute size of the change in redistributive preferences, but by their proportional incidence through IC-adjusted social weights. Hence, two categories may face the same absolute shift in welfare weights and nevertheless receive opposite capacity rankings if that shift falls proportionally on different parts of the type distribution.

To see the logic, consider the perturbation of the marginal capacity value:
\[
\Delta \Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)
=
\mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[
\left(
u_q(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\G_i(\theta)\,\Delta q_i(\theta,s)
+
u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\Delta \G_i(\theta)
\right)
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}
\right].
\]
This expression decomposes the effect of a preference perturbation into two components: a behavioral effect through the induced change in the optimal allocation \(q_i(\theta,s)\), and a direct reweighting effect through the change in effective social weights \(\Delta \G_i(\theta)\). Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc} shows that these two effects combine in a particularly simple way, since
\[
\frac{\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)}{\varepsilon_i(s)}
=
\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right].
\]
As a result,
\[
\Delta \Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)
=
\mathbb{E}_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s)\,
\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}
\right].
\]

Thus, categories are ranked by whether the preference shift tilts social priority toward types for whom relaxing capacity is most valuable in the IC-constrained allocation. A designer who focuses only on the sign or size of the raw preference shift, while ignoring its proportional and IC-weighted incidence across types, may therefore misrank categories.



\begin{proposition}[Revenue ordering]\label{prop:revenueordering}
Consider an equilibrium allocation $\{k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast\}_{i\in N}$. Define the two endogenous subsets 
\[
N^+:=\{i\in N:\beta_i^\ast>0\},
\qquad
N^0:=\{i\in N:\beta_i^\ast=0\},
\]
which partition \(N\) between categories that contribute and does not contribute to the budget (either may be empty). Then:

\noindent\textbf{(i)} At any equilibrium, $\max_{i \in N^+} \tilde\lambda_i < \min_{i \in N^0} \tilde\lambda_i$, so $N^+$ and $N^0$ are strictly separated in terms of average welfare weights, regardless of within-type preference structure.

\noindent\textbf{(ii)} Fix a baseline category $i \in N^+$, and let category $j \in N^+$ be obtained from $i$ through a marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences with $\Delta\tilde\lambda:=\tilde\lambda_j-\tilde\lambda_i$ and $\Delta k:=k_j^\ast-k_i^\ast>0$. Then
\[
I_j^\ast<I_i^\ast \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)<-\frac{\Delta\tilde\lambda}{\Delta k}.
\]
The sign of $\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$ is determined by the sign of \eqref{Eujmd}. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, this reduces to
\[
I_j^\ast<I_i^\ast \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G}]\frac{\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\theta]}{\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\theta^2]} > \Delta\tilde\lambda.
\]
\end{proposition}

The previous theorem identifies how capacities are ordered; this proposition goes one step further by i) establishing the identity of which category should be contributing to the budget based solely on the average social weights; and ii) ranking the equilibrium revenue requirements \(I_i^\ast\) within the contributing set.\footnote{Part (i) follows directly from the optimality conditions alone and requires no additional structure on preferences, while part (ii) is local and requires knowing how preferences vary within each category. The binary contribute/not-contribute decision is therefore simpler to characterize than the capacity ranking, and the revenue ranking within the contributing set.}

This result is of separate interest because the ranking of equilibrium revenue requirements is governed by a different mechanism than the ranking of capacities and which category should be contributing to the budget. While the latter depends on how redistributive preferences affect the marginal scarcity value \(\Pi_i\), the former depends on how additional capacity changes the endogenous shadow cost of the budget constraint, \(\beta_i\). The condition
\[
\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)
<
-\frac{\Delta\tilde\lambda}{\Delta k}
\]
has a natural economic interpretation: the term \(-\Delta\tilde\lambda/\Delta k\) measures the increase in average redistributive concern per unit of additional capacity, whereas \(\partial \beta_i/\partial k_i\) captures how the shadow cost of revenue responds when that capacity is expanded. Hence, a category that is more favored by redistributive preferences receives a lower equilibrium revenue requirement only when the induced reduction in the shadow cost of revenue is sufficiently strong. This links the across-category allocation directly back to the within-category analysis, since the sign of \(\partial \beta_i/\partial k_i\) is itself governed by \eqref{Eujmd}, i.e. by whether a capacity expansion relaxes or tightens the budget tradeoff inside the category.

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the condition has a particularly sharp interpretation, governed entirely by the two sufficient statistics $R_\theta(\theta)$
$R_\mathcal{G}(\theta)$ introduced in Section~\ref{sec:within}. A higher average redistributive weight is not sufficient for a category to face a lower equilibrium revenue requirement. In fact, a rise in average social concern, \(\Delta\tilde\lambda>0\), can be offset, and even reversed in its revenue implications, if it is not directed toward the types that matter most for the IC-adjusted revenue tradeoff. What matters is therefore not only the level of redistributive concern, but also how it is distributed across types. This composition effect is summarized by
\[
\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G}]
\frac{\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\theta]}{\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i}[R_\theta^2]}.
\]


We now derive global implications of Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order} by imposing sufficient structure on the primitives. The theorem gives a local comparison between nearby categories; the role of the next assumption is to order all categories along a common path of preference shifts, so that these local comparisons can be aggregated into a global ranking.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Order}
\begin{assumption}[Ordered preference path]\label{ass:ordered-path}
For each adjacent pair \(i-1\) and \(i\), there exists a positive \(C^1\) path $(t,\theta)\mapsto \lambda(t,\theta) \text{ on }[0,1]\times\Theta$ such that \(\lambda(0,\theta)=\lambda_{i-1}(\theta)\) and \(\lambda(1,\theta)=\lambda_i(\theta)\). Along this path, two conditions hold for every \(t\in[0,1]\):

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
\item the average redistributive weight is weakly increasing, $\partial_t \tilde\lambda(t)\ge 0$, where $\tilde\lambda(t):=\mathbb{E}_{\theta}[\lambda(t,\theta)]$,
\item the relative tilt of redistributive weights is toward higher types, in the sense that $\partial_t \log \lambda(t,\theta)$ is weakly increasing in \(\theta\) on \(\Theta\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path} orders categories along a common redistributive path. Condition~(i) says that moving from category \(i-1\) to category \(i\) weakly raises the average welfare weight \(\tilde\lambda\). Condition~(ii) says that this increase is not neutral across types: it is proportionally stronger for higher types. Together, these two restrictions ensure that the local comparison in Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order} can be iterated across adjacent categories. Using Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}, they imply that effective social weights shift upward along the ordering in a way that is strong enough to generate a global monotone allocation.

\begin{corollary}[Capacity ordering along an ordered preference path]
Consider an equilibrium allocation \(\{k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast,\beta_i^\ast\}_{i\in N}\). Under Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path}:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
\item \(N^+\) is an initial segment and \(N^0\) a terminal segment of \(N\),
\item within \(N^+\), the shadow cost \(\beta_i^\ast\) is weakly decreasing,
\item the equilibrium capacity profile is globally weakly monotone:
\[
k_1^\ast\le\cdots\le k_n^\ast.
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

The corollary provides a sharp economic ranking of categories by participation in revenue extraction and capacity allocation, though not yet a full ordering of equilibrium revenue requirements \(I_i^\ast\), which is discussed below. A higher average social weight \(\tilde\lambda_i\) alone is not enough for a category to receive more capacity: what also matters is how redistributive preferences are tilted across types. Categories that place proportionally more weight on higher types have a larger effective weight $\G_i(\theta)=J_i(\theta)\bigl(\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i\bigr)+\Lambda_i(\theta)$, not only through the average term \(\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i\), but also through the informational-rent component \(\Lambda_i(\theta)\). Under Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path}, these two forces move in the same direction. As a result, categories that are higher in the redistributive ordering optimally bear a lower shadow cost of revenue and receive a larger capacity.


\subsection{Optimal investment}

\begin{proposition}[Optimal investment level]\label{prop:investment}
The investment problem $\max_{k\geq 0} W(k)$ admits an interior solution $k^\ast > 0$. The optimal investment level satisfies
\[
I'(k^\ast) = \frac{1}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}\sum_i \mu_i\, \mathbb{E}_{(s,\theta_i)}\!\left[u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right],
\]
where $\tilde\lambda^\ast := \zeta_I$ is the equilibrium marginal cost of funds. It is unique and and satisfies 
\[\max_{i \in N^+} \tilde\lambda_i < \tilde\lambda^\ast \leq \min_{i \in N^0} \tilde\lambda_i.\]
\end{proposition}


\begin{corollary}[Investment response to redistributive preference perturbations]\label{cor:investment}
Assume Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}. Suppose each category $i$ becomes a new category $j$ through a marginal mean-preserving perturbation of $\lambda_i(\theta)$ where \(\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast\) denotes the first-order change in the equilibrium marginal cost of funds. Then:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
\item The induced change in optimal investment satisfies
\[
W''(k^\ast)\,\Delta k^\ast
=
I'(k^\ast)\,\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast
-
\frac{1}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}
\sum_i \mu_i\,\Pi_i\,\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G}],
\]
\item  If $\tilde\lambda^\ast$ is unchanged by the perturbation ( \(\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast = 0\)), then
\[
\Delta k^\ast>0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\sum_i \mu_i\,\Pi_i\,\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G}]>0.
\]
\item  In particular, if the mean-preserving perturbation follows a smooth ordered path that tilts redistributive preferences toward higher types, then \(\mathbb E_{\theta_i}[R_{\mathcal G}]>0\) for all \(i\), and hence, when \(\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast=0\), \(\Delta k^\ast>0\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}
