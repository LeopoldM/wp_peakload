\begin{abstract}
This paper studies peak-load pricing in essential goods markets such as electricity, transport, and health services, when consumers have private information about their willingness to pay, belong to observable categories, and the market designer has redistributive objectives. I characterize the optimal mechanism and provide new insights into two policy-relevant questions: who benefits from a capacity expansion, and how to rank consumers by redistributive priority. For a given consumer category, I show that neither a capacity expansion nor a shift toward more redistributive preferences generates a predictable distribution of gains and losses across types. The former depends on capacity and on how the ability to raise revenue is distributed across types, the latter on how social priorities are proportionally reweighted across types. Building on these within-category results, I characterize the optimal tagging rule for peak-load pricing, showing how observable categories should be ranked by capacity allocation, budget contribution, and redistributive priority. Finally, I establish the incomplete-information counterpart to the canonical peak-load investment rule and show that the distortion from private information persists at the optimum in contrast to the utilitarian case.

\section{Introduction}\label{chap3sec:sec1}
When demand is uncertain and capacity is limited, the standard framework of peak-load pricing answers two related questions: who should receive the scarce good, and who should finance the capacity needed to relieve that scarcity. These questions are especially important for essential goods (in particular electricity, network industries, and health services), because inadequate rationing and capacity shortages in these markets impose costs that extend far beyond the consumers directly rationed.\footnote{In electricity markets, \citep{Hinchberger2024} find a deadweight loss of about \$2 billion annually from flat retail pricing over several US markets, while the 2021 Texas blackout caused \$80--130 billion in economic losses \citep{GoldingKumarMertens2021,Busby2021}. The 2025 \textit{Urban Mobility Report} reports annual congestion costs of \$269 billion in U.S. urban areas \citep{Schrank2025}. In vaccine allocation, \cite{Moore2022} showed that misallocation of vaccines during COVID-19 led to about 1.3 million direct deaths worldwide by the end of 2021.} The issue is all the more relevant because they are industries characterized by structural pressure to expand capacity, driven by demand growth, technological change, and the transition to low-carbon supply.\footnote{In electricity generation, the IEA projects almost 4,600 GW of additional renewable capacity worldwide between 2025 and 2030 \citep{IEA2025b}. For electricity grids, the European Commission estimates total investment needs of around \texteuro 1.2 trillion by 2040 \citep{EuropeanCommission2025}. In urban transport, the OECD estimates average investment needs of USD 400 per capita per year in high-income cities between 2015 and 2050 \citep{Wagner2018}.} In that context, the rule that governs rationing today simultaneously determines the revenue stream that finances capacity, making the allocation of scarce supply and the financing of expansion part of the same economic problem. But these are also precisely the sectors in which distributional concerns are strongest. When capacity is scarce, the allocation rule determines who is served; when capacity is expanded, the financing rule determines who bears the burden of paying for it. This creates a fundamental normative tension. The classical logic of peak-load pricing points toward allocating scarce capacity to the users with the highest marginal willingness to pay and financing new investment through prices that reflect scarcity. For many essential goods, however, policymakers are reluctant to let either access during shortages or the burden of financing expansion be determined solely by willingness to pay.\footnote{Redistributive intervention takes several forms in practice. In energy markets, governments have used price caps and subsidies to protect consumers from price spikes \citep{Fetzer2024,Amores2025}, while nonlinear tariffs have been designed to shift revenue burdens toward higher-usage consumers \citep{Borenstein2012,Levinson2022,Smith2022,Randriamaro2026}. In transport, the distributional impact of congestion pricing depends heavily on how revenues are recycled \citep{Eliasson2006}. In health emergencies, redistribution takes the form of non-price rationing schemes based on vulnerability or social role rather than willingness to pay \citep{Pathak2024,Wang2023,akbarpour2023economic}.} As a result, the objectives of efficiency, cost recovery, and redistribution need not align. The consumers who should receive the good under an efficient scarcity allocation are not necessarily those a redistributive regulator would want to prioritize, and those from whom investment costs are easiest to recover are not necessarily those on whom society would want the burden to fall.

To study this tension, I consider a market designer, for instance, a regulated firm acting under a mandate to serve consumers, who must choose capacity and a state-contingent allocation mechanism for a good with stochastic demand. The good is subject to an ex post hard capacity constraint\footnote{Capacity is determined before the state of the world is realized, but the allocation may depend on that realization. This captures the fact that infrastructure and financing decisions in markets such as electricity, transport, or health services must be taken before demand conditions are known, even though the actual use of scarce capacity can adjust once those conditions are observed. The cost of unserved demand is infinite, which justifies imposing a hard capacity constraint. Hence, it is never optimal for the market designer to allow excess demand to materialize.} and the capacity must be financed under an ex ante budget constraint. Consumers belong to observable categories, but within each category, they privately know their willingness to consume, summarized by a single preference parameter that scales the value they derive from receiving a given quantity. The designer evaluates allocations using welfare weights placed on consumer surplus. These weights are treated as exogenous reduced-form objects rather than derived from explicit microfoundations. The interpretation is that the designer cannot directly observe whether a consumer is vulnerable or socially deserving and must instead rely on the willingness-to-pay information elicited through the mechanism as a proxy for social need.\footnote{In this paper, for instance, when the market designer assigns higher welfare weights to higher types, it is equivalent to stating that the market designer favors consumers with a higher demand. For a related critique of using observed electricity demand as a proxy for consumersâ€™ social value, see \cite{Borenstein2025}, who shows that much of the variation in residential electricity use reflects household size, rooftop solar, and climate rather than imprudent consumption.} The analysis proceeds in the following steps. I begin by taking the allocation of capacity and revenue requirements across categories as given and characterizing the optimal mechanism for a single category. This analysis shows how the effects of a capacity expansion and of redistributive preferences can be summarized by interpretable objects. I then use these results to determine how capacity and revenue should be allocated across categories. Finally, I endogenize the total investment level, delivering an incomplete-information counterpart to the canonical peak-load investment rule.

The core tension comes from the interaction between incentive constraints, redistributive preferences, and capacity scarcity. Conditional on capacity and revenue requirements, I first characterize the optimal mechanism for a single category and then study how the constrained optimum responds to changes in capacity and redistributive preferences. Beyond alleviating scarcity, capacity expansion indirectly affects the surplus available through the mechanism, which can be redistributed to consumers through transfers. However, incentive compatibility requires the mechanism to leave rents to higher types, and once the designer assigns different social values to consumption and transfers across types, those rents can reduce the available surplus, making the mechanism no longer welfare-neutral. This interaction yields the \textit{capacity-sorting margin}. I show that the sign of this margin, together with the ordering of types by their ability to generate surplus, characterizes the set of winners and losers from a capacity expansion, and that this incidence depends on the level of capacity. Concretely, giving the same consumer a higher welfare weight does not guarantee that the consumer benefits more from a capacity expansion. I then study how the constrained allocation responds to changes in redistributive preferences at fixed capacity. The relevant object is the proportional change in effective social weights, which measures the total social return from allocating a marginal unit to a consumer. This yields the \textit{preference-sorting margin}. I provide conditions under which average welfare weights and the direction of redistributive preferences determine the sign of this margin. I then show that even when redistributive preferences are shifted toward lower or higher types, the resulting changes in social weights can be uneven across consumers. Redistributive reforms, therefore, need not benefit the consumers they are intended to favor.

Using the same two sorting margins that organize the single-category mechanism, I then characterize the optimal allocation across categories, namely how total capacity and total revenue requirements are assigned across observable groups. The capacity ranking depends on the preference-sorting margin: categories receive more capacity when redistributive preferences place proportionally more weight on the types for whom scarcity relief is most valuable, so capacity is not necessarily ranked either by average welfare weights or in terms of absolute effective social weights. The extensive margin, which categories contribute to the budget, is governed solely by average welfare weights, providing a clean separation that requires no information about the within-type structure of preferences. However, the intensive margin, how much each contributing category pays, depends on the interaction between the two sorting margins, since a category's ability to raise revenue reflects both how capacity is allocated to it and how its redistributive preferences sort consumption across types.

The final result returns to the canonical logic of peak-load pricing by characterizing the optimal aggregate investment level. Under utilitarian preferences, informational rents are pure transfers in the social objective, so the complete- and incomplete-information investment rules coincide at the optimum. Under redistributive preferences, by contrast, the informational wedge persists even at the optimum, directly shaping both investment and welfare. The new optimality condition equates the marginal investment cost with the incentive-adjusted social value of marginal consumption, aggregated across contributing categories and normalized by the equilibrium marginal cost of funds. Comparative statics on the optimal investment level follow from the same two margins. First, redistributive preference changes affect investment through their effect on the average marginal value of capacity (via the preference-sorting margin) and through the equilibrium marginal cost of funds (via the capacity-sorting margin). Second, I show how the capacity-sorting margin interacts with the marginal investment cost to determine the effect of a global capacity extension on the across-category capacity ranking, and therefore, who gains and loses within each category from that expansion.
\paragraph{Related Literature} The classic peak-load pricing literature studies the efficient allocation of a scarce, non-storable good across states when capacity is fixed in the short run and costly to expand in the long run \citep{m1949tarification,Steiner1957,vickrey1963pricing}. Off peak, the optimal allocation sets users' marginal utility equal to operating marginal cost; on peak, capacity binds and the good is allocated so that marginal utilities are equalized at a common scarcity value, while optimal investment equates the marginal cost of capacity to that scarcity value \citep{crew1995theory,vickrey1969congestion}.\footnote{One important part of this literature concerns implementation, which I illustrate in the discussion. The central result is that the efficient utilitarian allocation can be decentralized through responsive or spot pricing. Prices equal operating marginal cost off-peak and the realized scarcity value on-peak, thereby allocating supply efficiently in real time \citep{Vickrey,Schweppe1988}.} This benchmark extends to incomplete information under a utilitarian objective. When consumers privately know their willingness to pay, the efficient allocation and investment can still be implemented through self-selection mechanisms \citep{spulber1992optimal,Chao1987}.\footnote{Once one moves away from the efficiency benchmark toward actual tariff design and policy adoption, distributional conflict quickly becomes central. In electricity, \cite{Joskow2012} stresses that dynamic pricing creates winners and losers and that fear of large redistributions is a major obstacle to adoption, while \cite{cahana2022distributional} shows that real-time pricing can be regressive. In transport, \cite{hall2021can} estimates the distributive trade-offs associated with alternative road-toll designs. For a broader discussion of equity concerns in congestion pricing, see \cite{Ecola}.} This paper shows how the benchmark changes once the designer has redistributive objectives. In a more general mechanism-design environment, I characterize the optimal allocation when consumers differ both in their willingness to pay and in their social priority, and show that peak-load pricing no longer equates marginal utilities to a common scarcity value. Instead, marginal utilities are distorted by effective redistributive weights, so the utilitarian spot-pricing logic breaks down even when individual transfers are feasible.\footnote{A related literature studies public-utility tariff design with redistributive objectives outside the core peak-load problem. \cite{Feldsteina,Feldsteinb} show that distributional equity invalidates the standard Ramsey--Boiteux rule. This insight has been applied notably to electricity by \cite{Levinson2022,Feger2022}. These papers share the conclusion that redistribution requires prices to differ across consumers, but they take the tariff format as given rather than deriving the full optimal mechanism from primitives.}

In the peak-load pricing literature, redistributive objectives under private information have received relatively little attention. \cite{spulber1992capacity} characterizes the self-selection contracts that implement the peak-load optimum under welfare weights, but does not study the comparative statics of redistribution, the incidence of capacity expansion, or the optimal investment rule.\footnote{Similarly, \cite{Rasanen1997} and \cite{Martimort2020a} study related public-utility environments in which redistributive preferences interact with incentive compatibility and distort the optimal tariff structure.} More broadly, the paper relates to the redistributive mechanism design literature, which studies similar tensions in settings such as externality regulation, rationing, subsidy design, and nonlinear pricing \citep{Pai2023,Gse2024,Dworczak2021,Kang2021,Kang2024,Makimattila2025,Cremer2002}. It is particularly close to \cite{akbarpour2023redistributive}, which also studies the allocation of a scarce resource to agents characterized by a publicly observed label and a privately observed type. The main differences are that my environment features a divisible good subject to a hard capacity constraint and an endogenous revenue requirement. As a result, the redistributive trade-off is shaped not only by primitives, but also by the equilibrium surplus generated by the constrained-optimal allocation.\footnote{\cite{Kang2024} studies a related redistributive mechanism design problem in which the correlation between demand and redistributive preferences also shapes the optimal allocation. Our setting differs most centrally in featuring a hard capacity constraint and an endogenous revenue margin.}

The main results can be expressed in terms of a small set of interpretable objects, connecting to the local-reform approach in public finance \citep{Saez2001}. In particular, the role of utility curvature mirrors that of demand or labor-supply responsiveness in the sufficient-statistics literature \citep{Chetty2010,Kleven2021}: it governs how scarce capacity is reallocated among consumers as capacity expands, and how changes in redistributive preferences translate into reallocation of quantity across types.\footnote{In that sense, it is related in spirit to reduced-form sorting effects such as the consumption-sorting channel in recent work on carbon taxation \citep{Bierbrauer2024,Gse2024}.} The paper also introduces two additional sufficient statistics specific to this environment: one governing how marginal revenue is sorted across types as capacity expands, and one governing how redistributive preference perturbations reallocate quantities across types. For both objects, I derive primitive conditions on redistributive preferences that clarify their economic interpretation.

Because consumers belong to observable categories while their types are privately known, the paper also provides foundations for group-based differentiation, in the tradition of \citep{Akerlof1978,Cremer2002}. This logic is especially relevant for essential goods subject to capacity constraints. In electricity markets, \citep{Borenstein2012} evaluates the California CARE program and studies how tariff design across household groups affects efficiency and redistribution, while \citet{Burger2020} provides related simulation work on alternative tariff designs protecting low-income consumers.\footnote{Similar issues arise in water and transport, where policy debates focus on group-based protections and access rules tied to observable characteristics; see \citep{HjortX.;Leflaive2020,Manville2022}.} Where prices are not an ethically acceptable rationing device, the same logic operates through non-price instruments: reserve systems in vaccine allocation explicitly allocate scarce capacity across observable groups such as essential workers, age cohorts, or medically vulnerable populations \citep{akbarpour2023economic,Pathak2024}. This paper provides new results on group-based capacity differentiation in a peak-load pricing framework, characterizing how observable categories should be ranked with respect to capacity allocation, budget contribution, and redistribution priority.



\section{Environment and mechanism}\label{sec2}

\subsection{Environment}

\noindent\textbf{Consumers.}
There is a unit mass of consumers. Each consumer is characterized by a pair $(i,\theta)$ and is subject to a common shock $s$ unrelated to type. The index $i \in N = \{1,\dots,n\}$ denotes the consumer category (e.g., households or industrial users). Categories are publicly observed, and category $i$ has mass $\mu_i > 0$ with $\sum_i\mu_i=1$. Conditional on category $i$, each consumer has a privately observed type $\theta \in \Theta_i = [\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$, drawn from a distribution $G_i$ with density $g_i > 0$ on $\Theta_i$. Types are independent across consumers and are known to the consumer but not to the market designer. All consumers are subject to a common demand shock $s \in S = [\underline{s},\overline{s}]$, drawn from a distribution $F$ with density $f > 0$. The realization of $s$ is observed by all agents.

\noindent\textbf{Preferences.}
A consumer of type $\theta$ derives value from consuming quantity $q$ in state $s$ given by
\[
\theta \, U(q,s) = \theta \int_0^q u(\tilde q,s)\, d\tilde q,
\]
with $U(0,s)=0$. The marginal willingness to pay $u(q,s)$ satisfies: $u_s > 0$, $u_q < 0$. Thus, marginal utility is higher in high-demand states, and it decreases with quantity. Transfers are separable, and there are no income effects. A consumer receiving allocation $(q_i(\theta,s), t_i(\theta,s))$ obtains expected surplus
\[
\E_s\!\left[\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\right].
\]
Participation requires that this surplus is nonnegative for all types:
\begin{equation}
\E_s\!\left[\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\right] \ge 0,
\qquad \forall \theta \in \Theta_i,
\tag{IR}\label{IR-def}
\end{equation}
where $\E_s[\cdot]$ denotes expectation with respect to the distribution of states $s$.

\noindent\textbf{Technology and feasibility.}
The market designer chooses the allocation of the good subject to capacity constraints. Let $q_i(\theta,s)$ denote the quantity allocated to a consumer of type $\theta$ in category $i$ at state $s$. The total quantity allocated in state $s$ is
\[
Q(s) = \sum_i \mu_i \E_{i}[q_i(\theta,s)],
\]
where $\E_i[\cdot]$ denotes expectation with respect to the distribution of types $\theta$. Capacity is determined by an investment level $k \ge 0$, with cost $I(k)$, where $I$ is increasing and convex. Production has zero marginal cost. Feasibility requires that total allocation does not exceed capacity in any state:
\begin{equation}
Q(s) \le k \qquad \forall s \in S.
\tag{K}\label{K-def}
\end{equation}
The designer must also satisfy a revenue requirement:
\begin{equation}
\sum_i\mu_i
\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[t_i(\theta,s)\right] - I(k) \ge 0.
\tag{R}\label{R-def}
\end{equation}
\noindent\textbf{Planner's objective.}
The market designer maximizes a weighted expected sum of consumer surpluses. Let $\lambda_i(\theta) \ge 0$ denote the welfare weight assigned to a consumer of type $\theta$ in category $i$, and let $\tilde\lambda_i := \E_{i}[\lambda_i(\theta)]$ denote the average weight in category $i$. The objective is
\[
\sum_i \mu_i \,
\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[
\lambda_i(\theta)\left(\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\right)
\right].
\]
\noindent\textbf{Timing.}
The interaction unfolds as follows: i) Consumers privately learn their type $\theta$. ii) The market designer chooses investment $k$. iii) A mechanism $(q_i,t_i)$ is proposed. iv) The shock $s$ is realized and allocations are implemented.

\subsection{Mechanism}\label{sec:PI}

I now consider the allocation problem under private information. The consumerâ€™s category $i$ is publicly observed, while the type $\theta$ is privately known to the consumer. 

By the revelation principle, it is without loss to focus on direct mechanisms. A direct mechanism specifies, for each category $i$, a quantity schedule $q_i(\theta,s)$ and a transfer schedule $t_i(\theta,s)$. For private information, the mechanism is feasible if it satisfies the previous constraints \ref{R-def}, \ref{K-def}, and \ref{IR-def}, as well as an incentive-compatibility constraint. Truthful reporting requires that, for all $\theta,\hat\theta \in \Theta_i$, 
\[
\E_s\!\left[\theta U(q_i(\theta,s),s) - t_i(\theta,s)\right] \;\ge\; \E_s\!\left[\theta U(q_i(\hat\theta,s),s) - t_i(\hat\theta,s)\right].
\tag{IC$^\times$}\label{IC-def1}
\] 
Constraint \ref{IC-def1} imposes incentive compatibility in expectation over the common shock $s$. Under \ref{IC-def1}, transfers can be chosen such that the expected surplus of a consumer of type $\theta$ in category $i$ satisfies 
%##########################################
\[ \tag{$\E CS_i$}\label{ECSi} \E CS_i(\theta):=\E_s\!\left[\theta U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)-t_i(\theta,s)\right] = \E\underline{CS}_i+\int_{\underline{\theta}_i}^{\theta}\E_s\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\tilde{\theta},s),s\right)\right]\,d\tilde{\theta}. \]
%##########################################
for some boundary term $\E\underline{CS}_i \ge 0$. This representation follows from standard envelope arguments.\footnote{In general, IC in expectation is weaker than pointwise IC: monotonicity of the expected marginal payoff does not, by itself, imply that the allocation is nondecreasing in $\theta$ for every state $s$ separately. I verify ex post that the optimal solution satisfies pointwise IC under Assumption Int. Off-peak, the allocation is independent of $\theta$. On-peak, Assumption~\ref{ass:interior} ensures that the effective social weight is strictly increasing in $\theta$, which implies that the allocation is strictly increasing in $\theta$ in every binding state simultaneously. The optimal allocation, therefore, satisfies pointwise IC, and imposing IC in expectation is without loss at the optimum.} 

\noindent\textbf{Virtual-surplus primitives.} Let $\gamma_i(\theta)$ denote the inverse hazard rate of $G_i$ and $J_i(\theta)$ the virtual surplus function associated with a consumer from category $i$ and type $\theta$
%##########################################
\[
\gamma_i(\theta):= \frac{1-G_i(\theta)}{g_i(\theta)} \quad \text{and} \quad  J_i(\theta):=\theta - \gamma_i(\theta).
\]
%##########################################
Importantly, I do not exclude $J_i(\theta) < 0$. A negative $J_i$ means that the surplus that can be raised from after allocating a unit of the consumer is lower than the information rent conceded to higher types. I maintain throughout the following regularity condition

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Reg}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:regularity}
For each category $i$, the virtual surplus $J_i(\theta)$ is strictly increasing on $[\underline{\theta}_i, \overline{\theta}_i]$.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Then define $\Lambda_i(\theta) := \gamma_i(\theta)\E[\lambda_i(\tilde\theta) \mid \tilde\theta \ge \theta]$. This term captures the social value of the information rents accruing to all types $\tilde\theta > \theta$ when type $\theta$ receives a marginal unit. Incentive compatibility forces the designer to also compensate all higher types, at a rate governed by the inverse hazard rate $\gamma_i(\theta)$, and these rents are evaluated at the average welfare weight of types above $\theta$.

\begin{lemma}[Virtual surplus representation]\label{lem:Transfer}
The program of the market designer can be expressed as: 
%##########################################
\begin{align*}
\max_{q_i(\theta,s), \, \E\underline{CS}_i}  \quad\quad 
&  \sum_{i \in N}  \mu_i   \; 
\Big\{ \;  \tilde{\lambda}_i \;\E\underline{CS}_i 
      + \E_{(s,\theta_i)} \big[\; \Lambda_i(\theta)\;  U(q_i(\theta,s),s)\; \big] \;  \Big\}
\tag{CS}\label{CSmd-def}  \\
\text{s.t.} \quad
& \sum_{i\in N}\mu_i
(\E_{(s,\theta_i)}\left[ U(q_i(\theta,s),s) J_i(\theta) \right] - \E\underline{CS}_i)\;-\; I(k)
\;\ge\; 0. \tag{R}\label{Rmd-def}
\end{align*}
%##########################################
with the same capacity constraint \ref{K-def} given allocation schedule $q_i(\theta,s)$ and the participation constraint for the lower type ($\E\underline{CS}_i\ge 0$).
\end{lemma}


The lemma separates the transfer and allocation problems. When one of the budget or IR constraints binds while the other is slack, a lump-sum transfer can be used to relax the binding constraint without violating the other. 



\section{The Constrained Peak-Load Mechanism}\label{sec4}

This section characterizes the constrained mechanism for a given pair $(k_i,I_i)$ and introduces the objects used in the rest of the analysis. The analysis proceeds in two parts. The first asks how a capacity expansion reshapes surplus across types. The second holds capacity fixed and asks how a change in redistributive preferences reallocates quantities and surplus.

\subsection{Characterization}

I begin with the basic structure of the optimal allocation for a single category. Following Lemma~\ref{lem:Transfer}, the problem selects an allocation rule $q_i(\theta,s)$ to maximize category $i$'s contribution to the designer's objective, subject to a category-specific capacity level $k_i$ and revenue requirement $I_i$. Since transfers enter consumer surplus with a negative sign, any slack in the revenue requirement can be rebated lump-sum to consumers while preserving incentive compatibility, raising the objective. Therefore the revenue requirement binds at the optimum: $\E_{(s,i)}[t_i(\theta,s)] = I_i$. Substituting the envelope representation \ref{ECSi} into this equality and integrating by parts with respect to $\theta$, using the definition of $\gamma_i(\theta)$ yields the single combined constraint
%##########################################
\[
\E \underline{CS}_i
=
\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right) J_i(\theta)\right]-I_i.
\tag{$\E \underline{CS}_i$}\label{underlineECSi}
\]
%##########################################
This reformulation combines the participation and budget requirements in a single constraint when the designer maximizes consumer surplus. Throughout the paper, I refer to this expression as the available surplus from the mechanism, which is redistributed via a uniform lump sum transfer to the consumers. Individual surpluses are then 
\[
  \E CS_i(\theta) = \underbrace{\Bigg.E\underline{CS}_i\Bigg.}_{ \text{fixed transfer} } +\underbrace{\Bigg. \int_{\underline\theta_i}^{\theta} \E_s\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s\right)\right]d\tilde\theta\Bigg. }_{\text{IC transfer}}. \tag{$\partial_k CS$}\label{partialkCS1}
\]
Substituting $\E \underline{CS}_i$ into the objective, the problem becomes
%##########################################
\begin{align*}
\max_{q_i(\theta,s)} \quad & \E_{(s,i)}\!\left[\Gamma_i(\theta)\,U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)-\tilde\lambda_i I_i\right]
\label{CSmdi-def}\tag{CS} \\
\text{s.t.}\quad &
\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right) J_i(\theta)\right]-I_i \ge 0,
\label{Rmdi-def}\tag{IR-R$_i$} \\
& \E_{i}\!\left[q_i(\theta,s)\right]\le k_i \qquad \forall s.
\label{Kmdi-def}\tag{K$_i$}
\end{align*}
%##########################################
where $\Gamma_i(\theta) :=  J_i(\theta)\,\tilde \lambda_i + \Lambda_i(\theta)$. Let $\varepsilon_i(s)\ge 0$ and $\beta_i\ge 0$ denote the multipliers on the capacity constraint \ref{Kmdi-def} and the IR/budget constraint \ref{Rmdi-def}.

The first-order condition is
%##########################################
\[
\label{KKT-qMD}\tag{FOC$_q$}
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\,\G_i(\theta)-\varepsilon_i(s)=0,
\]
%##########################################
where $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta) := \Gamma_i(\theta) + J_i(\theta)\,\beta_i$ is the effective weight of a type $\theta$. It captures the total social return from allocating a marginal unit to type $i$: the revenue channel $\tilde\lambda J_i$ values the available surplus $J_i$ that can be redistributed to all consumer, so it is valued at the average welfare weight, $\Lambda_i$ captures the redistributive value of the rent conceded to higher types, and $\beta J_i$ reflects the tightness of the budget constraint. When the budget is slack, $\beta = 0$ and $\mathcal{G}_i$ reduces to $\Gamma_i$. I focus on interior solutions and impose two regularity conditions. The first discipline of the allocation rule: it ensures all types receive a strictly positive allocation and that the monotonicity constraint is automatically satisfied at the optimum.\footnote{Appendix~\ref{app:three-nonmonotone-G} shows that with three types, monotonicity of effective weights holds if and only if redistributive preferences are not too concentrated on any single type. If monotonicity fails, standard Myerson-style ironing restores it by pooling types in the non-monotone region. Over any ironed interval, the optimal allocation is constant across types, so the sorting margins in the paper vanish rather than reverse, and their effect depends on the non-ironed types. This stands in contrast to settings where the supply side independently restricts optimal mechanisms, either through indivisibility, as in \cite{CONDORELLI2013582}, or through a fixed quality schedule, as in \cite{akbarpour2023redistributive}.}

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Int}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:interior}
For all $\theta\in\left[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i\right]$, $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)>0$ and $\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$ is non-decreasing in $\theta$, so that the monotonicity constraint on $q_i(\theta,s)$ is satisfied at the optimum. Moreover, the marginal utility function satisfies $\lim_{q\rightarrow 0}u(q,s) = +\infty$.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Define $S_i=[\underline s,s_i)$ and $T_i=[s_i,\overline s]$ as the off-peak and on-peak sets under the optimal allocation, where $s_i$ satisfies $\E_{i}\!\left[q_i(\theta,s_i)\right]=k_i$.

\begin{lemma}[Off-peak and on-peak allocation]\label{lem:FOC}
Assume~\ref{ass:interior} holds and that the problem is convex. Under the weighted objective with type-dependent weights $\lambda(\theta)$, the allocation is characterized by a unique quantity schedule $q_i(\theta,s)$ for each $\theta$ and $s$:
%##########################################
\[
\label{eq:SB-Smd1}\tag{SB-S}
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)=0
\quad s\in S_i,
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\,\G_i(\theta)=\varepsilon_i(s)
\quad s\in T_i.
\]
\end{lemma}

\subsection{Sufficient statistics and assumptions}\label{sec:suffstat}

I now define the sufficient statistics that drive the main results of the paper. I start with the capacity-sorting margin and then discuss the preference-sorting margin.

\subsubsection{Capacity margin}

Define
\[
R_q(\theta):=-\frac{u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)}{u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)},
\]
The object $R_q$ is determined by the curvature of demand and is the reciprocal of the coefficient of absolute risk aversion, with $R_q(\theta)\geq 0$ since $u_q<0$. It governs how an increase in $k$ is distributed across types at the optimal allocation. For instance, differentiating \ref{KKT-qMD} at a fixed budget multiplier $\beta_i$ and equating across two types $i,j$ yields $dq_i/R_q(\theta_i)=dq_j/R_q(\theta_j)$. 

Next, define
\[
R_\theta(\theta):=\frac{J_i(\theta)}{\mathcal G_i(\theta)},
\]
as the ratio of a type's ability to raise the available surplus ($J_i$) to its effective social weight ($\mathcal{G}_i$), and measures the marginal revenue generated per unit of social value when serving type $i$. In particular, it governs how the marginal virtual surplus $u(q_i,s)J_i(\theta)$ varies across types. If $R_\theta(\theta)$ is increasing in $\theta$, then the marginal virtual surplus tends to be lower for lower types.

Define the weights
\[
w_i(\theta,s):=-\frac{1}{u_q\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\mathcal G_i(\theta)},
\quad
\overline w_i(s):=\E_{i}\!\left[w_i(\theta,s)\right].
\]
These weights measure the responsiveness of $q_i$ to the scarcity rent. To see this, differentiating \ref{KKT-qMD} gives the equality $ R_q(\theta) = w_i(\theta,s)\varepsilon_i(s)  $, so $w_i$ governs how the optimal allocation responds to a change in capacity. 

Now, combine the previous objects to define
\[
\E_i^w[uJ\mid s]
:=
\frac{1}{\overline w_i(s)}
\E_{i}\!\left[w_i(\theta,s)\,u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)J_i(\theta)\right]
=
\frac{1}{\overline w_i(s)}
\E_{i}\!\left[R_\theta(\theta)R_q(\theta)\right].
\]
Average over binding states yields
\[
\E_{(s,i)}^w[uJ\mid s] := \mathbb E_s\!\left[\E_i^w[uJ\mid s]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]. \tag{EuJ}\label{Eujmd}
\]
The sign of this weighted value is the \textit{capacity-sorting margin}. It admits two economic interpretations, each operative in a different regime. When the budget constraint is slack ($\beta_i = 0$), it has the same sign as the derivative of the available surplus \ref{underlineECSi} with respect to $k_i$. When the budget constraint binds ($\beta_i > 0$), its sign determines whether a capacity expansion loosens or tightens the budget, via $\partial_k \beta_i$. Both interpretations are established formally in Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus} and illustrated in the two-type case of Section~\ref{sec3TwoType}. The two expressions of $\E_i^w[uJ\mid s]$ above give two interpretations of the capacity-sorting margin. The first interpretation, reading the middle expression, is that $R_q(\theta_i)$ governs how a capacity expansion is distributed across types, while $R_\theta(\theta_i)$ governs the marginal revenue generated by each type's allocation. When $R_q$ is decreasing in $\theta$, a capacity expansion flows disproportionately to low types; when $R_\theta$ is also increasing in $\theta$, those are precisely the types that contribute least to available surplus, so expansion reduces this surplus and tightens the budget constraint. The second interpretation, reading the right-hand expression, is that $\E_i^w[uJ\mid s]$ is a $w_i$-weighted average of marginal virtual surplus $u(q_i)J_i$, where the weights $w_i$ jointly reflect the responsiveness of each type's demand and its effective social value.

To obtain a sharp characterization, I impose the following regularity condition.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Monot.}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:monotone}
\(R_q(\theta)\) and \(R_\theta(\theta)\) are both monotone on \([\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]\). Moreover, \(R_q(\theta)\) exhibits log-monotone differences in \((\theta,k_i)\).
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

In particular, I show in Lemma~\ref{lem:SC-dir} of the Appendix that \ref{Eujmd} is single-crossing in \(k_i\) if whether \(R_q(\theta)\) and \(R_\theta(\theta)\) are increasing or decreasing, and on whether \(R_q(\theta)\) exhibits positive or negative log-monotone differences in \((\theta,k_i)\). Hence, the available surplus to be redistributed  is either single-peaked or single-dipped.\footnote{The economic interpretation of log-monotone differences in \((\theta,k_i)\) is intuitive. Suppose that, for each \(k_i\), $R_\theta(\theta)$ is increasing in $\theta$, so higher types receive higher effective virtual surplus, and that \(R_q(\theta)\) is log-submodular in \((\theta,k_i)\). Then capacity expansion disproportionately raises \(R_q\) for lower types, in the sense that for \(k_i'>k_i\), the ratio \(R_q(\theta,k_i')/R_q(\theta)\) is decreasing in \(\theta\). Thus, as capacity expands, the weighted average \ref{Eujmd} shifts monotonically from the regime in which the average effect dominates toward the regime in which the sorting effect dominates.} I discuss in section~\ref{appsec:singlecross} in the Appendix, the conditions for establishing the monotonicity of those ratios. In particular, 
\[
\label{signRt}\tag{$\sign R_\theta$} \sign\!\left(R_\theta'(\theta)\right) = \sign\!\left(J_i(\theta)\right)\,\sign\!\Bigg(\underbrace{\frac{J_i'(\theta)}{J_i(\theta)}}_{\text{revenue motive}} - \underbrace{\frac{\Lambda_i'(\theta)}{\Lambda_i(\theta)}}_{\text{redistributive motive}}\Bigg).
\] For types contributing positively to the available surplus ($J_i(\theta) > 0$), $R_\theta$ is increasing whenever the revenue motive grows proportionally faster than the redistributive motive. For types contributing negatively ($J_i(\theta) < 0$), the reverse holds: $R_\theta$ is increasing whenever the revenue motive grows proportionally slower. When those assumptions fail, the same sufficient statistic remains valid, but its sign need not vary monotonically with \(k_i\).

Finally, a useful specification used in the paper is CARA utility as a consumer's responsiveness is identical regardless of the quantity allocated. 
 
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{CARA}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:CARA}
$U(q,s) = v(s)\exp(-\alpha q)$ for some $\alpha > 0$ and some function $v(s) > 0$.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, $R_q = 1/\alpha$ is constant: a consumer's responsiveness is identical regardless of the quantity allocated. Additional capacity is therefore distributed across types in proportions determined solely by welfare weights, and the behavioral channel through which a preference shift distorts the allocation across types vanishes.

\subsubsection{Preference margin}

I start by defining a perturbation of redistributive preferences. Let $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta)$ denote the marginal change in redistributive preferences. The induced change in the effective social weight is
\[
\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)
:=
J_i(\theta)\bigl(\Delta\tilde{\lambda}_i+\Delta\beta_i\bigr)
+\Delta\Lambda_i(\theta),
\qquad
\Delta\Lambda_i(\theta)
:=
\gamma_i(\theta)\E_i\!\left[\Delta\lambda_i(\tilde\theta)\mid \tilde\theta\ge\theta\right],
\]
where $\Delta\beta_i$ is the induced change in the budget multiplier at the new optimum. The key statistic is the proportional change in the effective social weight of type $\theta$,
\[
R_{\mathcal{G},i}(\theta)
:=
\frac{\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)}{\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)},
\]
which measures how the perturbation tilts the effective weight of type $\theta$ relative to the baseline allocation. This is the preference-sorting margin, and it governs how a change in redistributive preferences reshapes the constrained allocation at fixed capacity. This object appears directly when differentiating \eqref{KKT-qMD}. Substituting $u(q_i(\theta,s),s)=\varepsilon_i(s)/\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)$ into the differentiated first-order condition yields
\[
\tag{$\Delta$FOC$_q$}\label{DeltaKKT-qMD}
\frac{\Delta q_i(\theta,s)}{R_q(\theta)}
=
R_{\mathcal G,i}(\theta)- \frac{\Delta \varepsilon_i(s)}{\varepsilon_i(s)}.
\]
A preference perturbation affects the optimal allocation through two forces. The first is the proportional adjustment of the scarcity rent, $\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)/\varepsilon_i(s)$, which is common to all types within state $s$ and captures how tight capacity becomes once preferences have shifted. The second is the proportional revaluation of each type's effective social weight, $R_{\mathcal G,i}(\theta)$, which captures how the perturbation changes the social value of serving type $\theta$ relative to the baseline allocation. At the category-state level, the relevant aggregate object is the weighted average $\E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]$, which summarizes the average preference tilt across types using the weights $w_i(\theta,s)$ defined above.

Appendix Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder} provides primitive conditions on preference perturbations under which $R_{\G}(\theta)$ has a constant sign across types. The first part of the Lemma states that a proportional tilt of welfare weights toward higher types shifts the social value of the information rent given to types above $\theta$ (i.e., $\Lambda_i(\theta)$) upward relative to the baseline across the type space.\footnote{The first result of the Lemma has a direct implication for the sign of $R'_\theta(\theta)$. From expression \ref{signRt}, only the IC term depends on preferences; the revenue term is determined solely by the type distribution. As a maintained example, assume $\Lambda_i^{1\,\prime}(\theta)<0$, $\Lambda_i^{2\,\prime}(\theta)<0$, and $J_i'(\theta)>0$. In that case, a preference shift toward higher types favors $R_\theta'(\theta)>0$. For types generating revenue ($J_i(\theta)>0$), $R_\theta'(\theta)>0$ holds under both weight functions. For types that do not generate revenue ($J_i(\theta)<0$), the condition in \ref{signRt} is easier to satisfy under a preference shift toward higher types, since $\Lambda_i^{\Delta\,\prime}/\Lambda_i^\Delta \ge \Lambda_i^{\prime}/\Lambda_i$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}.} The second part connects the direction of a preference shift to the sign of $R_{\G}(\theta)$, and requires two conditions. The effective weight must not decrease on average, and the average preference shift must be at least as large as the effective-weight shift after accounting for the budget shadow cost. These conditions simplify under two special cases on the equilibrium object $\beta_i$, which will be used later for the across-category results. Tilting welfare weights proportionally toward higher types, while not decreasing the average weight, yields $R_{\G}(\theta)>0$ for all $\theta$; and conversely, shifting proportionally toward lower types while not increasing the average weight yields $R_{\G}(\theta)<0$ for all $\theta$. A standard perturbation in the redistribution literature is a (monotonic) mean-preserving rotation of $\lambda_i(\theta)$, i.e.\ $\Delta\tilde\lambda_i=0$ (see \cite{Kang2024} for a recent use in the context of redistributive mechanism design). Under such a rotation, $R_{\G}(\theta)$ has a constant sign throughout the type space. It is positive when the rotation favors higher types, and negative when it favors lower types.


\subsection{A two-type illustration of the sorting margins}\label{sec3TwoType}

To illustrate the two sorting margins in the simplest possible environment, consider a single category with two types, $\theta\in\{\theta_L,\theta_H\}$, occurring with probabilities $p_L$ and $p_H$, where $p_L+p_H=1$. I focus on the peak allocation in the case where the participation constraint is slack, so $\beta=0$, and suppress the state index. A type-$\theta$ consumer then obtains utility $\theta U(q)$ from quantity $q$. In particular, the example shows how the capacity-sorting margin governs the surplus incidence of a marginal capacity expansion, and how the preference-sorting margin, through the proportional change in effective social weight $R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)$, governs the direction of reallocation induced by a change in redistributive preferences.

In the two-type case, the expressions for available surplus and individual surplus simplify as follows.
\[\tag{$\E \underline{CS}_{LH}$}\label{underlineECSTwo}
\E \underline{CS} := \underbrace{\Bigg.\sum_i p_i \theta_i U(q_i)\Bigg.}_{\text{surplus}} -  \underbrace{\Bigg.\sum_i p_i \gamma_i U(q_i)\Bigg.}_{\text{IC rent}} - \underbrace{\Bigg.I\Bigg.}_{\text{cost}} = p_L J_L U(q_L) + p_H J_H U(q_H)  - I,
\]
\[
\tag{ECS$_{LH}$}\label{ECSlh} ECS(\theta_L) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\underline{CS}}_{\text{fixed transfer}}, \qquad ECS(\theta_H) = \mathbb{E}\underline{CS} + \underbrace{(\theta_H - \theta_L)U(q_L)}_{\text{IC transfer}}.
\] 
Hence the low type receives the full redistributable surplus as a lump-sum transfer, while the high type receives the same lump-sum transfer plus a type-dependent informational rent.

The inverse hazard rate of the low type is $\gamma_L = p_H/p_L$, while $\gamma_H = 0$ since no information rent accrues above the high type. The virtual terms are \[J_L = \theta_L - \frac{p_H}{p_L}(\theta_H-\theta_L), \qquad J_H = \theta_H.\] $J_L < 0$ arises when the low type's value lies below the high type's value weighted by the share of high types, $\theta_L < p_H \theta_H$. The social value of this information rent flowing upward to $\theta_H$ when $\theta_L$ receives a marginal unit is \[\Lambda_L = \frac{p_H}{p_L}(\theta_H-\theta_L)\lambda_H, \qquad \Lambda_H = 0\] 

\paragraph{Capacity sorting margin with two types.} I now study how a capacity expansion affects the available surplus, \ref{underlineECSTwo}. The two-type surplus decomposition in \ref{ECSlh} implies that the sign of \ref{dkECS} determines the individual welfare change. In particular, whenever \ref{dkECS} $< 0$, the low type loses from an increase in $k$ regardless of the welfare weight $\lambda_L$.

Differentiating $ECS(\theta_L)$ with respect to $k$, factorizing by $d_k q_L$, $\varepsilon$ and $1/R_q(\theta_L)$ yields
\[
\tag{$\partial_k \mathbb{E}\underline{CS}_{LH}$}\label{dkECS} \partial_k ECS(\theta_L) = \frac{d_k q_L}{R_q(\theta_L)} \; \varepsilon \; \Big( \underbrace{\theta_L \; p_L \frac{R_q(\theta_L)}{\Gamma_L} + \theta_H \; p_H \frac{R_q(\theta_H)}{\Gamma_H}}_{\text{available surplus}} - \underbrace{(\theta_H - \theta_L) \; p_H \frac{R_q(\theta_L)}{\Gamma_L}}_{\text{IC cost}} \Big).
\] 
Equivalently, the same object can be rewritten in terms of the capacity-sorting margin of Section~\ref{sec:suffstat}: 
\[
\partial_k\E\underline{CS} = \frac{1}{\overline{w}}\left(p_L\,R_q(\theta_L)\frac{J_L}{\mathcal{G}_L} + p_H\,R_q(\theta_H)\frac{J_H}{\mathcal{G}_H}\right) = \frac{1}{\overline{w}}\sum_i p_i\,R_\theta(\theta_i)\,R_q(\theta_i) = \frac{1}{\overline{w}}\sum_i p_i\,w_i\,u(q_i)\,J_i.
\]
Increasing $k$ has two opposite effects. It raises the available quantity and therefore the available surplus terms. However, expanding $k$ also forces the designer to leave a larger informational rent to $\theta_H$, which mechanically reduces the transfer $t_H$ and therefore the available surplus $\mathbb{E}\underline{CS}$.

A CARA specification allows to characterize clearly when the low type loses from a capacity expansion, define $\lambda_L^\ast$ as the cutoff that solves \ref{dkECS} $=  0$. When $J_L < 0$, this cutoff is unique, and
\[
\partial_k ECS(\theta_L) < 0
\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
\lambda_L > \lambda_L^\ast = \frac{\theta_L p_H \lambda_H}{p_H(\theta_H - \theta_L) - \theta_L p_L} = -\frac{\theta_L p_H \lambda_H}{p_LJ_L}.
\]
Raising $\lambda_L$ moves $\Gamma_L$, and the effect of $\Gamma_L$ on the available surplus goes in the same direction: both channels are scaled by the low type's net contribution $p_L J_L$. The two effects reinforce each other so the net effect of $\lambda_L$ on \ref{dkECS} is unambiguously negative. Note that a larger $\theta_L$ raises the cutoff, making it harder to obtain \ref{dkECS}$< 0$. Intuitively, raising $\theta_L$ has two effects: a positive direct effect, through the gain in surplus from serving the low type and the reduction in $\theta_H - \theta_L$, and a negative effect since $\Gamma_L$ rises with $\theta_L$. In the two-type case, the positive effect always dominates. Figure~\ref{fig:TwoTypesWithinECS} illustrates individual surplus for both types as a function of capacity $k$, under three values of the welfare weight $\lambda_L$: below, at, and above the cutoff $\lambda_L^\ast$.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figure/TwoTypesWithin_ECS.png}
    \caption{Individual surplus $ECS_i(k)$ as a function of capacity $k$, for three welfare weights $\lambda_L \in \{0.1, 0.4, 0.9\}$ with $\lambda_H = 0.8$, where $\lambda_L = 0.4$ is the cutoff $\lambda_L^\ast$. Left panel: high type. Right panel: low type. The high type always gains from capacity expansion. The low type gains when $\lambda_L < \lambda_L^\ast$, is indifferent at the cutoff, and loses when $\lambda_L > \lambda_L^\ast$.}
    \label{fig:TwoTypesWithinECS}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Preference-sorting margin with two types.}
I now show that a change in redistributive preferences need not reallocate quantity toward the type whose welfare weight rises. Because I focus on the case $\beta=0$, $\mathcal{G}=\Gamma$. Consider a perturbation of redistributive preferences $(\Delta\lambda_L,\Delta\lambda_H)$, inducing a change in the average weight
$\Delta\tilde\lambda = p_L\,\Delta\lambda_L + p_H\,\Delta\lambda_H$.
The induced change in effective social weights is
\[
\Delta\Gamma_L
=
J_L\,\Delta\tilde\lambda
+
\frac{p_H}{p_L}\left(\theta_H-\theta_L\right)\Delta\lambda_H,
\qquad
\Delta\Gamma_H
=
\theta_H\,\Delta\tilde\lambda.
\]
These expressions make the two forces transparent. The first term, $\Delta\tilde\lambda J_i$, is the revenue motive: it revalues each type's contribution to available surplus at the new average weight. The second term is the redistributive motive: it revalues the informational rent flowing upward to higher types. Since no rent accrues above the high type, $\Delta\Lambda_H=0$, so $\Delta\Gamma_H$ depends only on the average perturbation. By contrast, $\Delta\Gamma_L$ contains both terms, because serving the low type also changes the informational rent left to $\theta_H$.

Two special cases follow directly. If only $\lambda_L$ changes, then $\Delta\tilde\lambda = p_L\,\Delta\lambda_L$ and $\Delta\lambda_H = 0$, so $\Delta\Gamma_H = p_L\theta_H\,\Delta\lambda_L$ and $\Delta\Gamma_L = p_L J_L\,\Delta\lambda_L$. The redistributive motive is then absent, and the perturbation operates purely through the revenue channel for both types. Under a mean-preserving perturbation, instead, $\Delta\tilde\lambda = 0$ and $\Delta\lambda_H = -(p_L/p_H)\,\Delta\lambda_L$, so $\Delta\Gamma_H = 0$ and $\Delta\Gamma_L = -(\theta_H-\theta_L)\,\Delta\lambda_L$. In that case, the high type's effective weight is unchanged and the entire perturbation operates through the redistributive motive of the low type alone.

Using \eqref{DeltaKKT-qMD} for $i=L$ and $i=H$ and subtracting gives
\[\tag{$\Delta q_{LH}$}\label{DeltaqLH}
\Gamma_L u_q(q_L)\,\Delta q_L - \Gamma_H u_q(q_H)\,\Delta q_H
=
\varepsilon\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_H)-R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_L)\right).
\]
Differentiating the capacity constraint yields $p_L\,\Delta q_L + p_H\,\Delta q_H = 0$, so that $\Delta q_H = -(p_L/p_H)\,\Delta q_L$ and the two quantities always move in opposite directions. Hence the allocation shifts toward the type whose effective weight experiences the larger proportional increase:
\[
\operatorname{sign}(\Delta q_L)
=
\operatorname{sign}\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_L)-R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_H)\right),
\qquad
\operatorname{sign}(\Delta q_H)
=
\operatorname{sign}\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_H)-R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_L)\right).
\]

The two special cases then yield sharp implications. When $\Delta\lambda_H=0$,
\[
R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_L) - R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_H)
=
\frac{J_L\,p_L\,\Delta\lambda_L}{\Gamma_L}
-
\frac{p_L\,\Delta\lambda_L}{\tilde\lambda} = 
-\frac{p_H(\theta_H-\theta_L)\lambda_H}{\Gamma_L\tilde\lambda}\,\Delta\lambda_L,
\]
so an increase in $\lambda_L$ alone lowers $q_L$. By contrast, when $\Delta\tilde\lambda=0$,
\[
R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_L) - R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_H)
=
-\frac{\theta_H-\theta_L}{\Gamma_L}\,\Delta\lambda_L,
\]
so a mean-preserving tilt toward the high type lowers $q_H$ and raises $q_L$. When only $\lambda_L$ rises, the perturbation works through the revenue motive, which raises the high type's effective weight proportionally more because it enters through $\theta_H$ rather than the smaller object $J_L$. Under a mean-preserving tilt, instead, the revenue motive is absent, so only the redistributive motive remains, which raises $\Gamma_L$ while leaving $\Gamma_H$ unchanged.

More generally, combining the expressions for $\Delta\Gamma_L$ and $\Delta\Gamma_H$ and simplifying yields
\[
R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_L)-R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_H)
=
\frac{p_H(\theta_H-\theta_L)}{\tilde\lambda\,\Gamma_L}
\left(\lambda_L\,\Delta\lambda_H-\lambda_H\,\Delta\lambda_L\right).
\]
Therefore,
\[
\operatorname{sign}(\Delta q_L)
=
\operatorname{sign}\left(\Delta\left(\frac{\lambda_H}{\lambda_L}\right)\right),
\qquad
\operatorname{sign}(\Delta q_H)
=
-\operatorname{sign}\left(\Delta\left(\frac{\lambda_H}{\lambda_L}\right)\right).
\]
Thus, the allocation shift depends on the change in the ratio $\lambda_H/\lambda_L$: the low type receives more quantity if and only if the relative increase in welfare weight is stronger for the high type. This ratio summarizes which of the two forces dominates: the redistributive motive that tilts the allocation toward the low type, or the revenue motive that tilts it toward the high type. The Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder} in the Appendix shows that this ratio characterization extends to the continuous-type case.

The two-type example makes the two sorting margins transparent, but some of its properties are specific to the binary environment. I now turn to the general analysis, which allows for a richer type space and does not rely on those special features.\footnote{For instance, the effective weight $\mathcal{G}_i$ is always increasing in $\theta$ with two types, and the highest type's quantity always rises with capacity. Appendix~\ref{app:three-nonmonotone-G} and Appendix~\ref{app:three-negative-qH} provide conditions under which these results can be reversed with three types.}

\subsection{Redistributive incidence of capacity expansion}\label{sec:surplus}
 
I now formally study the following question: who benefits from a marginal increase in capacity? Even though a larger $k_i$ relaxes scarcity, the surplus gains from this relaxation need not accrue to all types once incentive constraints and revenue extraction are taken into account. This subsection shows that the incidence of capacity expansion is pinned down by the sign of \ref{Eujmd}, the capacity-sorting margin, together with the ordering of types induced by $R_\theta(\theta)$.

The individual surplus change follows from \ref{ECSi}
%##########################################
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\E CS_i(\theta)}{\partial k_i} = \underbrace{\Bigg.\frac{\partial\E\underline{CS}_i}{\partial k_i}\Bigg.}_{ \text{fixed transfer} } +\underbrace{\Bigg. \int_{\underline\theta_i}^{\theta} \E_s\!\left[\frac{\partial q_i(\tilde\theta,s)}{\partial k_i} u\!\left(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s\right)\right]d\tilde\theta\Bigg. }_{\text{IC transfer}}. \tag{$\partial_k CS$}\label{partialkCS1}
\end{equation}
%##########################################
The derivative of the type-independent component is obtained from \ref{ECSi} while differentiating the first-order condition \ref{KKT-qMD} with respect to $k_i$ gives
%##########################################
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial q_i(\theta,s)}{\partial k_i} = w_i(\theta,s)\left( \frac{1}{\overline w_i(s)} -\frac{\partial\beta_i}{\partial k_i} \Bigl(\E^w_i[uJ\mid s] -u\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)J_i(\theta)\Bigr) \right). \tag{$\partial_k q_i$}\label{partialkq1}
\end{equation}
%##########################################

Two observations: when \ref{Rmdi-def} binds (the IR-constrained allocation), the boundary term us null: the entire surplus change is then carried by the IC rent in \ref{partialkCS1}. On the other hand,  when \ref{Rmdi-def} is slack (the IR-unconstrained allocation), then the second term in \ref{partialkq1} is null as $\beta_i = 0$. I assume the following so that the single-crossing structure is preserved after aggregation over states.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Multi}
\begin{assumption}\label{ass:mult}
$u(q,s) = \kappa(q)\zeta(s)$ for some functions $\kappa$ and $\zeta$ with $\kappa_q < 0$ and $\zeta_s > 0$.
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:mult}, $R_q = -\kappa(q)/\kappa_q(q)$ depends only on $q$ and not on $s$: a consumer's responsiveness is identical regardless of the realization of the common shock.

\begin{theorem}[Redistributive effects of capacity expansion]\label{thm:surplus}
Fix category \(i\), a budget requirement \(I_i>0\) and assume~\ref{ass:monotone} and~\ref{ass:mult} hold. Define the set of cutoffs 
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\[\tilde\Theta_i := \left\{\tilde\theta_i\in[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i] : \partial\E CS_i(\tilde\theta_i)/\partial k_i=0\right\}.\]
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\noindent\textbf{IR-unconstrained allocation.} If \ref{Eujmd} is positive, then an increase in \(k_i\) increases expected surplus for every type \(\theta\). If \ref{Eujmd} is negative, \(\tilde\Theta_i\) contains at most a unique cutoff; and if it exists expected surplus decreases for all \(\theta<\tilde\theta_i\) and increases for all \(\theta>\tilde\theta_i\).

\noindent\textbf{IR-constrained allocation.} The lowest type always lies on the boundary: \(\underline\theta_i\in\tilde\Theta_i\). If \(R_\theta(\theta)\) is increasing, a second cutoff \(\tilde\theta_i\in(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)\) may exist and when it does:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
    \item if \ref{Eujmd} is positive, expected surplus increases for all \(\theta\in(\underline\theta_i,\tilde\theta_i)\) and decreases for all \(\theta\ge\tilde\theta_i\);
    \item if \ref{Eujmd} is negative, expected surplus decreases for all \(\theta\in(\underline\theta_i,\tilde\theta_i)\) and increases for all \(\theta\ge\tilde\theta_i\).
\end{enumerate}
If \(R_\theta(\theta)\) is decreasing, the inequalities are reversed.
\end{theorem}

Expanding capacity is not always surplus-improving for all consumers, and the identity of who gains/loses depends on the regime and on the joint sign of \ref{Eujmd} and \(R_\theta(\theta)\). Importantly, the mechanism through which a capacity expansion determines the progressivity/regressivity of the capacity expansion is not the same across regimes. Under the IR-unconstrained regime, it operates through changes in the available surplus \ref{underlineECSi}, while under the IR-constrained regime it operates through changes in the optimal allocation \(q_i(\theta,s)\).

When the participation constraint is slack (\(\beta_i=0\)), the optimal allocation is increasing in \(k_i\) for all types in binding states. Hence, the IC component is always increasing. However, the sign of the type-independent component in \ref{partialkCS1} depends entirely on the sign of \ref{Eujmd}, which therefore summarizes the marginal redistributive effect of expanding capacity. If \ref{Eujmd}>0, both the common surplus component and the induced IC rents increase with \(k_i\), so all types gain. If \ref{Eujmd}<0, lower types lose while higher types gain, with a unique cutoff as stated in Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus}.\footnote{I show in the proof that the type-dependent part in \ref{partialkCS1} (the marginal IC rent) is increasing in \(\theta\). This monotonicity property yields the uniqueness of the cutoff in part~(ii), as well as the ordering of the types that experience a decrease in surplus.}

When the participation constraint binds, the fixed transfer is null, so the surplus effect of a capacity expansion is driven by the quantity margin. However, the monotonicity of quantities in $k_i$ may fail because a capacity expansion also changes the tightness of the financing constraint through the endogenous response of \(\beta_i\), as shown in \ref{partialkq1}. The bracketed term in that expression is the deviation of a type's marginal virtual surplus \(u(q_i(\theta,s),s)J_i(\theta)\) from its \(w_i\)-weighted average; its sign is therefore determined by the relative ranking of types according to marginal virtual surplus, which is captured by \(R_\theta(\theta)\). The sign of \(\partial \beta_i/\partial k_i\) is again governed by \ref{Eujmd}, which captures how capacity expansion tightens or loosens the budget constraint. Therefore, the condition on \ref{Eujmd} and $R_\theta(\theta)$ has a direct interpretation: suppose that expanding \(k_i\) makes the budget less tight, so that \(\beta_i\) decreases in \(k_i\). The need for revenue extraction then falls, and the designer lowers the marginal weight on types that generate higher marginal virtual surplus.

Assumption~\ref{ass:monotone} allows us to characterize cleanly the set of winners and losers from a capacity expansion. Without this requirement, the model may exhibit a richer set of cutoffs. In Appendix Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation}, I show that \ref{Eujmd} also governs the transition between the IR-unconstrained and IR-constrained regimes. This transition depends on whether the available surplus is single-peaked or single-dipped. Figure~\ref{fig:impl_surplus} summarizes this regime geometry by illustrating how the distributional incidence of a capacity expansion changes across regimes and across capacity levels, while the section~\ref{appsec:implementation-region} in the Appendix provides the full characterization. A direct implication is that the redistributive effect of additional capacity is not invariant to the installed capacity level: the same marginal expansion can be progressive at low \(k_i\) and regressive at high \(k_i\). I also provide a closed-form numerical simulation in the Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_redistribution_marg_k_uniform} illustrating how the identity of the winners and losers from a capacity expansion depends on the current level of capacity.

\subsection{Preference margin and optimal allocation}\label{sec:redist}

The previous section asked how a given redistributive objective shapes the surplus consequences of capacity expansion. I now reverse the question: holding $k_i$ fixed, how does a change of redistributive preferences reshape the optimal allocation? This allows us to study how incentive constraints shape the incidence of redistributive preference changes for a given capacity constraint, via the effective social weights.\footnote{This comparative static is also the building block of the across-category analysis in Section~\ref{sec:across}. Comparing the optimal allocations of two categories that differ marginally in preferences is equivalent to evaluating the local effect of a preference perturbation at fixed capacity.} 

\begin{lemma}[Reallocation under preference perturbations]\label{lem:realloc}
Fix category \(i\) and an across-category allocation \((k_i,I_i)\). For any local perturbation $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta)$ and any allocation regime,
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s) = R_q(\theta)\!\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta) - \E^w_i\!\left[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s\right]\right), \qquad s\in T_i.
\]
\end{lemma}

To interpret Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}, type $\theta$ receives more quantity if and only if its proportional change in effective social weight exceeds the proportional average. The intuition is the following. From \ref{DeltaKKT-qMD}, the linearization of
the pointwise first-order condition yields
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s)
=
w_i(\theta,s)\bigl(u(q_i,s)\,\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)-\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)\bigr)
=
R_q(\theta)\!\left(R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)-\mathbb E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]\right).
\label{Deltaq}\tag{$\Delta$q}\]
That is the condition of receiving more or less allocation is determined by whether the preference change raises the marginal utility valued at the effect social weights of type $\theta$ by more or less than the equilibrium adjustment in the scarcity rent.\footnote{ The equivalence between the two equations stems from the observation that the aggregate proportional change in the effective social weight is also the proportional change in the scarcity rent
\[
\frac{\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)}{\varepsilon_i(s)}
=
\E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right].
\]
} Since capacity is fixed, the reallocation is zero-sum across types within each binding state: $\mathbb E_i\!\left[\Delta q_i(\theta,s)\right]=0$ for each $s\in T_i$.

The sorting effect of the optimal allocation induced by a preference perturbation is different than the direction of the perturbation itself. Appendix Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation} shows that the behavioral response $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$ can be non-monotone in types, so a tilt of preferences toward higher or lower types does not mechanically translate into a monotone quantity reallocation. This non-monotonicity of the optimal allocation stems from the fact that the element that drives its response is the proportional change $R_{\G}(\theta)$ and not the absolute change $\Delta \G_i(\theta)$. While a change in preferences may induce a monotonic variation in the absolute change $\Delta \mathcal G_i(\theta)$ along types, this need not be the case for the proportional change $R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)$. To see this, consider the extreme types $\underline{\theta}_i$ and $\overline{\theta}_i$ and a perturbation of an optimal allocation in the IR-unconstrained regime. At $\underline{\theta}_i$, the social value of the information rent is the information rent valued at the expected welfare weights: $\gamma_i(\theta)\tilde\lambda_i$, so the second and third terms cancel: $\G_i( \underline{\theta}_i)= \underline{\theta}_i  \tilde\lambda_i$. At $\overline{\theta}_i$, the information rent is absent: $\gamma_i(\theta)=0$, so the second and third terms are null: $\G_i(\overline{\theta}_i)= \overline{\theta}_i  \tilde\lambda_i$. The perturbation yeilds similar values:  $\Delta \G_i( \underline{\theta}_i)= \underline{\theta}_i  \Delta \tilde\lambda_i$ and $\Delta \G_i(\overline{\theta}_i)= \overline{\theta}_i  \Delta \tilde\lambda_i$. Therefore, while I can have $\Delta \G_i(\underline{\theta}_i) \neq \Delta \G_i(\overline{\theta}_i)$, I always have $R_\G(\underline{\theta}_i) = R_\G(\overline{\theta}_i)$ for any perturbation of redistributive preferences. In particular, the second part of Appendix Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation} further shows that, under mean-preserving perturbations ($\Delta \tilde\lambda_i=0$), the induced gains and losses must necessarily occur at the type boundaries in the opposite direction from the interior reallocation, whatever the direction of preference changes.

Similarly to the capacity extension expression \ref{partialkCS1}, the individual surplus change follows from \ref{ECSi}
%##########################################
\begin{equation*}
  \Delta CS_i(\theta) = \Delta \E\underline{CS}_i + \int_{\underline\theta_i}^{\theta} \E_s\!\left[\Delta q_i(\tilde\theta,s) u\!\left(q_i(\tilde\theta,s),s\right)\right]d\tilde\theta
\end{equation*}
Using lemma \ref{lem:realloc}, the following expression characterizes the change of available surplus \ref{Eujmd} due to a perturbation of preferences
\[
\Delta\E\underline{CS}_i =\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\,R_\theta(\theta)\,R_q(\theta)\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s\right]\right)\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right].
\]
\begin{proposition}[Surplus effects of redistributive preference perturbations]\label{prop:surplus-redist}
Assume Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA} and unimodality of $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$. Define the set of cutoffs
\[
\tilde\Theta_i^\Delta := \left\{\tilde\theta_i\in[\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i] : \Delta\E CS_i(\tilde\theta_i)=0\right\}.
\]
\noindent\textbf{IR-unconstrained allocation.} There exist at most three cutoffs in $(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)$. The pattern of $\Delta\E CS_i(\theta)$ is jointly determined by two signs: $\mathrm{Cov}_{i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})$, which governs the starting value,
\[\sign\!\left(\Delta\E CS_i(\underline\theta_i)\right)=\sign\!\left(\mathrm{Cov}_{i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})\right),\]
and $R_{\G}$, which governs the interior shape. If $R_{\G}\ge 0$ for all $\theta$: expected surplus follows the pattern $+,-,+,-$ when $\mathrm{Cov}_{i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})>0$, and $-,+,-$ when $\mathrm{Cov}_{i}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}})<0$. If $R_{\G}\le 0$ for all $\theta$, the starting value and all interior inequalities are reversed.

\noindent\textbf{IR-constrained allocation.} The lowest type always lies on the boundary: $\underline\theta_i\in\tilde\Theta_i^\Delta$. There exist at most two additional cutoffs $\theta_1<\theta_2$ in $(\underline\theta_i,\overline\theta_i)$. If $R_{\G}\ge 0$ for all $\theta$, expected surplus decreases for $\theta\in(\underline\theta_i,\theta_1)$, increases for $\theta\in(\theta_1,\theta_2)$, and decreases for $\theta\in(\theta_2,\overline\theta_i)$. If $R_{\G}\le 0$ for all $\theta$, the inequalities are reversed.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:surplus-redist} delivers a sharp redistributive policy result. Even when the direction of a preference shift is unambiguous (as in Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}), the resulting surplus changes are generically non-monotone across types.\footnote{Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA} is used only to sharpen this result by delivering a closed-form expression for the boundary term \ref{underlineECSi}. The unimodality of \(R_{\mathcal G}\) is used only to limit the number of sign changes of \(\Delta \E CS_i(\theta)\). If \(R_{\mathcal G}\) is not unimodal, additional crossings may arise, so the proposition should be read as a conservative characterization of the possible alternating surplus regions.}

In both regimes, the origin of the non-monotonicity in the change in surplus is the same: the individual surplus change combines an IC-rent channel and a level channel, and neither of them follows the direction of the preference perturbation mechanically. Figure~\ref{fig:surplus-redist} provides a simple geometry illustration of the proposition.

The first channel is the IC-rent channel. By construction, informational rents are built from the allocation received by lower types. As a result, when the perturbation changes the quantity assigned to some type \(\tilde\theta\), this change is inherited by all higher types through the rent formula. The shape of the IC-rent term is therefore determined by the shape of \(\Delta q_i(\theta,s)\) across types. Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc} shows that this behavioral response is itself governed by the gap between the proportional change in effective social weight, \(R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)\), and its equilibrium average. Since \(R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)\) is non-monotone, the induced quantity response is also generically non-monotone. Hence, even when the underlying preference shift is monotone, the associated IC-rent effect on surplus need not be monotone across types.

The second channel is a level effect, whose source depends on the regime. When the participation constraint is slack, the change in surplus contains both the fixed component $\Delta \E \underline{CS}_i$ and the IC-rent term. The fixed component is common across types and depends on how the perturbation changes the total surplus available for redistribution. In the two-type example, differentiating \eqref{underlineECSTwo} and using \eqref{DeltaqLH} yields
\[
\Delta\E\underline{CS}_i
=
\frac{\varepsilon}{\alpha}p_Lp_H
\left(R_\theta(\theta_H)-R_\theta(\theta_L)\right)
\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_H)-R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta_L)\right)
=
\frac{\varepsilon}{\alpha}\mathrm{Cov}(R_\theta,R_{\mathcal{G}}).
\]
If a consumer who receives the greatest proportional change also participates most in the revenue, then the available surplus for all consumers increases. When participation binds, the lowest type remains at the boundary, so that \(\Delta \mathbb E CS_i(\underline\theta_i)=0\). In that case, the level effect is pinned down by the endpoint condition, and the entire surplus profile is shaped by the accumulation of the non-monotone quantity response through the IC formula.

Taken together, these two channels explain why the surplus effect of a redistributive preference perturbation is generically non-monotone across types. A shift in preferences toward higher or lower types does not translate mechanically into a monotone pattern of winners and losers, because the perturbation operates through the endogenous reallocation of quantities and through the way informational rents accumulate along the type space.

 I illustrate in Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_withinperturbation} in the Appendix a numerical simulation. I assume an initial category characterized by utilitarian preferences, and then compute two (mean-preserving) perturbations in welfare weights $\lambda_i(\theta)$: case A exhibits higher weights on higher types, while case B exhibits higher weights on lower types. In the second panel, $R_\G(\theta)$ is positive for case A and negative for case B, following Lemma~\ref {lem:preferenceorder}. The third panel shows how the optimal allocation responds to the perturbation: for instance the allocation of $\overline{\theta}_i$ decrease in case A and increase in case B while $\lambda(\theta)$ is the highest in case A and the lowest in case B. The fourth panel confirms the illustration in Figure~\ref{fig:surplus-redist}.

\section{Redistributive Allocation Across Observable Categories}\label{sec:across}

Having characterized the constrained mechanism for a given pair $(k_i,I_i)$, I now turn to the designer's problem of allocating total capacity and total revenue requirements across categories. The previous section provides the objects that govern this outer allocation. The question here is how those same forces translate into differences in the marginal values of capacity and revenue across categories, and hence into the optimal assignment of capacity and budget contributions across observable groups.

\subsection{Characterization}

Since the objective and category-level constraints are separable across categories, with interaction only through the aggregate constraints on capacity and revenue, the planner's problem decomposes into category-specific value functions \(V_i(k_i,I_i)\) and an outer allocation problem over \(\{k_i,I_i\}_{i\in N}\). Given the value functions of the single-category problem \(V_i(k_i,I_i)\), the designer chooses \(\{k_i,I_i\}_{i\in N}\) to solve
\[
\max_{\{k_i,I_i\}_{i\in N}} \sum_i\mu_i\,V_i(k_i,I_i)
\]
subject to
\[
\sum_i\mu_i k_i = k,
\qquad
\sum_i\mu_i I_i = I(k),
\qquad
k_i\ge 0,\quad I_i\ge 0 \quad \forall i\in N.
\]
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
where $V_i(k_i, I_i)$ is defined as
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\[
V_i(k_i, I_i) \;:=\; \max_{q_i(\theta,s)} \;\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[\Gamma_i(\theta)\,U\!\left(q_i(\theta,s),s\right)\right] - \tilde\lambda_i I_i
\]
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
subject to incentive compatibility and the category-specific constraints. By the envelope theorem, the two partial derivatives of $V_i$ are
\[
\partial_{k_i} V_i(k_i,I_i) \;=\; \overline \varepsilon_i \; , \qquad \partial_{I_i} V_i(k_i,I_i) \;=\; -\tilde\lambda_i - \beta_i(k_i,I_i).
\]
where $\overline \varepsilon_i =\E_s\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right]$.

The envelope decomposition shows that the problem is governed by only two marginal objects. First,
\[
\Pi_i(k_i,I_i) \;:=\; \partial_{k_i} V_i(k_i,I_i) \;=\; \E_s\!\left[\varepsilon_i(s)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right] \;=\; \E_{(s,i)}\!\left[ u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\G_i(\theta)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right], \]
is the expected shadow value of relaxing the category-specific capacity constraint. As in canonical peak-load pricing, the marginal value of an additional unit of capacity is equal to the expected scarcity rent. Equivalently, using the first-order condition \ref{KKT-qMD}, it can be written as the expected aggregate marginal utility generated by extra capacity. The difference relative to the canonical model is that this marginal utility is here weighted by the effective social weight \(\G_i(\theta)\), rather than by $\theta$. This IC-adjusted weighting will be crucial for the comparative statics below.\footnote{Note that \(\Pi_i(k_i,I_i)\) is strictly positive whenever category \(i\) is capacity-constrained with positive probability, since \(\varepsilon_i(s)>0\) in every on-peak state.}

Second,
\[
\partial_{I_i}V_i(k_i,I_i)
=
-\tilde\lambda_i-\beta_i(k_i,I_i),
\]
is the effective marginal social cost of requiring one additional unit of revenue from category \(i\). The term \(\beta_i\) captures the canonical marginal cost of tightening the budget, while \(\tilde\lambda_i\) captures the additional social cost induced by redistributive concerns.

\begin{lemma}[Across-category allocation]\label{lem:outer}
The outer problem admits at least one solution, and the set of optimal allocations is convex. The optimality conditions are, for each $i \in N$,
\[
\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast) \;=\; \zeta_k, \qquad \tilde\lambda_i + \beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast) \;=\; -\zeta_I + \xi_i,
\]
with complementary slackness $\xi_i I_i^\ast = 0$ for all $i$ and where $\zeta_k, \zeta_I \in \mathbb{R}$ denote the multipliers on the capacity and revenue equality constraints, and $\xi_i \ge 0$ the multiplier on $I_i \ge 0$.\footnote{The Inada conditions on $u(\cdot)$ ensure that $k_i>0$ at the optimum, so the multiplier on $k_i \ge 0$ is zero.}
\end{lemma}

The two optimality conditions have the usual economic interpretation. The designer allocates capacity until the marginal value of every categories, measure by $\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$, is the same across all categories. He equalizes the effective marginal social cost of revenue extraction $\tilde\lambda_i + \beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$ across categories that contribute to the budget.



\subsection{Ranking of the optimal allocation}\label{sec:rankingall}

I now study how redistributive preferences shape the allocation of capacity and revenue across categories. I study two rankings: the capacity ranking, which determines how much capacity each category receives, and the revenue ranking, which determines how much each category contributes to the budget, the latter decomposing into an extensive margin governing which categories contribute at all, and an intensive margin governing how much each contributing category pays. I begin with a local comparison between two nearby categories, representing category \(j\) as a marginal variation of category \(i\), and then use this comparison to derive a global ordering along a path of redistributive preferences. Throughout, all categories differ in their welfare weight functions \(\lambda_i(\theta)\) and share the same support \(\Theta\).

\paragraph{Capacity ranking.} 
Using the first-order conditions in Lemma~\ref{lem:outer}, consider the induced difference in the marginal value of capacity around category \(i\):
\[
\Delta \Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)
=
\E_{(s,i)}\![
(
\underbrace{u_q(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\G_i(\theta)\,\Delta q_i(\theta,s)
}_{\text{behavioral effect}}+
\underbrace{u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,\Delta \G_i(\theta)}_{\text{weight effect}}
)
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}
].
\]
This expression separates the effect of a preference variation into two margins: a behavioral effect, through the induced change in the optimal allocation \(q_i(\theta,s)\), and a direct reweighting effect, through the change in effective social weights \(\Delta \G_i(\theta)\). By Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc} and \ref{Deltaq}, the allocation response has the same decomposition. At the aggregate level, however, the two direct reweighting terms offset each other. What remains is the average proportional difference in effective social weights $\E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]$.

\begin{theorem}[Capacity ordering]\label{thm:cat-order}
Suppose category \(j\) differs from a baseline category \(i\) only through a marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences. Fix the baseline optimum \((k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\). Then, to first order in \(\Delta\lambda\), the following capacity ranking holds
\[
k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\E_s\!\left[
\varepsilon_i(s)\,
\E_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal G}\mid s\right]
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}
\right]>0.
\]
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the condition reduces to $k_j^\ast>k_i^\ast$ if and only if $\E_i[R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)]>0$. 
\end{theorem}

The theorem provides a direct outer-margin counterpart to the single-category analysis. The same object \(R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)\) that governed the reallocation of quantities and the surplus effects of preference differences within a category now determines which category receives more capacity at the outer optimum. Therefore, the relevant comparison is not which category has the larger average effective weight nor the absolute size of the difference in redistributive preferences, but whether category \(j\)'s advantage in effective weights is concentrated on types for which category \(i\)'s effective weight is low.

I provide, in Figure \ref{fig:appendix_two_scenarios_six_panels} in the Appendix, simple numerical examples of redistributive preferences around the utilitarian benchmark, that is, \(\lambda_i(\theta)=1\). These examples show that the ranking induced by the absolute effect, \(\E_{i}\!\left[\Delta \G_i(\theta)\right]\), and the ranking induced by the proportional effect, \(\E_{i}\!\left[R_{\G,i}(\theta)\right]\), can be non-trivial and may even go in opposite directions. In two cases, I consider pairs of categories \(i\) and \(j\) whose redistributive weights \(\lambda_i(\theta)\) and \(\lambda_j(\theta)\) both deviate from the constant benchmark \(1\), while all other parameters are held fixed. I show that even when category \(i\) has a higher average effective weight relative to the utilitarian benchmark whereas category \(j\) has a lower one, so that \(\E_{i}\!\left[\Delta \G_i(\theta)\right] > 0 > \E_{\theta_j}\!\left[\Delta \G_j(\theta)\right]\), it is still possible to have \(\E_{i}\!\left[R_{\G,i}(\theta)\right] < 0 < \E_{\theta_j}\!\left[R_{\G,j}(\theta)\right]\).

I now derive global implications of Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order} by imposing additional structure on redistributive preferences. The theorem provides a local comparison between nearby categories; the role of the next assumption is to place all categories along a common ordered path, so that these local comparisons can be aggregated into a global ranking.

\renewcommand{\theassumption}{Order}
\begin{assumption}[Ordered preference path]\label{ass:ordered-path}
For each adjacent pair \(i-1\) and \(i\), there exists a positive \(C^1\) path $(t,\theta)\mapsto \lambda(t,\theta) \text{ on }[0,1]\times\Theta$ such that \(\lambda(0,\theta)=\lambda_{i-1}(\theta)\) and \(\lambda(1,\theta)=\lambda_i(\theta)\). Along this path, two conditions hold for every \(t\in[0,1]\):

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
\item the average redistributive weight is weakly increasing, $\partial_t \tilde\lambda(t)\ge 0$, where $\tilde\lambda(t):=\E_{\theta}[\lambda(t,\theta)]$,
\item the relative tilt of redistributive weights is toward higher types, in the sense that $\partial_t \log \lambda(t,\theta)$ is weakly increasing in \(\theta\) on \(\Theta\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{assumption}
\renewcommand{\theassumption}{\arabic{assumption}}

Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path} orders categories along a common redistributive path. Condition~(i) requires average welfare weights to be weakly increasing along the ordering. Condition~(ii) requires this increase to be proportionally stronger for higher types. Together, these two restrictions allow the local comparison in Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order} to be iterated across adjacent categories. By Appendix Lemma~\ref{lem:preferenceorder}, they imply a monotone ordering of effective social weights that carries over to the equilibrium capacity profile.

\begin{corollary}[Capacity ordering along an ordered preference path]\label{cor:ordered-path}
Consider an equilibrium allocation \(\{k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast,\beta_i^\ast\}_{i\in N}\). Under Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path}, the equilibrium capacity profile is globally weakly monotone:
\[
k_1^\ast\le\cdots\le k_n^\ast.
\]
\end{corollary}

The corollary provides a sharp economic ranking of categories by capacity allocation. Capacity depends on both the average welfare weight $\tilde\lambda_i$ and the distribution of redistributive preferences across types. Categories that place proportionally more weight on higher types have a larger $\G_i(\theta)$, not only through the average term \(\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i\), but also through the informational-rent component \(\Lambda_i(\theta)\). Under Assumption~\ref{ass:ordered-path}, these two forces move in the same direction. As a result, categories that are higher in the redistributive ordering optimally receive a larger capacity.

In Figure \ref{fig:appendix_N_categories_six_panels} in the Appendix, I provide a numerical illustration of the sufficient condition. I compute the optimal allocation of \(k_i\) across 50 categories for a given aggregate capacity \(k\). I consider two cases. In both, categories are ordered so that, between two adjacent categories \(i\) and \(i+1\), the average welfare weight is strictly increasing. In the first case, redistributive preferences are gradually tilted toward higher types, whereas in the second they are gradually tilted toward lower types. For comparability, I assume that, for a given category \(i\), the average welfare weight is the same across the two cases. I show that, in the second case, capacity may be increasing across some initial categories, but that the ranking eventually reverses. In the first case, which satisfies the condition of the corollary, the optimal allocation \(k_i^\ast\) is clearly strictly increasing.

\paragraph{Revenue ranking.} To rank categories in terms of revenue requirements, I again use the first-order condition from Lemma~\ref{lem:outer}. For a local comparison around category \(i\), a first-order expansion of the outer condition for category \(j\) gives
\[
0
=
\Delta\tilde\lambda
+
\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,\Delta k
+
\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial I_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\,\Delta I
+
\Delta_{\lambda}\beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast),
\]
where \(\Delta_{\lambda}\beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\) denotes the direct effect of the preference perturbation on the category-specific shadow cost at fixed \((k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\). This expression shows that the comparison of equilibrium revenue requirements across categories depends on two forces: the difference in average welfare weights, \(\Delta\tilde\lambda\), and the endogenous adjustment of the shadow cost \(\beta_i\), which reflects both the induced change in capacity and the direct effect of the preference perturbation. The single-category analysis provides the sign of \(\partial_{k_i}\beta_i\) and characterizes \(\Delta_{\lambda}\beta_i\), yielding the following result.

\begin{proposition}[Revenue ordering]\label{prop:revenueordering}
Consider an equilibrium allocation $\{k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast\}_{i\in N}$. Define the two endogenous subsets 
\[
N^+:=\{i\in N:\beta_i^\ast>0\},
\qquad
N^0:=\{i\in N:\beta_i^\ast=0\},
\]
which partition \(N\) into categories that contribute to the budget and categories that do not contribute to the budget (either may be empty). Then:

\noindent\textbf{Extensive margin.} At any equilibrium, $\max_{i \in N^+} \tilde\lambda_i < \min_{i \in N^0} \tilde\lambda_i$, so \(N^+\) and \(N^0\) are strictly separated in terms of average welfare weights, regardless of the within-type structure of preferences.

\noindent\textbf{Intensive margin.} Fix a baseline category $i \in N^+$, and let category $j \in N^+$ be obtained from $i$ through a marginal perturbation of redistributive preferences with $\Delta\tilde\lambda:=\tilde\lambda_j-\tilde\lambda_i$ and $\Delta k:=k_j^\ast-k_i^\ast>0$. Then to the first order
\[
I_j^\ast<I_i^\ast \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad -\underbrace{\Bigg.\left(\Delta k\frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)+\Delta_{\lambda}\beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)\right)\Bigg.}_{\text{revenue effect}}<\underbrace{\Bigg.\Delta\tilde\lambda\Bigg.}_{\text{preference effect}}.
\]
where the sign of $\Delta k \frac{\partial \beta_i}{\partial k_i}(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$ is determined by the sign of \ref{Eujmd} and $\Delta_{\lambda}\beta_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)$ is the  first-order effect of the preference perturbation on the $\beta_i$. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA},  and using the first-order expression for $\Delta k$,this reduces to
\[
I_j^\ast<I_i^\ast \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \underbrace{\Bigg.\frac{\E_i[R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)]}{\E_i[R_\theta^2(\theta)]}\Bigg.}_{\Delta k} \underbrace{\Bigg.\E_i[R_\theta(\theta)]\Bigg. }_{\partial_k\beta}+ 
\underbrace{\Bigg.\frac{
\Cov_i\!\left(
R_{\theta}(\theta),R_{\mathcal G}(\theta)
\right)
}{
\Var_i\!\left(
R_{\theta}(\theta)
\right)
}\Bigg.}_{\Delta_\lambda \beta} < \Delta\tilde\lambda.
\]
\end{proposition}

The proposition completes the characterization on the revenue side. The extensive margin is governed solely by average welfare weights: categories with sufficiently low average redistributive weight contribute to the budget, whereas categories with sufficiently high average redistributive weight do not. Within the contributing set, however, equilibrium revenue requirements depend not only on average welfare weights but also on the category's ability to raise revenue, through both the induced change in equilibrium capacity and the direct effect of the preference perturbation on the shadow cost of revenue.

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, this tradeoff becomes especially transparent, because the revenue-ordering condition can be expressed entirely in terms of the same sufficient statistics that govern the previous sorting results. The capacity difference is summarized by marginal capacity value and captured by the proportional change $\E_i[R_\G(\theta)]$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:cat-order}). The effect of a larger capacity on the shadow cost of revenue is summarized by the distribution of virtual surplus across types  $\E_i[R_\theta(\theta)]$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus}). Finally, the direct effect of the preference perturbation on the ability to raise revenue is captured by the covariance term, as in Proposition~\ref{prop:surplus-redist}. Under CARA, the revenue ranking is therefore entirely governed by the sorting of consumers along these two objects.

\section{Optimal investment}
The third and final step endogenizes total investment. I now let \(k\) itself be a choice variable. The designer solves
\[
\max_{k\geq 0} W(k),
\]
where \(W(k)\) denotes the outer value function once total revenue is tied to the endogenous investment cost \(I(k)\), with \(I\) strictly increasing and convex.

Increasing \(k\) relaxes scarcity in states where capacity binds, but it also requires additional revenue to finance the larger investment. The optimal investment level is therefore pinned down by equating the marginal value of relaxing the capacity constraint with the marginal financing cost of additional capacity. As shown in Proposition~\ref{lem:investment}, this condition is governed by the same objects, \(\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\), \(\Pi_i\), and \(\tilde\lambda^\ast\), that organize the previous analysis.

\begin{lemma}[Optimal investment and the incomplete-information peak-load rule]\label{lem:investment}
The investment problem \(\max_{k\geq 0} W(k)\) admits a unique interior solution \(k^\ast > 0\). The optimal investment level satisfies
\[
I'(k^\ast)
= \frac{1}{\mu^+\tilde\lambda^\ast}\sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\,\Pi_i(k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast)=
\frac{1}{\mu^+}
\sum_{i\in N^+} \mu_i\,
\E_{(s,i)}\!\left[
u(q_i(\theta,s),s)\,
\frac{\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}\,
\mathbf{1}_{\{s\in T_i\}}
\right],
\]
where \(\mu^+ := \sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\) denotes the mass of contributing categories and \(\tilde\lambda^\ast := -\zeta_I > 0\) is the equilibrium marginal cost of funds. Moreover,
\[
\max_{i \in N^+} \tilde\lambda_i < \tilde\lambda^\ast \leq \min_{i \in N^0} \tilde\lambda_i.
\]
\end{lemma}

Lemma~\ref{lem:investment} is the incomplete-information counterpart of the classic peak-load investment rule. In the complete-information benchmark with redistributive preferences, incentive constraints play no role. There is no information rent, so \(J_i(\theta)=\theta\) and \(\Lambda_i(\theta)=0\). Hence the normalized incomplete-information object collapses to its complete-information counterpart:
\[
\frac{\mathcal G_i(\theta)}{\tilde\lambda^\ast}
\; \longrightarrow \;
\theta,
\]
which yields equation~\ref{eq:FB-k1} in the Appendix.

This stands in contrast with the literature on peak-load pricing under incomplete information but without redistributive concerns. Under utilitarian preferences, incomplete information distorts welfare away from the complete-information benchmark when investment is off the optimum, but this distortion disappears at the optimal investment level \(k^\ast\). In our framework, by contrast, the gap relative to complete information persists even at the optimum.

Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_OptimalFBSB} in the Appendix provides a numerical illustration of welfare \(W(k)\) under four scenarios: utilitarian versus redistributive preferences, and complete versus incomplete information, holding the primitives fixed. The figure shows that, under redistributive preferences, the complete- and incomplete-information welfare profiles differ even at the optimum. By contrast, under utilitarian preferences, the two profiles coincide at the optimal investment level \(k^\ast\).

I conclude by using the capacity- and preference-sorting margins developed in the previous analysis to characterize how redistributive preference perturbations affect both the optimal investment level and the incidence of a global capacity expansion across categories.

\begin{proposition}[Investment response to redistributive preference perturbations]\label{prop:investment}
Assume Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}. Suppose each category \(i\) becomes a new category \(j\) through a marginal and monotonic mean-preserving perturbation of \(\lambda_i(\theta)\), and let \(\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast\) denote the first-order change in the equilibrium marginal cost of funds. Then the induced change in optimal investment satisfies
\[
W''(k^\ast)\,\Delta k^\ast
=
-\underbrace{\Bigg.\sum_i\mu_i\,\Pi_i\,\mathbb E_i[R_{\mathcal G,i}]\Bigg.}_{\text{average effect}}
\qquad +  \qquad \underbrace{ \Bigg.I'(k^\ast)\,\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast\Bigg.}_{\text{sorting effect}}
\]
where
\[
\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast
=
-
\frac{
\sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\,\overline\varepsilon_i\,
\Cov_i\!\left(
R_{\theta,i}(\theta),R_{\mathcal G,i}(\theta)
\right)
}{
\sum_{i\in N^+}\mu_i\,\overline\varepsilon_i\,
\Var_i\!\left(
R_{\theta,i}(\theta)
\right)
}.
\]
Here \(W''(k^\ast)<0\) and \(\overline\varepsilon_i \geq 0\).
\end{proposition}

The proposition shows that redistributive preference perturbations affect optimal investment through the same two forces as in the previous analysis. The first operates through the preference-sorting margin, by changing the category-level marginal value of capacity \(\Pi_i\) as allocations are reshaped through \(R_{\mathcal G,i}\). The second operates through the capacity-sorting margin, by changing the equilibrium marginal cost of funds \(\tilde\lambda^\ast\), with the sign again governed by the covariance between the redistributive shift and the type profile. At the aggregate level, these two effects jointly determine whether optimal investment rises or falls.

I illustrate this result in Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_exampleoptimallevel} in the Appendix, where I simulate how the optimal investment level \(k^\ast\) responds to redistributive preference changes. I start from a benchmark with two utilitarian categories, \(i\) and \(i+1\). I then iteratively perturb preferences and recompute the optimal investment level for each successive pair of categories: in the first step, category \(i\) remains utilitarian while category \(i+1\) is a marginal perturbation of \(i\); in the next step, category \(i+1\) becomes the new baseline and category \(i+2\) is constructed as a perturbation of it; and so on. I consider two cases. In the first, redistributive preferences are increasingly tilted toward higher types; in the second, they are increasingly tilted toward lower types. In the numerical examples I consider, the sorting and average effects move in the same direction under monotone mean-preserving perturbations, producing a clear opposite shift in optimal investment.

Finally, I connect a marginal increase in aggregate capacity \(k\) to its redistributive incidence across categories and types. While I do not provide a formal characterization here, the previous results already identify the two objects that govern this effect: the incidence on a single category of \(k_i\), and the response of equilibrium category capacities \(k_i^\ast\) to a change in aggregate capacity \(k\). This yields a simple condition that captures the main economic mechanism at work. Consider a neighborhood of an interior optimum in which the set of active and contributing categories remains unchanged. Then
\[
\frac{d\,\E CS_i(\theta)}{dk}
=
\frac{\partial \E CS_i(\theta)}{\partial k_i}\,
\frac{dk_i^\ast}{dk}.
\]
The first term is the single-category incidence characterized in Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus}. The second term captures how a marginal increase in aggregate capacity is redistributed across categories. As shown in Appendix Lemma~\ref{lem:dkidk}, this term depends on two objects. The first is the category-specific expression
\[
\Omega_i^{-1}\left(
\mathbb \E_{(s,i)}^w[uJ\mid s]
-I'(k^\ast)
\right), \quad \text{where} \quad \Omega_i
:=
\E_s\!\left[\frac{1}{\overline w_i(s)}\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right].
\]
The first term measures whether the marginal revenue \ref{Eujmd} for category \(i\) is high or low relative to the marginal investment cost. The second is the corresponding aggregate expression,
\[\tag{$d\lambda^\ast$}\label{dlambda}
\sum_{j\in N } \mu_j\,\Omega_j^{-1}
\left(
\E_{(s,j)}^w[uJ\mid s]
-I'(k^\ast)
\right),
\]
which governs how the equilibrium marginal cost of funds \(\tilde\lambda^\ast\) responds to a change in aggregate capacity.\footnote{In this sense, the aggregate response mirrors the previous proposition on \(\Delta\tilde\lambda^\ast\): what matters, again, is how the marginal revenue compares with the relevant aggregate benchmark.}

This yields a simple interpretation of the redistributive effect of a global capacity expansion. The allocation of additional capacity depends on both the category-level comparison between \ref{Eujmd} and \(I'(k^\ast)\), and the corresponding aggregate object \ref{dlambda}. The sign of \ref{dlambda} determines the direction of the reallocation: if \ref{dlambda} is positive, categories with relatively high marginal revenue receive more of the expansion; if \ref{dlambda} is negative, categories with relatively low marginal revenue receive more. Moreover, as shown in the Appendix and used in Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus}, \ref{Eujmd} moves monotonically with \(k_i\): depending on \(R_\theta\) and \(R_q\), it may switch from positive to negative, or from negative to positive. This yields a qualitative prediction based on these two ratios and the sign of \ref{dlambda}.

To illustrate, take two categories \(i\) and \(j\), and assume \(R'_\theta(\theta)>0\), so that in both categories the virtual term \(u(q_i(\theta,s),s)J_i(\theta)\) is increasing in \(\theta\). Suppose that, at the initial allocation, \(k_j^\ast \gg k_i^\ast\), and that \ref{Eujmd} is positive for the low-capacity category \(i\) but negative for the high-capacity category \(j\). If \ref{dlambda} is positive, the global expansion reallocates relatively more capacity toward category \(i\), compressing category-level allocations and generating convergence. If \ref{dlambda} is negative, the opposite occurs: the expansion reallocates relatively more capacity toward category \(j\), widening category-level allocations and generating divergence. Figure~\ref{fig:general_surplus} illustrates this mechanism.

\subsection{Why the two-type logic does not extend to three types}
\label{app:twovsthree}

\subsubsection{Monotonicity of effective weights}
\label{app:three-nonmonotone-G}
In this section, I show that in the two-type case, $\mathcal{G}_H > \mathcal{G}_L$ holds for any $\beta \geq 0$ and any welfare weights $\lambda_L, \lambda_H > 0$. This does not extend to three types: with three types $\theta_L < \theta_M < \theta_H$ and equal probabilities $p_i = 1/3$, monotonicity of effective weights at any adjacent pair need not hold.

\noindent \textit{Two types.} Using $J_H - J_L = (\theta_H - \theta_L)/p_L > 0$ and $\Gamma_H - \Gamma_L = \lambda_L(\theta_H - \theta_L) > 0$,
\[\mathcal{G}_H - \mathcal{G}_L = (\Gamma_H - \Gamma_L) + \beta(J_H - J_L) = (\lambda_L + \beta/p_L)(\theta_H - \theta_L) > 0.\]
\textit{Three types.} Assume equal spacing $\theta_M - \theta_L = \theta_H - \theta_M = d$. A direct computation gives
\[\mathcal{G}_H - \mathcal{G}_M = d\cdot\frac{1}{3}\left[2(\lambda_L + \lambda_M) - \lambda_H + 6\beta\right],\]
\[\mathcal{G}_M - \mathcal{G}_L = d\cdot\frac{1}{3}\left[2(\lambda_L + \lambda_H) - \lambda_M + 6\beta\right].\]
At $\beta = 0$, each condition reduces to a simple primitive restriction:
\[\mathcal{G}_H > \mathcal{G}_M \iff \lambda_H < 2(\lambda_L + \lambda_M), \qquad \mathcal{G}_M > \mathcal{G}_L \iff \lambda_M < 2(\lambda_L + \lambda_H).\]
Either condition can fail depending on which welfare weight is disproportionately large relative to the other two.

\subsection{The sign of \(\partial_k q_H\) with \(\beta>0\)}
\label{app:three-negative-qH}

Assume that utility function is CARA where $\alpha = 1$. In the two-type case with $\beta > 0$, $\partial_k q_H > 0$ always. With three equally likely types, $\partial_k q_H < 0$ if and only if $R_{\theta}(\theta_H)((R_{\theta}(\theta_L) + (R_{\theta}(\theta_M)) > (R_{\theta}(\theta_L)^2 + (R_{\theta}(\theta_M)^2$, where $R_{\theta}(\theta_i) := J_i/\mathcal{G}_i$.

Differentiating the first-order condition $\mathcal{G}_i u(q_i) = \varepsilon$ with respect to $k$ and imposing the binding revenue constraint yields
\[
\partial_k\beta = -\frac{\E R_\theta}{\mathrm{Var}}, \qquad \partial_k q_i = 1 - \frac{\E R_\theta}{\mathrm{Var}}(R_{\theta}(\theta_i) - \E R_\theta),
\]
where $\E R_\theta := \sum_i p_i R_{\theta}(\theta_i)$ and $\mathrm{Var} := \sum_i p_i(R_{\theta}(\theta_i) - \E R_\theta)^2 > 0$.

\textit{Two types.} Since $R_{\theta}(\theta_H) > (R_{\theta}(\theta_L)$ always and $(R_{\theta}(\theta_L) < 0$ when $\beta > 0$ and $J_L < 0$,
\[
\partial_k q_H = -\frac{(R_{\theta}(\theta_L)}{p_H(R_{\theta}(\theta_H) - (R_{\theta}(\theta_L))} > 0.
\]

\textit{Three types.} With $p_i = 1/3$, substituting and simplifying gives
\[
\partial_k q_H = \frac{3[(R_{\theta}(\theta_L)^2 + (R_{\theta}(\theta_M)^2 - R_{\theta}(\theta_H)((R_{\theta}(\theta_L) + (R_{\theta}(\theta_M))]}{2[R_{\theta}(\theta_H)^2 - R_{\theta}(\theta_H) (R_{\theta}(\theta_L) - R_{\theta}(\theta_H) (R_{\theta}(\theta_M) + (R_{\theta}(\theta_L)^2 - (R_{\theta}(\theta_L) (R_{\theta}(\theta_M) + (R_{\theta}(\theta_M)^2]}.
\]
The denominator is strictly positive. Hence $\partial_k q_H < 0$ if and only if $R_{\theta}(\theta_H)((R_{\theta}(\theta_L) + (R_{\theta}(\theta_M)) > (R_{\theta}(\theta_L)^2 + (R_{\theta}(\theta_M)^2$.

\),
\(

\),
\(
(\lambda_L,\lambda_M,\lambda_H)=(1.25,1,0.8)
\),
and revenue requirement
\(
I=0.5
\).

\subsection{Complete-information benchmark}\label{appsec:CI}

\subsubsection{Optimal allocation}
Under complete information, the market designer observes each consumer's category $i$ and type $\theta$, so allocation rules and transfers can condition directly on true types. Since there are no incentive constraints, redistributive preferences enter the allocation problem through the pointwise weights $\lambda_i(\theta)$. Relative to the standard peak-load benchmark, this changes both the optimal marginal condition and the way revenue is extracted.

Let $S^*=[\underline{s},s^*)$ and $T^*=[s^*,\overline{s}]$ denote the off-peak and on-peak regions, where $s^*$ is the unique threshold such that
\[
\sum_i \mu_i\,\E_{i}\!\left[q_i^*(\theta,s^*)\right]=k.
\]
Let $\lambda^*$ denote the lowest welfare weight such that extracting full expected surplus from all types with $\lambda_i(\theta)\leq \lambda^*$ is sufficient to finance investment. Equivalently,
\[
\sum_i \mu_i \E_{(s,i)}\!\left[\theta\, U\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\,\mathbf 1_{\{\lambda_i(\theta) < \lambda^*\}}\right]
< I(k)
\leq
\sum_i \mu_i \E_{(s,i)}\!\left[\theta\, U\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\,\mathbf 1_{\{\lambda_i(\theta) \leq \lambda^*\}}\right].
\]
For each category $i$, whenever the equation $\lambda_i(\theta)=\lambda^*$ admits a solution, let $\theta_i^*(k)$ denote the associated cutoff type.

Under complete information, the revenue side of the problem is therefore organized by a sharp partition. Types with $\lambda_i(\theta)\leq \lambda^*$ are contributors: the planner extracts their full expected gross surplus to help finance investment. Types with $\lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*$ are non-contributors: they receive zero net transfer. This binary structure is specific to complete information and disappears once incentive constraints force the planner to leave information rents.

\begin{proposition}[Complete-information allocation with redistribution]\label{prop:FBalloc}
The optimal allocation is characterized by a unique quantity schedule $q_i^*(\theta,s)$ satisfying
\[\tag{FOC$_S^{fb}$}\label{FB-S}
\theta\,u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)=0,
\qquad s\in S^*,
\]
and
\[\tag{FOC$_T^{fb}$}\label{FB-T}
\begin{aligned}   
& u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\,\beta \theta= \varepsilon(s), & \text{if } \lambda_i(\theta)\leq \lambda^*,\\[6pt]
& u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\,\lambda_i(\theta)\theta=\varepsilon(s), & \text{if } \lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*,
\end{aligned}
\qquad s\in T^*.
\]
Transfers are independent of $s$ and satisfy $t_i^*(\theta)=\E_s\!\left[\theta\,U\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\right]$ for strictly contributing types $\lambda_i(\theta)<\lambda^*$, $t_i^*(\theta)=0$ for non-contributing types $\lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*$, and the participation constraint may be slack only at the marginal type $\lambda_i(\theta)=\lambda^*$.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:FBalloc} isolates the two dimensions along which redistribution departs from the traditional peak-load benchmark. First, it changes the planner's marginal condition. Off-peak, the allocation remains efficient.\footnote{I abstract from marginal production costs in order to isolate the interaction between scarcity and redistribution. This comes at the cost of shutting down an additional pricing margin: with positive marginal cost, the planner would also trade off allocative efficiency in non-scarcity states. Incorporating that margin would extend the standard analysis without changing the main mechanism emphasized here, namely how redistribution alters the scarcity component of peak-load pricing. Moreover, since the relevant objects vary continuously across states, no additional boundary term arises at the transition between off-peak and on-peak states.} On-peak, however, the marginal condition becomes type-specific for non-contributing consumers.  Without redistributive concerns, the optimal allocation is simply $\theta\,u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)=\varepsilon(s)$.

Therefore, a uniform spot price fails to implement the first best whenever the non-contributing set has positive mass, since the type-specific marginal condition cannot be replicated by a single price. Among contributing types alone, however, a spot price implements the optimal allocation, as they all face the same marginal condition.

The complete-information benchmark highlights two features that disappear under private information. First, with observable types, the planner can separate contributors from non-contributors directly, so redistribution operates through a sharp partition of consumers. Under incomplete information, this partition can no longer be drawn within categories: IC links all types through the rent schedule, so revenue extraction becomes category-based and any revenue requirement distorts the whole within-category allocation. Second, under complete information redistribution and revenue extraction are largely separable, whereas under incomplete information they conflict directly because changing the allocation of one type affects the information rents of others. As a result, the key question is no longer only who contributes, but how capacity and redistributive weights are sorted across the type distribution, which generates non-trivial winners and losers from both capacity expansions and preference shifts.\footnote{Additional details on the investment margin under complete information are reported in section~\ref{appsec:CI} of the Appendix.}

\subsubsection{Individual surplus and optimal investment}

Differentiating the complete-information allocation rule with respect to aggregate capacity naturally introduces different weights for contributing and non-contributing consumers:
\[
w_i^c(\theta,s):=-\frac{1}{\beta\,\theta\,u_q\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)} > 0,
\qquad
w_i^{nc}(\theta,s):=-\frac{1}{\lambda_i(\theta)\,\theta\,u_q\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)} > 0.
\]
Let
\[
\overline w^c(s):=\sum_i\mu_i\,\E_i\!\left[w_i^c(\theta,s)\,\mathbf 1_{\{\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*\}}\right],
\qquad
\overline w^{nc}(s):=\sum_i\mu_i\,\E_i\!\left[w_i^{nc}(\theta,s)\,\mathbf 1_{\{\lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*\}}\right],
\]
with $\overline w(s):=\overline w^c(s)+\overline w^{nc}(s)>0$. Define the weighted marginal contributor surplus by
\[
\tag{Euj$^*$}\label{EujFB}
\E_i^w[u^c\mid s]
:=
\frac{1}{\overline w(s)}\,
\E_i\!\left[w_i^c(\theta,s)\,\theta\,u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\,\mathbf 1_{\{\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*\}}\right]
+ \theta_i^*(k)
U\!\left(q_i\!\left(\theta_i^*(k),s\right),s\right)
g_i\!\left(\theta_i^*(k)\right)
\frac{d\theta_i^*(k)}{dk}.
\]
The first term is an inframarginal component: the weighted expected on-peak marginal utility of contributing types. The second is a boundary term that captures the surplus of types crossing into the contributing set when the contributor cutoff moves with investment.

\begin{proposition}[Individual surplus and optimal investment under complete information]\label{prop:CI-surplus}
Assume that both the contributing set and the non-contributing set have positive mass.

\noindent\textbf{(i)} For every type such that $\lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*$, individual surplus is weakly increasing in $k$ if
\[
\E_s\!\left[\sum_i \mu_i \, \E_i^w[u^c\mid s]\,\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T^*\}}\right] \geq I'(k).
\]

\noindent\textbf{(ii)} Suppose the optimal investment $k^*>0$ is interior. Then
\[
\tag{FOC$^{CI}_k$}\label{eq:FB-k1}
\frac{1}{\mu^c}\,
\E_s\!\left[\sum_i \mu_i\, \E_i\!\left[\theta\,u\!\left(q_i^*(\theta,s),s\right)\,\mathbf 1_{\{\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*\}}\right]\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T^*\}}\right]
= I'(k^*),
\]
where
\[
\mu^c:=\sum_i \mu_i\Pr_i\!\left(\lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*\right)
\]
denotes the total mass of contributing types.
\end{proposition}

A key feature of Proposition~\ref{prop:CI-surplus} is that surplus effects are uniform within each group: all non-contributing types move in the same direction simultaneously. There is no within-group cutoff separating winners from losers. This uniformity reflects the absence of information rents. Conditional on the partition induced by $\lambda^*$, the investment condition depends only on contributors' on-peak marginal utilities. In the uniform-weight benchmark, all types contribute and \ref{eq:FB-k1} collapses to the standard peak-load rule.

\subsubsection{Implementation with a two-part tariff}

The complete-information first best can be implemented with a tariff
\[
T_i(x,s\mid\theta)=A_i(\theta)+p_i(\theta,s)\,x.
\]
Contributing types face a common on-peak unit price,
\[
p(s)=
\begin{cases}
0, & s\in S^*,\\
\varepsilon(s)/\beta, & s\in T^*,\ \lambda_i(\theta)\leq\lambda^*,
\end{cases}
\]
while non-contributing types face type-dependent on-peak unit prices,
\[
p_i(\theta,s)=
\begin{cases}
0, & s\in S^*,\\
\varepsilon(s)/\lambda_i(\theta), & s\in T^*,\ \lambda_i(\theta)>\lambda^*.
\end{cases}
\]
The fixed fee $A_i(\theta)$ is then chosen to implement the transfer rule: it extracts the full expected surplus of contributing types and exactly offsets the variable payment of non-contributing types so that $t_i^*(\theta)=0$.

This implementation highlights the two margins through which redistribution operates under complete information. The variable price determines the allocation and is common only within the contributing group. The fixed fee determines extraction and creates the sharp contributor versus non-contributor partition that contrasts with the smooth rent profile arising under incomplete information.

\subsection{Single crossing of \ref{Eujmd}}\label{appsec:singlecross}

This appendix establishes the single-crossing property of \ref{Eujmd} invoked in the main text, and provides the primitive foundations of Assumption~\ref{ass:monotone}.

\begin{definition}
$R_q$ is \emph{log-submodular (resp. log-supermodular) in $(\theta,k_i)$} if $\partial^2 \log R_q/\partial\theta\,\partial k_i\leq (\text{resp.} \geq 0)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Single-crossing]\label{lem:SC-dir}
Assume that, for each \(k_i\), the map \(\theta\mapsto R_\theta(\theta)\) is monotone, and that \(R_q(\theta)\) has log-monotone differences in \((\theta,k_i)\). Then the map
\[
k_i \longmapsto \E_{(s,i)}^w[uJ\mid s]
\]
is single-crossing in $k_i$. Moreover, if a sign change occurs, its direction is pinned down by the combination of monotonicities: it crosses from $+$ to $-$ when $R_\theta$ is increasing and $R_q$ is log-submodular, or when $R_\theta$ is decreasing and $R_q$ is log-supermodular; it crosses from $-$ to $+$ in the two remaining cases.
\end{lemma}

The sign of both ratios depends on the model primitives as follows. For $R_q'(\theta)$, only the curvature of $u$ matters:
%##########################################
\[\sign\!\left(R_q'(\theta)\right)=-\sign\!\left((u_q(\cdot))^2-u(\cdot)u_{qq}(\cdot)\right),\]
%##########################################
so $R_q'(\theta)<0$ for linear and quadratic demand, while $R_q'(\theta)>0$ for CES/isoelastic demand. For $R_\theta'(\theta)$, the answer depends on whether the virtual surplus $J_i(\theta)$ grows faster or slower than the IC term $\Lambda_i(\theta)$ as $\theta$ increases,
%##########################################
\[
\sign\!\left(R_\theta'(\theta)\right) = \sign\!\left(J_i(\theta)\right)\,\sign\!\Bigg(\underbrace{\frac{J_i'(\theta)}{J_i(\theta)}}_{\text{revenue term}} - \underbrace{\frac{\Lambda_i'(\theta)}{\Lambda_i(\theta)}}_{\text{IC term}}\Bigg).
\]
%##########################################
The first term flags whether a type contributes to revenue ($J_i(\theta)>0$) or not ($J_i(\theta)<0$). The second compares the proportional growth rates of the two: when $\Lambda_i'(\theta)<0$, the IC term is shrinking, and the revenue term automatically dominates, giving $R_\theta'(\theta)>0$.\footnote{It is not possible to have a clear-cut sign of $R'_\theta(\theta)$. Indeed, the sign of $\Lambda_i'(\theta)$ depends on $g_i(\theta)$ and $\lambda_i(\theta)$: $\Lambda_i'(\theta)=-\Lambda_i(\theta)\frac{g_i'(\theta)}{g_i(\theta)}-\lambda_i(\theta)$. For instance, under a uniform distribution $\Lambda_i'(\theta)<0$, while its sign may be ambiguous for other distributions. Hence, under a uniform distribution, the sign of $R_\theta(\theta)$ is ambiguous as soon as $J_i(\theta)<0$. A utilitarian market designer (i.e., $\lambda_i(\theta)=G_i(\theta)$) does not imply a unique sign for $R_\theta(\theta)$, since it places implicit weight on higher types: in that case $\G_i(\theta)=\theta$, and the IC term in \ref{signRt} is positive (equal to $1/\theta$). If instead the correlation between types and social weights is zero, then the IC term is null and the sign of $R_\theta'(\theta)$ is determined solely by $J_i'(\theta)$, which is positive under regularity of $g_i(\theta)$.} 


\begin{remark}[Primitive foundation]\label{rem:prim}
On the IR-unconstrained region, where $\beta_i=0$, the log-monotone-differences condition becomes a condition on primitives alone: since $\partial q_i/\partial k_i = w_i/\overline{w}_i$ is proportional across types, the sign of $\partial^2\log R_q/\partial\theta\,\partial k_i$ reduces to the sign of $R_q''(q)$, so $R_q$ is log-submodular whenever $R_q''(\cdot)\le 0$ and log-supermodular whenever $R_q''(\cdot)\ge 0$. On the IR-constrained region, the response of the shadow cost $\beta_i$ enters the derivative of $\partial q_i/\partial k_i$ and the condition is no longer implied by the curvature of $U$ alone. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, however, $R_q = 1/\alpha$ is constant, so all cross-derivatives of $\log R_q$ vanish identically; the log-monotone-differences condition is therefore satisfied with equality regardless of the regime considered. Finally, note that unlike the log-monotone-differences condition on $R_q$, the monotonicity of $R_\theta(\theta)$ does not depend on $k_i$: from \ref{signRt}, its sign is determined entirely by the type distribution and the welfare weights.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Implementation feasibility under capacity expansion}\label{appsec:implementation-region}

This appendix collects the full characterization of the implementation region together with a numerical illustration. 

\begin{proposition}[Implementation feasibility under capacity expansion]\label{prop:implementation}
Fix category \(i\) and a budget requirement \(I_i>0\). Let \(k_i^+\) be the capacity level at which the capacity constraint never binds.

Assume that \ref{Eujmd} is \emph{single-crossing in \(k_i\) from \(+\) to \(-\)} on \([0,k_i^+]\). Then \(\E\underline{CS}_i(k_i)\) is single-peaked on \([0,k_i^+]\) and the set of capacities for which the IR-unconstrained allocation is feasible (equivalently, \(\E\underline{CS}_i(k_i)\ge 0\)) has the following form:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
\item If \(\E\underline{CS}_i(k_i^+)\ge 0\), there exists a unique cutoff \(\tilde k_i\in(0,k_i^+]\) such that the IR-unconstrained allocation is optimal for all \(k_i\in[\tilde k_i,k_i^+]\).
\item If \(\E\underline{CS}_i(k_i^+)<0\), the IR-unconstrained allocation is either never optimal on \([0,k_i^+]\) or optimal only on an intermediate interval \([\tilde k_i^-,\tilde k_i^+]\subset(0,k_i^+)\).
\end{enumerate}

If instead \ref{Eujmd} crosses from \(-\) to \(+\), then \(\E\underline{CS}_i(k_i)\) is single-dipped on \([0,k_i^+]\) and either \(\E\underline{CS}_i(k_i)<0\) for all \(k_i\in[0,k_i^+]\), or there exists a unique \(\tilde k_i\in(0,k_i^+]\) such that \(\E\underline{CS}_i(k_i)\ge 0\) for all \(k_i\in[\tilde k_i,k_i^+]\).
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation} identifies the geometry of the IR-unconstrained region. When \ref{Eujmd} switches from \(+\) to \(-\), the implementation region may arise only for intermediate values of \(k_i\): low levels of capacity keep the financing constraint tight, while high levels of capacity may shift the gains from further expansion toward types that contribute relatively less to revenue. By contrast, when marginal expansions continue to increase the mechanism's revenue, the undistorted region expands as capacity increases.


Figure~\ref{fig:appendix_redistribution_marg_k_uniform} illustrates Theorem~\ref{thm:surplus} and Proposition~\ref{prop:implementation}. The left panel shows the boundary term \ref{ECSi} as a function of $k_i$ for two type supports, tracing both the implementation region and the sign of \ref{Eujmd}. The dashed curve has a lower $\underline{\theta}_i$, which increases the mass of consumers with $J_i<0$ and shrinks the range of $k_i$ for which \ref{Eujmd} is positive, shifting $k^\ast$ to the left. The right panels illustrate the individual surplus effects at two capacity levels: at $k_1$, where \ref{Eujmd} is positive, all types gain from a capacity expansion; at $k_2$, where \ref{Eujmd} is negative, only types above the cutoff $\tilde\theta_i$ benefit.

\subsection{Preference perturbations and effective social weights}\label{app:preferenceorder}

This subsection provides primitive conditions on redistributive preference perturbations that ensure a well-behaved change in effective social weights across types. In particular, it characterizes when the induced change in effective weights has a constant sign, a property that is useful for deriving sharp comparative statics in the main text.

Recall that $\Lambda_i(\theta)$ measures the social value of the information rent given to types above $\theta$, evaluated at the welfare weights. I consider a perturbation of redistributive preferences $\lambda_i(\theta)$ and study how it affects the associated objects.

\begin{lemma}[Preference shifts and effective social weights]\label{lem:preferenceorder}
Consider a weight function $\lambda_i(\theta)$ and its perturbation $\lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)$, with $\Delta\lambda_i(\theta):=\lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)-\lambda_i(\theta)$ and $\Delta\G_i(\theta):=\G_i^\Delta(\theta)-\G_i(\theta)$. Suppose $\G_i(\theta),\G_i^\Delta(\theta)>0$ for all $\theta\in[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$. Define:
\[
m_i(\theta) = \frac{\lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)}{\lambda_i(\theta)}, \qquad c_\Lambda:=\frac{\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta}{\tilde\lambda_i},
\qquad
c_{\G}:=\frac{\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta+\beta_i^\Delta}
{\tilde\lambda_i+\beta_i}.
\]
If $m_i(\theta)$ is increasing on $[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$, then for $\theta\in[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$,
\[
\frac{\Lambda_i^{\Delta\,\prime}(\theta)}{\Lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)} \;\ge\; (\text{resp.} \le) \; \frac{\Lambda_i^{\prime}(\theta)}{\Lambda_i(\theta)}.
\]
Moreover, if $1\le c_{\G}\le c_\Lambda$ (resp. $c_\Lambda\le c_{\G}\le 1$), then for all $\theta\in[\underline{\theta}_i,\overline{\theta}_i]$, $\Delta\G_i(\theta)\ge(\text{resp. }\le)0$. In particular, if either $\beta_i=\beta_i^\Delta=0$ or $\beta_i^\Delta+\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta=\beta_i+\tilde\lambda_i$, then the condition reduces to $\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta\ge (\text{resp. }\le)\tilde\lambda_i$.
\end{lemma}

The first part of the lemma shows that a proportional tilt of welfare weights toward higher types shifts $\Lambda_i^\Delta(\theta)$ upward relative to $\Lambda_i(\theta)$ across the type space. The second part connects the direction of a preference shift to the sign of $\Delta\G_i(\theta)$, under conditions that relate the average change in welfare weights to the corresponding change in effective weights. In particular, monotone mean-preserving rotations of $\lambda_i(\theta)$ generate a constant-sign change in $\Delta\G_i(\theta)$ across types.

\subsection{Additional results on preference-induced reallocation}\label{app:pref-reallocation}

This appendix presents the technical results underlying the sorting effect induced by redistributive preference perturbations. The key identity is Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}, which shows that, at fixed capacity,
\[
\Delta q_i(\theta,s) = R_q(\theta)\!\left(R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)-\mathbb{E}_i^w\!\left[R_{\mathcal{G}}\mid s\right]\right), \qquad s\in T_i.
\]
Hence the quantity response depends on deviations of the proportional change in effective social weight from its capacity-weighted average. 

\begin{proposition}[Non-monotone reallocation under preference perturbations]\label{prop:rprefreallocation}
Assume $R_{\mathcal{G}}(\theta)$ is not (a.e.) constant.

\noindent\textbf{(i)} Under the IR-unconstrained allocation, $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$ is not monotone on $\Theta_i$ for any perturbation. Under the IR-constrained allocation, assume that $\beta_i^\Delta+\tilde\lambda_i^\Delta=\beta_i+\tilde\lambda_i$, then the same conclusion holds whenever $|\Delta\tilde\lambda_i|$ is sufficiently small.

\noindent\textbf{(ii)} Assume a mean preserving perturbation such that $\Delta\tilde\lambda_i=0$. Then $\Delta q_i(\cdot,s)$ is not monotone, the cutoff set $\{\theta:\Delta q_i(\theta,s)=0\}$ contains at least two elements, and letting $\theta_-\le\theta_+$ denote its minimum and maximum elements:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=1cm]
\item if $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\ge 0$ for all $\theta$, then $\Delta q_i(\theta,s)\le 0$ for $\theta\in[\underline\theta_i,\theta_-)\cup(\theta_+,\overline\theta_i]$;
\item if $\Delta\mathcal{G}_i(\theta)\le 0$ for all $\theta$, then $\Delta q_i(\theta,s)\ge 0$ for $\theta\in[\underline\theta_i,\theta_-)\cup(\theta_+,\overline\theta_i]$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation} shows that the sorting effect is generically non-monotone. Even when preferences shift uniformly toward higher or lower types, the induced quantity response may not be ranked monotonically across the type space. Intuitively, the revenue motive, embedded in $J_i(\theta)$, and the redistributive motive, embedded in $\Lambda_i(\theta)$, interact through the endogenous scarcity adjustment. This forces reallocations toward some interior types at the expense of others, rather than producing a simple upward or downward tilt of the quantity schedule.

The second part sharpens the non-monotonicity result under mean-preserving perturbations. In that case, the boundary types necessarily move in the opposite direction from the interior region where the reallocation concentrates. Thus, even a preference shift that pointwise favors higher types need not raise the quantities received by the highest types; the IC structure instead pushes the adjustment toward intermediate types.

\subsection{Redistributive preferences and the sorting effect}\label{app:sorting}

This section examines how a change in redistributive preferences affects the sufficient statistic \ref{Eujmd} that governs the surplus incidence of capacity expansion.
The identity from a local perturbation
\[\Delta R_\theta(\theta) = -R_\mathcal{G}(\theta) R_\theta(\theta)\]
provides a formal bridge between the two sufficient statistics that govern both the within- and across-category results. A preference perturbation reshapes who gains from capacity expansion precisely by changing how virtual surplus is sorted across types. I discuss in Appendix Proposition~\ref{prop:Elasticity} the conditions under which a change in preferences can clearly affect how virtual surplus is sorted across types (notably the monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$). When it fails, their joint effect on sorting can no longer be signed from primitives alone, and sharp results are no longer available.
 Differentiating $\E^w[uJ\mid s] = \varepsilon_i(s)\,\E_i[R_q(\theta)R_\theta(\theta)]$ with respect to the preference perturbation yields three terms:
\[
\Delta\E^w[uJ\mid s] = \underbrace{\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)\,\E_i[R_q R_\theta]}_{\text{scarcity rent}} + \underbrace{\varepsilon_i(s)\,\E_i[\Delta R_q \cdot R_\theta]}_{\text{behavioral}} + \underbrace{\varepsilon_i(s)\,\E_i[R_q \cdot \Delta R_\theta]}_{\text{sorting}}.
\]
The scarcity rent term is governed by $\Delta\varepsilon_i(s)/\varepsilon_i(s) = \E^w_i[R_\G\mid s]$, established in Lemma~\ref{lem:realloc}. The sorting term is governed by the identity $\Delta R_\theta(\theta) = -R_\G(\theta)R_\theta(\theta)$, which is the formal bridge discussed in the main text. The behavioral term cannot be signed in general, as established in Proposition~\ref{prop:rprefreallocation}. Proposition~\ref{prop:Elasticity} below provides conditions under which the sorting term can nonetheless be signed from primitives. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:CARA}, the behavioral term vanishes entirely since $R_q$ is constant, and both the scarcity rent and sorting terms collapse to a single covariance condition between $R_\G(\theta)$ and $R_\theta(\theta)$.

\begin{proposition}[Elasticity condition]\label{prop:Elasticity}
Assume that $R_\theta(\theta)$ and $R_q(\theta)$ are monotone, that $R_{\G}(\theta)$ has a constant sign, and that, for all $\theta$ such that $R_{\G}(\theta)R_\theta(\theta)\neq 0$,\footnote{At isolated zeros of $R_\theta(\theta)$, the cross-term $-R_\theta(\theta)R_{\G}'(\theta)$ vanishes and the sign of $\Delta R_\theta'(\theta)$ is determined by $-R_{\G}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)$ directly. At isolated zeros of $R_{\G}(\theta)$, the constant-sign hypothesis forces $R_{\G}'(\theta)=0$ by differentiability, so the cross-term vanishes there as well. Monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ is preserved in both cases.}
\[
\left|\frac{R_{\G}'(\theta)}{R_{\G}(\theta)}\right| \le \left|\frac{R_\theta'(\theta)}{R_\theta(\theta)}\right|.
\]
Then $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ is monotone and
\[
\mathrm{sign}\!\left(\Cov_{i}\!\left(\Delta R_\theta(\theta),R_q(\theta)\right)\right) = \mathrm{sign}\!\left(-R_{\G}(\theta)R_\theta'(\theta)\right)\cdot \mathrm{sign}\!\left(R_q'(\theta)\right).
\]
If the elasticity condition fails, monotonicity of $\Delta R_\theta(\theta)$ can no longer be inferred from primitives alone.
\end{proposition}

The elasticity condition compares two elasticities: (i) $\left|R_{\G}'(\theta)/R_{\G}(\theta)\right|$, which measures how sharply the preference change varies across types, and (ii) $\left|R_\theta'(\theta)/R_\theta(\theta)\right|$, which measures how sharply the existing sorting pattern varies across types. The condition requires that the preference change be smoother in proportional terms than the existing sorting pattern. To interpret its economic meaning, consider the case where lower types are allocated more capacity ($R_q'(\theta)\le 0$), higher types get higher rents ($R_\theta'(\theta)\ge 0$), the change of preferences is toward higher types ($R_{\G}(\theta)\ge 0$), and I seek to reduce the adverse sorting effect. Then a preference change, even in the correct direction, can exacerbate adverse sorting by creating local distortions. The sufficient elasticity condition binds in two regions: (a) types contributing negatively to the trade-off ($R_\theta(\theta)<0$) but receiving increasing proportional weights ($R_{\G}'(\theta)\ge 0$), and (b) types contributing positively to the trade-off ($R_\theta(\theta)>0$) but receiving decreasing proportional weights ($R_{\G}'(\theta)\le 0$). In both regions, preference changes that are too concentrated reverse the sign of $\Delta R_\theta'(\theta)$; the elasticity bound ensures changes remain sufficiently gradual.

Figure~\ref{fig:variation_uniform_preference_tradeoff} illustrates this: even when the direction of a preference shift favors the same consumers, a particular concentration of social weight adjustments can exacerbate adverse sorting, worsening rather than improving the trade-off effect.

\subsection{Marginal response of category allocations to aggregate capacity}\label{app:dkidk}

The next lemma characterizes the local response of the optimal category allocation to a marginal change in aggregate capacity. The result is local: it holds on a neighborhood of an interior optimum in which the set of active and contributing categories is unchanged. Its main purpose is to show that cross-category heterogeneity enters only through the sufficient statistic
\[
\E_{(s,i)}^w[uJ\mid s],
\]
which is the same object that governs the within-category comparative statics.

\begin{lemma}[Marginal response of category allocations to aggregate capacity]\label{lem:dkidk}
Fix an interior optimum \(\{k_i^\ast,I_i^\ast\}_{i\in N}\) and suppose that, on a neighborhood of the optimum, the set of active and contributing categories is unchanged. For each interior category \(i\), define
\[
\Omega_i
:=
\E_s\!\left[\frac{1}{\overline w_i(s)}\mathbf 1_{\{s\in T_i\}}\right].
\]
Then the marginal response of category \(i\) is
\[
\frac{dk_i^\ast}{dk}
=
\frac{
\Omega_i^{-1}
\left(
\E_{(s,i)}^w[uJ\mid s]
-I'(k^\ast)
\right)
}{
\sum_{j\in N } \mu_j\,\Omega_j^{-1}
\left(
\E_{(s,j)}^w[uJ\mid s] 
-I'(k^\ast)
\right)
}
\mathcal{B}
-\Omega_i^{-1}\tilde\lambda^\ast I''(k^\ast).
\]
where
\[\mathcal{B} =\left(
1+\tilde\lambda^\ast I''(k^\ast)\sum_{j\in N } \mu_j \Omega_j^{-1}
\right)>0\]
as $\tilde\lambda^\ast I''(k^\ast)>0$. In particular, since \(\Omega_i>0\), for a given marginal change in aggregate capacity, the sign of the category capacity change is fully determined by the sufficient statistic \ref{Eujmd} 

\end{lemma}

The lemma shows that the response of \(k_i^\ast\) to aggregate expansion depends on category \(i\) only through the sufficient statistic \(\E_{(s,i)}^w[uJ\mid s]\). This makes it possible to connect the outer comparative statics to the within-category ordering results.
